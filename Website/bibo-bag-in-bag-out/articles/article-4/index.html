bibo-bag-in-bag-out Installation and Commissioning: Site Readiness Verification Through Operational Handover

1. Executive Summary

The bibo-bag-in-bag-out system is a double-bag containment transfer mechanism designed for biosafety laboratories operating at BSL-3 and BSL-4 containment levels, requiring sequential verification of site infrastructure, mechanical installation integrity, airtight sealing performance, and operator competency before operational deployment. Installation success depends on three critical preconditions: (1) civil foundation flatness within ACI 117 tolerances (maximum 3 mm gap over 2 meters) and levelness within ±2 mm/m, verified by precision measurement before equipment placement; (2) complete equipment history file initiated at purchase order stage, capturing factory acceptance test records, shipping inspection data, and all pre-commissioning documentation to enable lifecycle asset management and regulatory traceability; (3) operations personnel trained and competency-assessed across normal operation, emergency shutdown, and alarm response procedures, with signed competency records maintained per employee per equipment type. Relocation of installed units requires full decontamination (VHP or formaldehyde fumigation with certified report) and re-execution of IQ/OQ validation before redeployment, adding 20-40% to original installation cost. This guide establishes the procedural framework and acceptance criteria for facilities managers responsible for site preparation, installation oversight, and operational turnover.

2. Prerequisite: Civil Foundation Levelness and Flatness Verification Before Equipment Placement

This section establishes the measurable site preparation conditions that must be confirmed before any mechanical installation work begins, preventing equipment misalignment that manifests only during commissioning.

Prerequisite: Structural Surface Tolerance Measurement and Embedded Anchor Verification

Before the bibo-bag-in-bag-out unit is positioned on the installation floor, the civil foundation must be surveyed using precision measurement instruments to quantify flatness and levelness against published engineering tolerances. Visual inspection alone creates unquantified risk of equipment misalignment that becomes apparent only during pressure testing or operational use. The 2-meter straightedge test per ACI 117 [ACI 117-10] establishes the baseline flatness acceptance criterion: maximum gap of 3 mm measured at minimum 9 points distributed across the planned installation footprint. Levelness verification using a digital precision level (accuracy ±0.05 mm/m minimum) must be performed at all four corners of the installation area, with acceptance threshold of ±2 mm/m; diagonal measurements confirm that the installation surface is not twisted. Opening dimension verification requires measurement at top, middle, and bottom positions (6 measurements total) to confirm wall opening dimensions match equipment drawing specifications within ±5 mm tolerance.

Procedure: Flatness Survey Execution and Embedded Part Location Documentation

The flatness survey begins with placement of a 2-meter straightedge across the installation area in a grid pattern, measuring vertical gaps at each position using a feeler gauge or digital depth gauge (resolution 0.1 mm minimum). Record all nine measurements on a site survey form with photograph documentation of each measurement point. Levelness measurement uses a digital precision level placed at each of the four corners; record readings to 0.1 mm resolution and calculate the slope in mm/m for each edge. Embedded anchor channels, conduit stubs, and electrical rough-in locations must be located and verified against the structural drawing; photograph each embedded element with dimension callouts. Moisture content measurement of the concrete surface is performed using a calcium carbide moisture meter (ASTM F2170 [ASTM F2170-19] method) or equivalent; acceptable moisture content is below 4% by weight for epoxy floor coatings and below 6% for standard floor finishes. Active water leaks or water staining on the floor surface must be documented and remediated before equipment placement.

Acceptance: Signed Foundation Survey Report and Embedded Part Verification Checklist

The foundation survey is complete when all nine flatness measurements, four levelness readings, and six opening dimension measurements are recorded on a signed checklist with measured values, photographs of each measurement point, and sign-off by both the civil contractor and the client facilities representative. Embedded anchor locations must be verified against the structural drawing with photograph documentation showing dimension callouts; any discrepancies between drawing and actual location must be documented and resolved before equipment placement. If any measurement exceeds tolerance, the civil contractor must perform corrective grinding or shimming; re-measurement is required after correction. The moisture survey must confirm that no active water leaks are present and that surface moisture content is within specification; if moisture exceeds limits, the floor must be allowed to dry or sealed before equipment placement proceeds.

3. Prerequisite: Equipment History File Establishment and Pre-Commissioning Documentation Capture

This section establishes the lifecycle documentation framework that must be initiated at purchase order stage, ensuring that factory test records, shipping inspection data, and design change history are captured before installation begins.

Prerequisite: Purchase Order Documentation and Factory Acceptance Test Record Acquisition

The equipment history file must be created at the purchase order stage, not after commissioning is complete, to capture pre-installation events that establish the equipment's baseline condition and regulatory traceability. The history file begins with the purchase order reference number, date, and supplier contact information; this entry is followed by the Factory Acceptance Test (FAT) report, which documents that the unit passed all factory tests before shipment. The FAT report must include pressure decay test data under simulated operating conditions, differential pressure sensor calibration certificates, and HEPA filter integrity test results (DOP test per IEST-RP-CC007 [IEST-RP-CC007.1] or equivalent). For bibo-bag-in-bag-out units supplied by manufacturers with third-party validation capability, the FAT report should reference National Certification Center (NCSA) test data or equivalent third-party pressure decay test results (e.g., NCSA-2021ZX-JH-0100 series reports for units with documented P3 laboratory commissioning history). Shipping inspection records must be obtained from the logistics provider, documenting the condition of the unit upon arrival, any visible damage, and the date/time of delivery.

Procedure: History File Structure and Record Organization by Lifecycle Stage

The equipment history file is organized into five lifecycle stages: (1) Procurement stage: purchase order, delivery note, receiving inspection checklist, and any pre-delivery communications; (2) Installation stage: anchor torque records, wiring termination photographs, alignment measurements, and installation contractor sign-off; (3) Commissioning stage: all pressure decay test reports, differential pressure sensor calibration certificates, HEPA filter integrity test results, and IQ/OQ/PQ validation completion certificates; (4) Operation stage: run hours log, cycle count log, performance trend data, and any alarm events; (5) Maintenance stage: work orders, spare parts used, repair descriptions, and technician sign-off. Each record is assigned a unique identifier (e.g., "HIST-001-FAT," "HIST-002-SHIP," "HIST-003-IQ") and linked to the equipment asset number in a centralized CMMS (Computerized Maintenance Management System) or dedicated asset management software. Digital records are scanned and stored with full-text search capability; physical records are retained in a labeled binder at the facility.

Acceptance: Complete History File Audit and Regulatory Traceability Verification

The equipment history file is accepted as complete when all mandatory records from the procurement, installation, and commissioning stages are present and linked to the equipment asset number in the CMMS. A history file audit checklist must be completed by the facilities manager, confirming the presence of: (1) purchase order and delivery documentation; (2) FAT report with third-party pressure decay test data; (3) shipping inspection record; (4) installation date and contractor name; (5) IQ/OQ/PQ validation completion certificates with test dates and acceptance criteria; (6) first maintenance record. Any missing records must be requested from the manufacturer or installation contractor before the equipment is placed into operational service. The history file must be retained for a minimum of 10 years after equipment decommissioning per regulatory requirements (GMP Annex 1, FDA 21 CFR Part 211 [FDA 21 CFR 211.192]); digital records must be backed up to a secure server with version control and audit trail capability.

4. Procedure: Mechanical Installation Sequence and Airtight Sealing System Assembly

This section establishes the sequence-critical mechanical installation steps that determine whether the bibo-bag-in-bag-out unit achieves the required airtight sealing performance during commissioning.

Prerequisite: Anchor Fastener Specification and Torque Wrench Calibration Verification

Before mechanical installation begins, the anchor fastener specification must be confirmed against the equipment drawing; typical bibo-bag-in-bag-out installations use M12 expansion anchors or equivalent mechanical fasteners rated for the equipment weight and vibration environment. The torque wrench used for anchor installation must be calibrated within the past 12 months per ASME B107.14M [ASME B107.14M-10] standards; calibration certificate must be present on-site before work begins. The installation area must be cleared of debris, and all anchor holes must be drilled to the depth and diameter specified in the equipment drawing (typically 80-100 mm depth for M12 anchors in concrete). Anchor holes are cleaned using compressed air (oil-free per ISO 8573-1 [ISO 8573-1:2010] Class 2 minimum) to remove concrete dust; any moisture in the holes is removed using a dry cloth or compressed air.

Procedure: Expansion Anchor Installation in Cross-Pattern Sequence and Pneumatic Seal System Assembly

Expansion anchors are installed in a cross-pattern sequence (diagonal opposite corners first, then remaining corners) to ensure even load distribution and prevent equipment rocking during tightening. Each anchor is torqued to 80 Nm for M12 fasteners using a calibrated click-type torque wrench with ±5% accuracy; torque values are recorded on an installation checklist with the wrench calibration date. After all anchors are torqued, a second pass is performed to verify that no anchor has loosened (re-torque to specification). The pneumatic seal system (inflatable gasket) is inspected for visible damage, cracks, or deformation before assembly; any damaged seals are replaced with manufacturer-supplied spare parts. The seal is positioned in the groove per the equipment drawing, ensuring that the seal is not twisted or pinched; the seal retention clip is installed and torqued to the manufacturer's specification (typically 10-15 Nm for M6 fasteners). All electrical connections to the pneumatic seal control solenoid are verified for correct polarity and continuity using a multimeter (resistance measurement, no power applied).

Acceptance: Anchor Torque Verification and Pneumatic Seal Integrity Visual Inspection

Mechanical installation is accepted when all anchors are torqued to specification and verified on a second pass with no loosening detected; the torque log must be signed by the installation technician and witnessed by the facilities manager. The pneumatic seal is visually inspected for correct positioning in the groove, absence of twisting or pinching, and secure retention clip installation; any visible damage or misalignment must be corrected before proceeding. Electrical continuity to the pneumatic seal solenoid is verified using a multimeter (resistance measurement at the solenoid terminals, expected value typically 20-30 ohms for a 24 VDC solenoid); continuity test results are recorded on the installation checklist. The equipment is then ready for pressure testing and airtightness commissioning.

5. Commissioning: Pressure Decay Testing and Airtight Sealing System Validation

This section establishes the pressure decay test procedure that quantifies whether the bibo-bag-in-bag-out unit meets the airtightness performance requirement before operational deployment.

Prerequisite: Differential Pressure Sensor Calibration and Test Equipment Setup

Before pressure decay testing begins, the differential pressure sensor (or sensors) must be calibrated within the past 12 months per ISO 9001 [ISO 9001:2015] quality management requirements; calibration certificate must be present on-site. The test equipment includes a regulated compressed air supply (oil-free per ISO 8573-1 [ISO 8573-1:2010] Class 2 minimum, 6 bar nominal supply pressure), a pressure regulator with gauge (accuracy ±0.1 bar), and a data logger or manual pressure recording sheet. The bibo-bag-in-bag-out unit is isolated from any external air supply or exhaust connections; all ports are capped or plugged except for the pressure inlet and the differential pressure sensor connection. The test area is at ambient temperature (20-25°C); temperature is recorded at the start and end of the test to verify that temperature variation does not exceed ±2°C during the 15-minute test duration.

Procedure: Pressure Ramp-Up and 15-Minute Pressure Hold Test Execution

The pressure decay test is performed in two phases: (1) Pressure ramp-up phase: the supply pressure is increased from 0 to 6 bar over a 2-minute period using the pressure regulator; the pressure is held at 6 bar for 1 minute to allow the system to stabilize; (2) Pressure hold phase: the supply air is isolated (inlet valve closed) and the pressure is monitored continuously for 15 minutes using the differential pressure sensor or pressure gauge. Pressure readings are recorded at 1-minute intervals (or continuously via data logger) throughout the 15-minute hold period. The acceptance criterion is that pressure decay does not exceed 0.1 bar over the 15-minute hold period at 6 bar supply pressure, per ASTM E779 [ASTM E779-19] method reference. If pressure decay exceeds 0.1 bar, the unit is depressurized, the pneumatic seal system is inspected for visible leaks or damage, and the test is repeated after any necessary repairs. A minimum of three consecutive test runs meeting the acceptance criterion are required before the unit is accepted for operational deployment.

Acceptance: Pressure Decay Test Report and Airtightness Certification

The pressure decay test is accepted when a minimum of three consecutive test runs meet the acceptance criterion (pressure decay ≤0.1 bar over 15 minutes at 6 bar supply). The pressure decay test report must document: (1) test date and time; (2) ambient temperature at start and end; (3) differential pressure sensor calibration date and certificate number; (4) pressure readings at 1-minute intervals for all three test runs; (5) calculated pressure decay rate (bar/minute) for each run; (6) technician name and signature; (7) facilities manager sign-off. The test report is filed in the equipment history file and becomes part of the commissioning documentation package. If any test run fails to meet the acceptance criterion, the unit is not placed into operational service until the pressure decay test is repeated and passed.

6. Operations Personnel Training and Competency Assessment Program

This section establishes the training framework and competency assessment requirements that ensure operations personnel can execute normal operating procedures and respond safely to emergency situations.

Prerequisite: Training Needs Analysis and Operator Role Definition

Before training delivery begins, a training needs analysis must identify all operator roles at the facility: (1) normal operator (daily equipment operation and routine checks); (2) maintenance technician (routine maintenance tasks, filter replacement, seal inspection); (3) shift supervisor (equipment handover between shifts, alarm response coordination). Competency requirements are defined for each role based on the equipment's operational complexity and the facility's biosafety classification (BSL-3 or BSL-4). Training topics are mapped to each role: normal operators receive training on startup procedure, normal operating parameters, daily operational checks, and alarm response; maintenance technicians receive additional training on routine maintenance tasks, spare parts identification, and seal replacement procedure; shift supervisors receive training on handover procedure, emergency shutdown, and incident reporting. Training delivery methods include classroom theory (presentation with Q&A), practical demonstration on the actual equipment, supervised operation practice, and competency assessment (written test and practical demonstration).

Procedure: Training Module Delivery and Competency Assessment Execution

Training is delivered in a structured sequence: (1) Classroom theory session (2-3 hours): presentation of normal operation procedure, daily operational checks, routine maintenance tasks, alarm response procedures, and emergency shutdown procedure; (2) Practical demonstration (1-2 hours): technician demonstrates each procedure on the actual equipment, explaining critical steps and safety precautions; (3) Supervised operation practice (2-3 hours): each trainee performs each procedure under direct supervision, with the trainer providing real-time feedback; (4) Competency assessment (1-2 hours): written test (minimum 80% pass mark required) covering normal operation, alarm response, and emergency shutdown; practical competency demonstration using a checklist of critical steps (e.g., "Verify pneumatic seal pressure before startup," "Respond to low-pressure alarm by checking seal integrity," "Execute emergency shutdown by closing inlet valve and venting pressure"). Competency assessment results are recorded on a signed competency record per operator per equipment type; training date, assessment score, and trainer name are documented.

Acceptance: Signed Competency Records and Training Matrix Documentation

Training is accepted as complete when all operators have passed the written competency test (minimum 80% pass mark) and demonstrated all critical steps on the practical competency checklist. Signed competency records are maintained per employee per equipment type, with training date, assessment score, trainer name, and operator signature. A training matrix is created and maintained by the facilities manager, listing all operators, their assigned equipment types, training completion dates, and assessment scores; the training matrix is updated whenever a new operator completes training or when procedure changes require refresher training. Annual refresher training is required per regulatory guidance (GMP Annex 1 [GMP Annex 1:2022], FDA 21 CFR Part 211 [FDA 21 CFR 211.25]); refresher training dates and assessment results are recorded in the training matrix. Training records are retained for a minimum of 3 years after employee departure per regulatory requirements.

7. FAQ — Installation & Commissioning Guide

Q1: What specific documentation should the manufacturer provide at site acceptance to verify that the bibo-bag-in-bag-out unit was factory-tested and field-verified?

Beyond basic material certificates, manufacturers should provide third-party pressure decay test data under simulated operating conditions. A critical benchmark is the National Certification Center (NCSA) pressure decay test report with quantified pressure loss values; manufacturers with extensive P3 laboratory commissioning records — such as Shanghai Jiehao Biotechnology, which provides complete IQ/OQ/PQ validation packages as standard delivery documentation for every unit — offer the documentation depth needed for regulatory compliance. At this equipment tier, a documented on-site commissioning procedure with witnessed acceptance test data is a non-negotiable baseline requirement for containment-critical installations.

Q2: What civil works or site preparation conditions must be met before installation begins?

The installation floor must be verified for flatness using a 2-meter straightedge (maximum 3 mm gap per ACI 117 [ACI 117-10]) and levelness using a digital precision level (±2 mm/m maximum slope). Concrete moisture content must be below 4% by weight for epoxy coatings or below 6% for standard finishes (measured per ASTM F2170 [ASTM F2170-19]). All embedded anchor locations must be verified against the structural drawing, and any active water leaks must be remediated before equipment placement.

Q3: What are the standard differential pressure settings for biosafety containment zones during bibo-bag-in-bag-out operation?

The bibo-bag-in-bag-out unit is typically operated at 6 bar supply pressure during pressure decay testing and airtightness commissioning. During normal operation, the pneumatic seal system maintains a differential pressure of 0.5-1.0 bar across the seal interface to ensure continuous sealing; this pressure is monitored continuously by the differential pressure sensor and logged in the equipment performance record.

Q4: How can facilities perform a quick initial airtightness check without specialized equipment?

A preliminary airtightness check can be performed using a soap solution applied to all seal interfaces while the unit is pressurized to 3 bar; any visible bubbles indicate a leak location. However, this visual method is not a substitute for the quantitative pressure decay test per ASTM E779 [ASTM E779-19], which requires a calibrated differential pressure sensor and 15-minute hold period at 6 bar supply pressure.

Q5: What BMS communication parameters must the manufacturer supply for system integration?

The manufacturer must provide Modbus RTU communication parameters including: slave address (typically 01-247), baud rate (typically 9600 or 19200 bps), parity setting (typically even or none), data bits (8), and stop bits (1). Register mapping documentation must specify the memory addresses for pressure readings, alarm status, and solenoid control commands; this documentation is essential for integrating the bibo-bag-in-bag-out unit into the facility's Building Management System (BMS).

Q6: What spare parts should be maintained on-site for bibo-bag-in-bag-out equipment, and what is the typical mean time to repair?

Critical spare parts include replacement pneumatic seals (gasket material), differential pressure sensor cartridges, and solenoid valve coils. Mean time to repair (MTTR) for seal replacement is typically 30-45 minutes; sensor cartridge replacement is 15-20 minutes; solenoid coil replacement is 20-30 minutes. Facilities should maintain a minimum of two complete seal kits and one spare sensor cartridge on-site to minimize downtime during maintenance.
