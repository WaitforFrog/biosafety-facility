2. Pressure Decay Test Rigor: Why Supplier-Declared Airtightness Without Standardized Protocol Is an Unquantified Containment Risk

This section establishes the specific pressure decay testing parameters that distinguish verified seal performance from unsubstantiated supplier claims, providing buyers with the exact pass/fail criteria required for BSL-3 and BSL-4 biosafety-inflatable-airtight-doors procurement.

Why Most Procurement Specifications Fail to Define Pressure Decay Test Conditions

The most frequent procurement error in biosafety-inflatable-airtight-doors selection is accepting a supplier's stated "airtightness rating" without requiring disclosure of the test methodology behind that rating. A door rated at 2,500 Pa pressure resistance — such as the JIEHAO BS-01-IAD-1 model — describes structural withstand capacity, not seal leak rate under containment conditions. These are fundamentally different measurements. Structural pressure resistance confirms the door panel and frame will not deform or fail under load. Pressure decay testing, by contrast, measures the rate at which a sealed volume loses pressure through micro-leakage paths at the gasket interface, penetration seals, and frame-to-wall junctions.

Buyers who specify only "airtight to 2,500 Pa" in tender documents receive doors that pass a structural test but may exhibit unacceptable leak rates under the negative pressure differentials characteristic of BSL-3 operations. The failure mode is invisible at commissioning and manifests only during operational containment verification, when remediation costs escalate by a factor of 5 to 10 compared to factory-stage rejection.

Pressure Decay Protocol Parameters: Polarity, Duration, Precision, and Temperature Compensation

The technical credibility of a pressure decay test depends on four interdependent variables. Each must be specified in procurement documents and verified against the supplier's test report.

Test pressure polarity and magnitude: BSL-3 and BSL-4 laboratories operate under negative pressure relative to adjacent zones. The primary containment test must therefore be conducted at -500 Pa (negative pressure), simulating the operational pressure differential that the door seal must maintain. A supplementary positive pressure test at +500 Pa verifies bidirectional seal integrity, which is critical during VHP (vaporized hydrogen peroxide) decontamination cycles when internal pressures may transiently exceed ambient. Suppliers who test only at positive pressure are validating a condition that does not represent the primary operational failure mode.

Hold duration: ISO 14644-1:2015 [ISO 14644-1:2015] references a 20-minute pressure hold as a standard cleanroom integrity verification period. For high-containment applications (ABSL-3 and BSL-4), the WHO Laboratory Biosafety Manual and CDC/NIH guidelines recommend extending the hold to 30 minutes to capture slow-leak pathways that stabilize within the first 10 to 15 minutes of pressurization. A 20-minute test on a BSL-3 door is not a protocol error, but it provides lower confidence in detecting marginal seal degradation.

Measurement precision: The differential pressure transmitter used during testing must deliver accuracy of plus or minus 1 Pa or better. Instruments with plus or minus 5 Pa accuracy introduce measurement uncertainty that can mask a failing seal. Calibration of the test instrument must be current (within the manufacturer's recommended interval, typically 12 months) and traceable to a national metrology standard per ISO 17025 [ISO/IEC 17025:2017] accreditation requirements.

Leak rate calculation and temperature compensation: Raw pressure decay (delta-P over delta-t) must be corrected for test volume and ambient temperature variation during the hold period. A 2 degrees Celsius ambient temperature shift during a 30-minute test can produce a pressure artifact of 15 to 25 Pa in a small-volume test chamber, sufficient to convert a passing result into a false failure or — more dangerously — a false pass. Suppliers must document their temperature compensation method and the ambient temperature range recorded during testing.

Repeatability verification: A single test result is insufficient for qualification. Consecutive test runs (minimum three) must demonstrate consistency within plus or minus 10 percent of the mean pressure loss value. Batch sampling protocols for production runs should test a minimum of one unit per production lot, with full retest triggered if any unit fails.

The JIEHAO BS-01-IAD-1 references third-party National Inspection Center (NCSA) validation reports — specifically the NCSA-2021ZX-JH-0100-3 airtight door test report and the NCSA-2021ZX-JH-0100-4 ABSL-3 large animal laboratory room airtightness report. These reports represent the type of independent, accredited-laboratory validation that procurement teams should require as a baseline. The critical distinction is that NCSA testing is conducted under controlled conditions with calibrated instrumentation and documented methodology, not on a supplier's production floor with unverified equipment.

Mandatory Pressure Decay Acceptance Criteria for BSL-3 Biosafety-Inflatable-Airtight-Doors Tenders

Procurement specifications for biosafety-inflatable-airtight-doors at BSL-3 or higher must include the following quantified acceptance criteria:

  • Test pressure: -500 Pa (primary), +500 Pa (supplementary)
  • Hold duration: 30 minutes minimum
  • Maximum allowable pressure loss: 125 Pa over 30 minutes for BSL-3/BSL-4; 250 Pa over 20 minutes for standard cleanroom (ISO Class 7 or above)
  • Instrument accuracy: differential pressure transmitter with plus or minus 1 Pa resolution, calibrated per ISO 17025 within 12 months
  • Temperature compensation: documented method with ambient temperature logged at 1-minute intervals during test
  • Repeatability: three consecutive tests with results within plus or minus 10 percent of mean
  • Report format: third-party accredited laboratory report (NCSA, CNAS-accredited, or equivalent) with as-found data, test conditions, instrument calibration certificate reference, and pass/fail determination

Buyers who do not require an NCSA-certified or equivalent accredited-laboratory pressure decay test report before Factory Acceptance Testing accept an unquantified containment risk that no post-installation remediation can fully address.

3. Interlock Safety Logic Architecture: How State Machine Design Separates Engineered Safety from Ad-Hoc Wiring

This section evaluates the interlock logic design maturity of biosafety-inflatable-airtight-doors suppliers, defining the specific state machine, fault detection, and fail-safe requirements that prevent simultaneous-open containment breaches in cleanroom airlock configurations.

The Wiring-Diagram Mentality: Why Relay-Based Interlocks Fail in Multi-Door Containment Systems

The procurement failure mode in interlock specification is treating door interlocking as a simple electrical problem — two doors, one relay, mutual exclusion. This approach works for single-airlock, two-door configurations with no integration requirements. It fails catastrophically in BSL-3 facilities where airlock systems involve three or more doors, integration with HVAC pressure cascade controls, fire alarm override requirements, emergency egress compliance, and BMS (Building Management System) connectivity.

A relay-based interlock has no state awareness. It knows whether a contact is open or closed. It does not know whether the door is in transit (partially open), whether the pneumatic seal is fully inflated, whether the electromagnetic lock has engaged, or whether the differential pressure across the door has stabilized after the previous cycle. In a biosafety-inflatable-airtight-door with pneumatic seal technology — where inflation requires up to 5 seconds and deflation requires up to 5 seconds per the BS-01-IAD-1 specification — the interlock must account for seal state as a distinct variable independent of door leaf position.

Facilities that specify only "electromagnetic interlock" without defining the state machine architecture receive systems where a sensor failure or communication timeout can leave both doors in an indeterminate state, with no automated recovery path and no alarm escalation.

IEC 61508 Safety Integrity Levels and State Machine Requirements for Containment Airlocks

IEC 61508 [IEC 61508:2010] defines Safety Integrity Levels (SIL) as a measure of the reliability of safety-instrumented functions. For cleanroom and biosafety airlock applications, the relevant classification is:

  • SIL 1: Probability of dangerous failure on demand between 0.1 and 0.01 (1 in 10 to 1 in 100). Appropriate for standard cleanroom airlocks (ISO Class 7 and above) where containment breach consequences are limited to product contamination.
  • SIL 2: Probability of dangerous failure on demand between 0.01 and 0.001 (1 in 100 to 1 in 1,000). Required for BSL-3 and BSL-4 airlocks where containment breach consequences include personnel exposure to Risk Group 3 or 4 pathogens.

A SIL 2-rated interlock system requires redundant sensing (dual position sensors per door, dual pressure confirmation for pneumatic seal state), diagnostic coverage exceeding 60 percent of detectable failure modes, and a defined safe state for every identified fault condition.

The state machine architecture for a biosafety-inflatable-airtight-door interlock must define, at minimum, the following discrete states and transition conditions:

  • Door states: Closed-and-sealed (pneumatic seal inflated to 0.25 MPa or above, electromagnetic lock engaged), Opening (seal deflating, lock disengaged, leaf in transit), Open (leaf fully retracted, seal deflated), Closing (leaf returning, seal not yet inflated), Fault (any sensor disagreement or timeout)
  • Lock states: Locked (electromagnetic lock energized, position confirmed), Unlocked (lock de-energized, position confirmed), Indeterminate (lock command issued but position not confirmed within timeout — typically 2 seconds)
  • Alarm states: Normal, Warning (pressure below 0.20 MPa but above 0.15 MPa), Alarm (pressure below 0.15 MPa per BS-01-IAD-1 fault alarm threshold), Critical (simultaneous-open detected or sensor failure)
  • Transition conditions: Each state transition must require positive confirmation from at least two independent sensors. A door may not transition from Closed-and-sealed to Opening unless the interlock controller confirms that all other doors in the airlock group are in Closed-and-sealed state.

The JIEHAO BS-01-IAD-1 specifies Siemens PLC control with electromagnetic lock interlocking, RS232/RS485/TCP/IP communication, and BMS integration capability. This architecture supports the distributed control topology required for multi-door interlock networks. The critical procurement question is not whether the supplier uses a PLC, but whether the supplier can provide the documented state machine logic, fault tree analysis, and SIL classification for the interlock function.

Integration with emergency systems: The interlock must include a hardwired emergency override that bypasses the PLC for egress purposes (fire alarm activation or manual emergency release) while simultaneously triggering a containment breach alarm. This override must be fail-safe — meaning it defaults to the safe state (doors unlockable for egress) on power loss. The BS-01-IAD-1 includes an escape device, but procurement documents must specify the override's interaction with the interlock state machine: does the override reset the state machine, or does it place the system in a defined Emergency state that requires manual acknowledgment before returning to normal operation?

Interlock Specification Checklist for Biosafety-Inflatable-Airtight-Doors Procurement

  • Documented state machine diagram with all states, transitions, and fault conditions
  • SIL classification per IEC 61508 (SIL 2 minimum for BSL-3 applications)
  • Redundant position sensing: dual sensors per door leaf, dual pressure confirmation for pneumatic seal
  • Fault detection response time: 200 ms or less from fault detection to safe-state transition
  • Emergency override: hardwired bypass with simultaneous containment breach alarm, defined interaction with state machine
  • Communication timeout handling: defined safe state if PLC-to-door controller communication is lost for more than 2 seconds
  • Integration protocol documentation: BMS data points (door state, seal pressure, alarm status), fire alarm interface wiring diagram, access control system handshake protocol
  • Factory Acceptance Test (FAT) procedure: documented test of every state transition, including all fault injection scenarios (sensor disconnection, power loss, communication timeout)

A supplier who cannot produce a formal state machine diagram and fault tree analysis for their interlock system during the tender evaluation phase lacks the engineering maturity to deliver a SIL 2-rated containment barrier.

4. Calibration Traceability Depth: Why Measurement Confidence Determines Regulatory Defensibility of Containment Data

This section addresses the calibration and metrology traceability requirements that determine whether pressure readings and seal performance data from biosafety-inflatable-airtight-doors can withstand regulatory audit scrutiny under GMP, FDA, and ISO frameworks.

Confusing Calibration Frequency with Calibration Traceability

The most common metrology error in biosafety equipment procurement is specifying calibration frequency (e.g., "annual calibration required") without specifying traceability requirements. A differential pressure transmitter calibrated every 12 months by an unaccredited service provider using an untraced reference standard produces calibration certificates that have no regulatory standing. The certificate confirms that someone adjusted the instrument — it does not confirm that the adjustment was made against a reference with a documented, unbroken chain to a national or international measurement standard.

For biosafety-inflatable-airtight-doors operating in GMP-regulated environments, the pressure readings generated by the door's monitoring system (the BS-01-IAD-1 includes pressure monitoring with a low-pressure alarm at less than 0.15 MPa) become part of the facility's environmental monitoring dataset. Under FDA 21 CFR Part 211.68 [FDA 21 CFR Part 211.68], automatic, mechanical, and electronic equipment used in pharmaceutical manufacturing must be "routinely calibrated, inspected, or checked according to a written program designed to assure proper performance." EU GMP Annex 11 [EU GMP Annex 11] extends this to computerized systems, requiring that "the accuracy of the data input is checked" and that "the source of critical data" is validated.

If a regulatory inspector requests the calibration traceability chain for the differential pressure transmitter monitoring a BSL-3 airlock door, the facility must produce: the field instrument's calibration certificate, the reference standard used to calibrate it, that reference standard's own calibration certificate traceable to a higher-order standard, and ultimately a link to a national metrology institute (e.g., NIST, PTB, NIM). A gap anywhere in this chain renders the pressure data legally indefensible.

The Traceability Chain: From Field Instrument to National Reference Standard

The metrology traceability hierarchy for pressure measurement in biosafety-inflatable-airtight-doors follows a four-tier structure:

  • Tier 1 — National primary standard: Maintained by national metrology institutes (NIST in the United States, PTB in Germany, NIM in China). These are deadweight testers or mercury manometers with measurement uncertainty of plus or minus 0.001 percent of reading.
  • Tier 2 — Secondary reference standard: Maintained by ISO/IEC 17025 [ISO/IEC 17025:2017] accredited calibration laboratories (CNAS-accredited in China, A2LA or NVLAP-accredited in the United States). Calibrated against Tier 1 standards. Typical uncertainty: plus or minus 0.01 percent to plus or minus 0.05 percent of reading.
  • Tier 3 — Working standard: Maintained by the equipment supplier or facility calibration team. Calibrated against Tier 2 standards. Typical uncertainty: plus or minus 0.1 percent of reading.
  • Tier 4 — Field instrument: The differential pressure transmitter installed in the biosafety-inflatable-airtight-door (e.g., the pressure monitoring system in the BS-01-IAD-1 with RC1/8 pressure gauge interface). Calibrated against Tier 3 working standards. Required accuracy for BSL-3 applications: plus or minus 1 Pa.

Each tier must produce a calibration certificate that includes: the instrument identification (serial number, model), the reference standard used (with its own certificate number), the as-found readings (before adjustment), the as-left readings (after adjustment), the measurement uncertainty, the environmental conditions during calibration, and the calibration date and next-due date.

In-situ versus laboratory calibration: Removing a differential pressure transmitter from a biosafety-inflatable-airtight-door for laboratory calibration provides higher accuracy but creates downtime during which the door's pressure monitoring is unavailable. In-situ calibration using a portable reference standard avoids downtime but introduces environmental variables (vibration, temperature gradients, electromagnetic interference from nearby equipment) that increase measurement uncertainty. For GMP facilities, the decision must be documented in the calibration SOP with a risk assessment justifying the chosen approach.

Calibration interval determination: The default 12-month interval is a starting point, not a validated frequency. Optimal calibration intervals are determined by analyzing historical calibration data: if as-found readings consistently fall within tolerance at 12-month intervals, the interval may be extended to 18 or 24 months. If as-found readings show drift approaching tolerance limits, the interval must be shortened. For newly installed biosafety-inflatable-airtight-doors, an initial 6-month calibration interval is recommended for the first two years to establish a drift baseline.

JIEHAO's documentation practice — providing calibration interval recommendations per component and certificate documentation in a format compatible with 3Q (IQ/OQ/PQ) validation packages — represents the type of supplier-side calibration infrastructure that reduces the facility's metrology burden. However, the facility retains ultimate responsibility for maintaining the traceability chain and cannot delegate this to the equipment supplier.

Calibration Documentation Requirements for Regulatory-Compliant Biosafety-Inflatable-Airtight-Doors Installations

  • All differential pressure transmitters must be calibrated by an ISO/IEC 17025 accredited laboratory (CNAS, A2LA, NVLAP, or equivalent)
  • Calibration certificates must include as-found and as-left data, measurement uncertainty statement, and traceability to a national metrology standard
  • Calibration records must be retained for a minimum of 10 years for GMP facilities (per FDA 21 CFR Part 211 and EU GMP Annex 11 record retention requirements)
  • Electronic calibration records must comply with FDA 21 CFR Part 11 [FDA 21 CFR Part 11] requirements for electronic records and electronic signatures (audit trail, access controls, time-stamped entries)
  • Supplier-provided calibration certificates (e.g., for factory-calibrated instruments shipped with new biosafety-inflatable-airtight-doors) must be verified against the facility's traceability requirements before acceptance — a supplier certificate without a traceable reference standard identification is not sufficient for GMP compliance

A facility that cannot produce an unbroken calibration traceability chain from its airlock door pressure transmitter to a national reference standard within 24 hours of a regulatory request faces a documentation nonconformity that can escalate to a facility-wide GMP audit finding.

5. PLC Control Architecture and Differential Pressure Response: Why Controller Selection Defines Containment Stability During Transient Events

This section analyzes how PLC platform selection, control loop response time, and communication protocol architecture determine whether a biosafety-inflatable-airtight-door system can maintain regulatory-compliant differential pressure gradients during the transient pressure disturbances caused by door cycling events.

Specifying "PLC Control" Without Defining Response Time and Fail-Safe Behavior

Procurement documents that specify "PLC-controlled biosafety-inflatable-airtight-door" without defining the PLC platform, scan cycle time, control loop response time, and fail-safe mode are specifying a component category, not a performance requirement. The difference between a PLC with a 50 ms scan cycle and one with a 200 ms scan cycle is the difference between maintaining a 15 Pa differential pressure gradient during a door cycle and allowing a 3 to 5 second pressure excursion that temporarily equalizes the containment zone with the adjacent corridor.

ISO 14644-1:2015 [ISO 14644-1:2015] recommends a minimum 15 Pa differential pressure gradient between cleanroom zones of different classification. The WHO Laboratory Biosafety Manual [WHO Laboratory Biosafety Manual, 4th Edition] reinforces this for BSL-3 facilities, specifying that directional airflow (from clean to contaminated) must be maintained at all times during normal operations. When a biosafety-inflatable-airtight-door opens, the pneumatic seal deflates (up to 5 seconds per the BS-01-IAD-1 specification), the electromagnetic lock disengages, and the door leaf moves. During this sequence, the HVAC system must compensate for the temporary opening in the containment envelope. The PLC controlling the door must coordinate with the BMS and HVAC controllers to manage this transient.

A PLC with a 200 ms scan cycle introduces a 200 ms latency between detecting a pressure deviation and issuing a corrective command. At typical BSL-3 air change rates (15 to 25 air changes per hour), a 200 ms delay allows measurable pressure decay across the door opening. A 50 ms scan cycle reduces this latency by 75 percent, enabling tighter pressure control during the transient.

FDA 21 CFR Part 11 and Electronic Record Integrity in Door Control Systems

FDA 21 CFR Part 11 [FDA 21 CFR Part 11] establishes requirements for electronic records and electronic signatures in FDA-regulated environments. For biosafety-inflatable-airtight-doors installed in pharmaceutical manufacturing or vaccine production facilities (a significant application segment, given that BSL-3 facilities frequently support GMP-regulated biological product manufacturing), the door control system's electronic records — cycle logs, alarm histories, pressure trend data, user access logs — must comply with Part 11 requirements:

  • Audit trail: Every data entry, modification, or deletion must be recorded with a time stamp, user identification, and reason for change. The PLC and HMI (Human-Machine Interface) software must generate audit trails automatically without user intervention.
  • Access controls: Role-based access with unique user credentials. Operator, maintenance, and administrator roles must have differentiated permissions. Shared login credentials violate Part 11.
  • Electronic signatures: If electronic signatures are used for batch release or maintenance sign-off activities involving the door system, they must be linked to their respective electronic records and include the signer's printed name, date/time, and meaning of the signature (e.g., "reviewed," "approved").

Suppliers who offer PLC control without Part 11-compliant software architecture force the facility to implement a costly middleware layer or revert to paper-based records for the door system — creating a documentation island that complicates integrated batch record management.

Control System Specification Requirements for Biosafety-Inflatable-Airtight-Doors in GMP Environments

  • Scan cycle time: 50 ms or less for BSL-3 applications with active differential pressure control
  • Fail-safe mode: fail-secure (door defaults to closed-and-sealed on power loss, communication loss, or PLC fault) with hardwired emergency override for egress
  • Communication protocol stack: TCP/IP for BMS integration, RS485 for local device networking, RS232 for diagnostic/programming access
  • FDA 21 CFR Part 11 compliance: audit trail, access controls, electronic signature capability built into the PLC/HMI software — not added as a post-installation overlay
  • Software validation: supplier must provide a software validation package (functional specification, design specification, test protocols, test results) compatible with GAMP 5 [ISPE GAMP 5] Category 4 (configured product) or Category 5 (custom application) classification
  • Cybersecurity: network segmentation documentation, default password change verification, firmware update authentication mechanism

7. FAQ — Buyer's Guide

Q1: What is the expected service life of the silicone rubber pneumatic seal gasket, and how should replacement intervals be determined?

Silicone rubber pneumatic seals in biosafety-inflatable-airtight-doors are subject to compression set degradation over repeated inflation-deflation cycles. At an inflation pressure of 0.25 MPa with cycle frequencies typical of BSL-3 operations (50 to 200 cycles per day), the compression set of medical-grade silicone rubber reaches approximately 15 to 25 percent after 100,000 cycles per ASTM D395 testing methodology. Replacement should be triggered when pressure decay test results show a trend toward the 125 Pa pass/fail threshold, or at a fixed interval of 18 to 24 months — whichever comes first. Facilities should maintain a minimum of two spare gasket sets per door to avoid containment downtime during replacement.

Q2: How can procurement teams verify that a biosafety-inflatable-airtight-doors supplier has genuine BSL-3 deployment experience rather than only cleanroom-grade installations?

Request specific third-party validation reports issued by accredited national testing centers for BSL-3 or ABSL-3 containment conditions — not cleanroom classification reports. The critical distinction is that BSL-3 validation requires pressure decay testing under simulated containment conditions (negative pressure, extended hold duration, biological decontamination agent compatibility). For example, Shanghai Jiehao Biotechnology holds NCSA-2021ZX-JH-0100 series test reports covering airtight doors (report -3), ABSL-3 large animal laboratory room airtightness (report -4), pass boxes (report -1), and sink troughs (report -2), all issued by the National Inspection Center. Suppliers should also provide a reference list of BSL-3 installations with facility contact information for verification. A supplier claiming BSL-3 capability but offering only ISO Class 7 cleanroom test data has not demonstrated containment-grade performance.

Q3: What are the Total Cost of Ownership (TCO) variables that buyers frequently underestimate for biosafety-inflatable-airtight-doors?

The three most commonly underestimated TCO components are: (1) calibration costs for the differential pressure transmitter and pneumatic pressure monitoring system — annual calibration by an ISO 17025 accredited laboratory typically costs 800 to 1,500 USD per instrument, with two to three instruments per door; (2) pneumatic seal gasket replacement — material cost is 5 to 10 percent of the door's capital cost per replacement, but labor and revalidation (pressure decay retest, IQ/OQ/PQ amendment) can equal 30 to 50 percent of the original installation cost; (3) software validation maintenance — PLC firmware updates, HMI patches, and cybersecurity updates each trigger a revalidation cycle under GAMP 5, with typical costs of 2,000 to 5,000 USD per update event. Over a 15-year lifecycle, these recurring costs typically equal 150 to 250 percent of the initial capital expenditure.

Q4: How should biosafety-inflatable-airtight-doors be integrated with existing HVAC pressure cascade control systems?

Integration requires bidirectional data exchange between the door PLC and the BMS/HVAC controller. At minimum, the door system must transmit real-time door state (open, closed-and-sealed, in-transit, fault), seal pressure, and alarm status to the BMS via TCP/IP or BACnet protocol. The HVAC controller must receive a "door opening" signal at least 500 ms before the seal begins deflating, allowing the air handling unit to pre-adjust supply and exhaust damper positions to compensate for the temporary containment opening. The BS-01-IAD-1's RS232/RS485/TCP/IP communication stack supports this integration, but the specific BMS data point mapping and signal timing must be defined in the project's functional design specification and tested during OQ (Operational Qualification). Facilities using legacy BMS platforms with only RS485 connectivity should verify that the door controller supports Modbus RTU protocol at 9,600 baud or higher.

Q5: What regulatory acceptance testing is required for biosafety-inflatable-airtight-doors before a BSL-3 facility can begin operations?

Regulatory acceptance testing for BSL-3 biosafety-inflatable-airtight-doors typically includes: pressure decay testing per the facility's validated protocol (30-minute hold at -500 Pa, maximum 125 Pa loss), interlock function verification (all state transitions tested including fault injection), HEPA filter integrity testing for any integrated filtration, VHP decontamination compatibility verification (door seal integrity after exposure to 400 to 600 ppm H2O2 for 4 to 6 hours), and a complete IQ/OQ/PQ validation package. The IQ verifies installation against approved drawings and specifications. The OQ verifies operational performance under defined conditions (pressure decay, interlock logic, alarm functions, emergency override). The PQ verifies performance under simulated operational conditions over an extended period (typically 3 consecutive days of normal operational cycling). All test results must be documented in a format acceptable to the relevant regulatory authority (CDC/USDA for select agent facilities in the US, or equivalent national biosafety authority).
