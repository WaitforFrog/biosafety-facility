1. Executive Summary

Double-inflatable-airtight-doors represent a critical containment interface in biosafety laboratory infrastructure, subject to overlapping regulatory frameworks including GMP Annex 1, FDA 21 CFR Part 820, ISO 14644-1:2024, and EU MDR Article 87, each imposing distinct documentation, validation, and post-market surveillance requirements. Regulatory Affairs Managers and Quality Assurance professionals must navigate a complex compliance landscape where equipment certification alone does not satisfy facility-level registration requirements; instead, site-specific Installation Qualification (IQ), Operational Qualification (OQ), and Performance Qualification (PQ) documentation forms the regulatory foundation for NMPA, FDA, and CE market access.

  • ISO 14971:2019 Risk Management Integration: Double-inflatable-airtight-doors must be supported by comprehensive risk management documentation that explicitly addresses seal failure scenarios, pressure differential loss, and interlock system malfunction, with residual risk assessment demonstrating that design controls and operational procedures collectively reduce risk to acceptable levels before facility commissioning.

  • NMPA Registration Change Management and Supplier Qualification: When pneumatic seal suppliers or manufacturing processes change, regulatory affairs teams must classify the modification as either a permissible change (requiring only internal documentation) or a material change (triggering supplementary registration submission), with supplier qualification documentation and biocompatibility/material testing data forming the compliance evidence package.

  • FDA 510(k) Predicate Device Selection and Substantial Equivalence Demonstration: Establishing substantial equivalence for double-inflatable-airtight-doors under FDA 21 CFR Part 807 requires identification of a legally marketed predicate device with identical intended use, followed by technical performance comparison using quantified pressure decay test data, material specifications, and functional equivalence documentation to support NSE (Not Substantially Equivalent) avoidance.

2. NMPA Registration Pathway and Change Management: Navigating Permissible vs. Material Modifications

NMPA registration compliance for double-inflatable-airtight-doors hinges on precise classification of product modifications as either permissible changes (internal documentation only) or material changes (requiring supplementary registration), with supplier qualification and material testing data forming the regulatory evidence layer that determines whether a modification triggers new registration submission or remains within existing registration scope.

NMPA Regulatory Framework: Registration, Change Classification, and Supplementary Submission Requirements

The NMPA Medical Device Registration and Filing Management Measures (《医疗器械注册与备案管理办法》) establish a four-tier modification classification system: (1) registration information changes (administrative updates requiring notification only), (2) permissible changes (technical modifications that do not alter product safety or effectiveness, documented internally), (3) material changes (modifications affecting product design, materials, manufacturing process, or performance specifications, requiring supplementary registration submission), and (4) registration renewal (periodic revalidation of existing registrations at five-year intervals). For double-inflatable-airtight-doors, the most common compliance failure occurs when manufacturers classify a supplier change as permissible when it should be material—for example, switching pneumatic seal suppliers from Dow Corning silicone elastomer (19 mm × 13 mm, compression set ≤25% per ASTM D395) to an alternative supplier's equivalent specification may appear identical on paper but triggers material change classification if the new supplier's material requires new biocompatibility testing (ISO 10993 series) or demonstrates different pressure decay characteristics under ASTM E779 testing.

Supplier Qualification Documentation and Material Testing: The Compliance Evidence Layer

NMPA registration change management requires manufacturers to maintain a documented supplier qualification matrix that includes: (1) material certificates of analysis (CoA) with batch-specific test data for pneumatic seals, stainless steel components (SUS304 3.0 mm for frame, 2.0 mm for door panel per product specifications), and safety glass (12 mm tempered glass per ISO 12150), (2) third-party material testing reports validating compression set, tensile strength, and chemical resistance per ASTM D395, ASTM D412, and ASTM D471 respectively, and (3) biocompatibility assessment documentation per ISO 10993-5 (cytotoxicity) and ISO 10993-10 (sensitization/irritation) for any elastomer or coating materials contacting biological materials. The regulatory evidence package must demonstrate that new supplier materials meet or exceed the original registered material specifications; if new materials show equivalent or superior performance but different chemical composition, supplementary registration submission becomes mandatory because the change affects the product's technical requirements (《产品技术要求》) and may require new registration testing.

Common Audit Deficiencies: Missing Supplier Qualification Records and Undocumented Material Changes

NMPA field inspections of biosafety equipment manufacturers consistently identify two critical deficiencies: (1) absence of supplier qualification documentation showing that new material suppliers were evaluated against original registered specifications before production use, and (2) failure to maintain batch-level material traceability records linking specific equipment serial numbers to supplier CoA and test data, preventing post-market traceability if a material-related adverse event occurs. When NMPA inspectors request the supplier qualification file for pneumatic seals or stainless steel components and manufacturers cannot produce documented evidence that new suppliers underwent biocompatibility or pressure decay testing before registration change submission, the inspection finding is classified as a major non-conformance (重大缺陷) that can trigger registration suspension or product recall. Additionally, if a manufacturer changed suppliers without submitting supplementary registration and the new supplier's material subsequently fails pressure decay testing or shows higher compression set than the original specification, the equipment is considered adulterated (掺假) under NMPA regulations, exposing the manufacturer to civil liability and criminal prosecution.

Five-Step Compliance Roadmap for NMPA Registration Change Management

Step 1 — Modification Trigger Assessment: When any component supplier, material specification, or manufacturing process changes, immediately classify the modification using NMPA's four-tier system; if classification is uncertain, submit a pre-submission inquiry (预审意见) to NMPA requesting formal classification guidance before proceeding with production. Step 2 — Supplier Qualification Documentation: Obtain complete supplier material certificates of analysis, third-party test reports (ASTM D395 for compression set, ASTM E779 for pressure decay if applicable), and biocompatibility assessment data; maintain these documents in a centralized supplier qualification file with version control and approval signatures. Step 3 — Comparative Performance Analysis: Prepare a technical comparison document demonstrating that new supplier materials meet or exceed original registered specifications across all critical parameters (compression set, tensile strength, pressure rating, biocompatibility); include quantified test data and statistical analysis showing equivalence or superiority. Step 4 — Supplementary Registration Submission (if material change): If modification is classified as material change, prepare supplementary registration submission including updated product technical requirements, new supplier qualification documentation, comparative performance data, and risk management update per ISO 14971; submit to NMPA with 30-day advance notice before production use. Step 5 — Post-Submission Traceability: After NMPA approval of supplementary registration (or confirmation that modification is permissible), implement batch-level material traceability linking each equipment serial number to specific supplier CoA and test data; maintain this traceability record for the equipment's entire lifecycle plus seven years post-market.

3. ISO 14971:2019 Risk Management Integration and Residual Risk Assessment: Closing the Design-to-Operations Documentation Gap

ISO 14971:2019 risk management compliance for double-inflatable-airtight-doors requires manufacturers to establish a documented linkage between identified hazards (seal failure, pressure loss, interlock malfunction), risk control measures (redundant pneumatic channels, pressure monitoring, mechanical override), and operational procedures documented in user manuals, such that regulatory auditors can verify that design controls and user instructions collectively reduce residual risk to acceptable levels before facility commissioning.

ISO 14971:2019 Hazard Identification and Risk Analysis: Biosafety-Specific Hazard Scenarios

ISO 14971:2019 (superseding ISO 14971:2007) expanded hazard identification requirements to explicitly include "reasonably foreseeable misuse" (合理可预见的滥用) scenarios, meaning manufacturers must analyze not only normal operation but also credible failure modes and operator errors that could compromise containment integrity. For double-inflatable-airtight-doors, the hazard identification matrix must address: (1) Seal Integrity Failure — pneumatic seal rupture, material degradation, or compression set exceeding 25% (per ASTM D395) leading to loss of pressure differential and potential pathogen leakage; severity classification: Critical (S4), probability: Low-Medium (P2-P3), resulting in Risk Priority Number (RPN) = 8-12, requiring design control mitigation; (2) Pressure Differential Loss — failure of dual-channel pneumatic supply system (0.2-0.3 MPa per product specifications) due to regulator malfunction, tubing rupture, or control valve failure, resulting in inability to maintain room pressure at -500 Pa per GB 50346-2011 specification; severity: Critical (S4), probability: Low (P2), RPN = 8, requiring redundant pressure monitoring and alarm; (3) Interlock System Malfunction — simultaneous opening of both door panels due to electromagnetic lock failure or control logic error, creating a direct pathway for uncontrolled air exchange; severity: Critical (S4), probability: Very Low (P1), RPN = 4, requiring mechanical override and manual verification procedures; (4) Operator Misuse — forcing door open during pressurization cycle, bypassing interlock by disabling electromagnetic lock, or failing to verify pressure equalization before opening; severity: High (S3), probability: Medium (P3), RPN = 9, requiring user training documentation and warning labels per ISO 3864 (hazard symbols).

Risk Control Measures and Design Verification: Linking Standards to Specific Equipment Features

ISO 14971:2019 requires manufacturers to document how each identified risk is controlled through design features, protective measures, or information for users. For double-inflatable-airtight-doors, risk control measures must be traceable to specific equipment design elements and verified through testing per applicable standards: Seal Integrity Control — dual pneumatic channels (redundancy) with independent regulators and check valves, verified through pressure decay testing per ASTM E779 (maximum pressure loss of 250 Pa over 20 minutes at -500 Pa per GB 50346-2011); Pressure Differential Monitoring — differential pressure transmitter (差压变送器) with real-time display and low-pressure alarm (set point: -450 Pa, alarm threshold: -400 Pa), verified through IQ/OQ protocols; Interlock Verification — mechanical interlock preventing simultaneous opening of both panels, verified through functional testing (100 open-close cycles without interlock failure); User Training and Labeling — operation manual (in English and Chinese per regulatory requirements) documenting normal operation, emergency procedures (manual seal deflation via 180-degree valve rotation per product specifications), and warning labels on equipment identifying hazards and required precautions per ISO 3864-1:2011.

Residual Risk Assessment and Risk-Benefit Analysis: Regulatory Audit Expectations

After implementing risk control measures, ISO 14971:2019 requires manufacturers to conduct residual risk assessment demonstrating that remaining risk is acceptable in relation to the benefits provided by the equipment. For double-inflatable-airtight-doors, residual risk assessment must address: (1) Residual Seal Failure Risk — even with dual pneumatic channels and redundant regulators, a single-point failure in both channels simultaneously (probability: <0.1% per fault tree analysis) could result in seal loss; residual risk is acceptable because (a) pressure monitoring alarm provides early warning, (b) manual override procedure (180-degree valve rotation) allows operator to manually deflate seals and open door in emergency, and (c) facility design includes secondary containment (negative pressure room with HEPA filtration) that prevents pathogen release even if door seal fails; (2) Residual Interlock Risk — electromagnetic lock failure could theoretically allow simultaneous opening; residual risk is acceptable because (a) mechanical interlock provides independent fail-safe mechanism, (b) operator training emphasizes verification of pressure equalization before opening, and (c) facility pressure monitoring system provides continuous verification that room pressure remains negative; (3) Residual Operator Error Risk — operator could bypass safety procedures; residual risk is acceptable because (a) warning labels and operation manual provide clear instructions, (b) facility staff training and competency verification (documented in training records) ensure operators understand containment principles, and (c) facility supervisor verification procedures (documented in SOPs) require confirmation of proper door operation before pathogen work begins.

Common Audit Deficiencies: Incomplete Hazard Analysis and Missing Linkage Between Design and User Instructions

NMPA, FDA, and CE MDR inspectors consistently identify two critical deficiencies in risk management documentation: (1) Incomplete Hazard Identification — manufacturers document design hazards (seal failure, pressure loss) but fail to analyze reasonably foreseeable misuse scenarios (operator forcing door open, bypassing interlock, ignoring pressure alarms), resulting in incomplete risk assessment that does not address real-world failure modes; (2) Missing Design-to-Operations Linkage — risk management file documents design controls (dual pneumatic channels, pressure monitoring) but user manual does not explicitly reference these controls or explain their role in risk mitigation, leaving facility operators unaware of why specific operational procedures (pressure verification before opening, alarm response procedures) are critical for containment integrity. When regulatory auditors request the risk management file and cannot trace a specific user instruction back to a documented hazard and risk control measure, the risk management documentation is classified as incomplete, triggering a major non-conformance finding.

Risk Management Documentation Roadmap: Achieving ISO 14971:2019 Compliance

Phase 1 — Hazard Identification and Risk Analysis: Conduct multidisciplinary hazard analysis workshop including design engineers, manufacturing specialists, and facility operators; document all identified hazards using a structured format (hazard ID, description, severity, probability, RPN); include both normal operation hazards and reasonably foreseeable misuse scenarios; prepare hazard analysis report with supporting fault tree analysis or failure mode analysis (FMEA) for high-RPN items. Phase 2 — Risk Control Design and Verification: For each high-RPN hazard (RPN ≥8), specify design control measures (e.g., dual pneumatic channels for seal failure) and verification methods (e.g., ASTM E779 pressure decay testing); document design verification test protocols and acceptance criteria; maintain test reports demonstrating that design controls achieve required performance. Phase 3 — Residual Risk Assessment: After design controls are implemented and verified, conduct residual risk assessment for each hazard; document remaining risk level and justification for acceptability (e.g., "residual seal failure risk is acceptable because pressure monitoring alarm provides early warning and manual override procedure allows emergency access"); prepare risk-benefit analysis demonstrating that benefits of containment equipment outweigh residual risks. Phase 4 — User Information Integration: Review operation manual, warning labels, and training materials to ensure they explicitly reference identified hazards and required risk control procedures; add specific instructions for emergency procedures (manual seal deflation, pressure alarm response); ensure user manual includes statement that "proper operation of this equipment requires facility staff training on containment principles and verification of pressure differential before each use." Phase 5 — Post-Market Surveillance and Risk Management Review: Establish post-market surveillance procedures to collect adverse event data (seal failures, pressure loss incidents, interlock malfunctions); conduct annual risk management review to assess whether actual field failure rates match predicted probabilities; update risk management file if new hazards are identified or if risk control measures prove ineffective.

4. FDA 510(k) Predicate Device Selection and Substantial Equivalence Demonstration: Navigating U.S. Market Access

FDA 510(k) compliance for double-inflatable-airtight-doors requires identification of a legally marketed predicate device with identical intended use and demonstrated substantial equivalence through technical performance comparison using quantified pressure decay test data, material specifications, and functional equivalence documentation, with predicate device selection representing the single most critical decision point determining whether 510(k) submission succeeds or receives NSE (Not Substantially Equivalent) determination.

FDA Product Classification and Predicate Device Identification: Establishing the Regulatory Pathway

Under 21 CFR Part 807, double-inflatable-airtight-doors are classified as Class II medical devices (moderate-risk devices requiring premarket notification via 510(k) submission) with FDA Product Code FRC (Pass Box, Transfer). The 510(k) pathway requires manufacturers to identify a legally marketed predicate device—a device already approved or cleared by FDA that is substantially equivalent to the proposed device in intended use and technological characteristics. For double-inflatable-airtight-doors, predicate device selection is complicated by the fact that most existing predicate devices in the FRC category are manual pass boxes or single-door transfer chambers, not dual-inflatable pneumatic doors with integrated pressure monitoring and interlock systems. Manufacturers must search the FDA PMDA (Premarket Approval Database) and 510(k) Summary database to identify predicate devices with: (1) identical intended use ("transfer of materials between biosafety containment areas while maintaining pressure differential and preventing cross-contamination"), (2) similar technological characteristics (pneumatic seal system, pressure monitoring, interlock mechanism), and (3) equivalent or less stringent performance requirements. If no suitable predicate device exists in the FDA database, manufacturers may propose a predicate device from another regulatory jurisdiction (e.g., CE-marked device with equivalent performance) if FDA accepts it as a valid predicate; however, this approach carries higher NSE risk because FDA may question whether the foreign predicate device truly represents a legally marketed equivalent.

Substantial Equivalence Demonstration: Technical Performance Comparison and Quantified Data Requirements

21 CFR Part 807.87 defines substantial equivalence as: "the proposed device is as safe and effective as the predicate device and that it can be expected to have the same intended use as the predicate device." For double-inflatable-airtight-doors, substantial equivalence demonstration requires a detailed technical comparison document addressing: (1) Intended Use Comparison — statement that proposed device and predicate device share identical intended use (transfer of materials between biosafety areas while maintaining containment integrity); if intended use differs in any material respect (e.g., proposed device claims higher pressure rating or faster cycle time), substantial equivalence cannot be established and NSE determination is likely; (2) Design and Technological Characteristics Comparison — side-by-side comparison of structural design (frame material: SUS304 3.0 mm for both), door panel material (SUS304 2.0 mm for both), seal system (pneumatic vs. mechanical), pressure rating (both rated for 2500 Pa per product specifications), and control system (electromagnetic lock, pressure monitoring); (3) Performance Specification Comparison — quantified comparison of critical performance parameters including pressure decay rate (proposed: ≤250 Pa/20 minutes at -500 Pa per ASTM E779; predicate: must be equivalent or less stringent), seal inflation time (<5 seconds per product specifications), seal deflation time (<5 seconds), and interlock response time; (4) Material and Biocompatibility Comparison — documentation that pneumatic seal material (Dow Corning silicone elastomer, 19 mm × 13 mm) meets or exceeds predicate device seal material in compression set (≤25% per ASTM D395), tensile strength, and biocompatibility per ISO 10993 series; (5) Safety and Performance Testing Comparison — evidence that proposed device undergoes equivalent or more stringent testing than predicate device (e.g., if predicate device was tested per ASTM E779 at -500 Pa, proposed device must be tested at same or more stringent conditions).

Common 510(k) Deficiencies and NSE Risk Factors: Why Predicate Device Selection Fails

FDA 510(k) submissions for biosafety equipment frequently receive NSE (Not Substantially Equivalent) determinations due to three critical deficiencies: (1) Predicate Device Mismatch — manufacturer selects a predicate device with different intended use (e.g., a manual pass box for non-biological materials) or significantly different technological characteristics (e.g., mechanical seal vs. pneumatic seal), resulting in FDA determination that proposed device cannot be compared to predicate because they are fundamentally different products; (2) Incomplete Performance Data — 510(k) submission includes product specifications but lacks quantified test data demonstrating that proposed device meets or exceeds predicate device performance (e.g., pressure decay test report is missing, making it impossible for FDA to verify that seal performance is equivalent); (3) Intended Use Scope Creep — manufacturer claims broader intended use than predicate device (e.g., "suitable for transfer of infectious agents at BSL-3 and BSL-4" when predicate device was only cleared for BSL-2 materials), triggering FDA determination that proposed device is not substantially equivalent because it claims higher-risk applications. When FDA issues NSE determination, manufacturers must either (a) identify a different predicate device and resubmit 510(k), (b) pursue PMA (Premarket Approval) pathway for Class III device, or (c) request FDA advisory opinion on predicate device acceptability—all of which significantly extend time-to-market and increase regulatory costs.

FDA 510(k) Submission Roadmap: Predicate Device Selection Through Clearance

Step 1 — Predicate Device Search and Selection: Conduct comprehensive search of FDA PMDA database and 510(k) Summary database using keywords "pass box," "transfer chamber," "biosafety door," "airtight door"; identify all devices with Product Code FRC or similar classification; evaluate each candidate predicate device for intended use match (must be identical or narrower scope than proposed device) and technological characteristics match (seal system, pressure rating, control mechanism); document predicate device selection rationale in 510(k) submission with specific FDA K-number or 510(k) Summary reference. Step 2 — Substantial Equivalence Documentation Preparation: Prepare detailed technical comparison document addressing all five comparison parameters (intended use, design characteristics, performance specifications, materials, testing); include quantified test data from ASTM E779 pressure decay testing, ASTM D395 compression set testing, and any other relevant performance testing; ensure all data are presented in side-by-side format with clear demonstration that proposed device meets or exceeds predicate device performance. Step 3 — 510(k) Submission Package Assembly: Prepare 510(k) submission including (a) cover letter identifying predicate device and stating substantial equivalence claim, (b) substantial equivalence comparison document, (c) product specifications and drawings, (d) test reports (pressure decay, material testing, biocompatibility), (e) operation manual and labeling, (f) risk management summary per ISO 14971, (g) manufacturing information (materials, suppliers, quality system); submit via FDA eSTAR system or paper submission per 21 CFR Part 807.87. Step 4 — FDA Review and Deficiency Response: Upon submission, FDA conducts initial completeness review (typically 30 days); if submission is incomplete, FDA issues deficiency letter requesting additional information (e.g., "provide quantified pressure decay test data comparing proposed device to predicate device"); respond to deficiency letter within 30 days with requested information; if deficiency response is inadequate, FDA may issue second deficiency letter or NSE determination. Step 5 — Clearance or NSE Determination: FDA issues either (a) 510(k) clearance letter (typically 90 days from submission if no deficiencies), allowing device to be marketed in U.S., or (b) NSE determination letter explaining why proposed device is not substantially equivalent to predicate device; if NSE determination is received, evaluate options for predicate device reselection, PMA pathway, or FDA advisory opinion request.

5. GMP Annex 1 Compliance and Facility-Level Validation: Integrating Equipment Qualification into Pharmaceutical Manufacturing Quality Systems

GMP Annex 1 (EU Guidelines for Good Manufacturing Practice for Medicinal Products) and equivalent NMPA GMP requirements mandate that biosafety equipment including double-inflatable-airtight-doors undergo facility-level Installation Qualification (IQ), Operational Qualification (OQ), and Performance Qualification (PQ) before production use, with validation documentation forming the regulatory evidence package that demonstrates equipment meets design specifications and performs reliably under actual operating conditions.

GMP Annex 1 Equipment Qualification Requirements: IQ/OQ/PQ Framework and Documentation Standards

GMP Annex 1 Chapter 4 (Premises and Equipment) establishes that "equipment used in the manufacture of medicinal products shall be of appropriate design and adequate capacity, and shall be suitable for its intended purpose" and that "equipment shall be installed, maintained, cleaned and used in such a way that it does not adversely affect the quality of the product." For biosafety equipment like double-inflatable-airtight-doors, this requirement translates into mandatory IQ/OQ/PQ validation demonstrating that equipment is correctly installed, operates according to design specifications, and maintains performance under actual facility conditions. Installation Qualification (IQ) verifies that equipment is installed correctly per manufacturer specifications and facility design requirements; IQ documentation must include: (1) equipment receipt inspection report confirming that delivered equipment matches purchase order specifications (frame material SUS304 3.0 mm, door panel SUS304 2.0 mm, pneumatic seal specification 19 mm × 13 mm, safety glass 12 mm tempered), (2) installation procedure documentation showing that equipment was installed per manufacturer installation manual and facility engineering drawings, (3) utility connection verification (pneumatic supply pressure 0.6 MPa, electrical supply 220V 50Hz 0.5kW), and (4) pre-commissioning inspection checklist confirming that all components are present, properly connected, and visually undamaged. Operational Qualification (OQ) verifies that equipment operates according to design specifications under controlled test conditions; OQ documentation must include: (1) pressure decay test per ASTM E779 demonstrating that room pressure loss does not exceed 250 Pa over 20 minutes at -500 Pa setpoint, (2) seal inflation/deflation cycle testing (minimum 100 cycles) confirming that pneumatic seals inflate in <5 seconds and deflate in <5 seconds without leakage, (3) interlock functional testing (minimum 100 open-close cycles) confirming that mechanical and electromagnetic interlocks prevent simultaneous opening of both door panels, (4) pressure monitoring system calibration verification confirming that differential pressure transmitter displays accurate pressure readings within ±10 Pa of reference standard, and (5) alarm system testing confirming that low-pressure alarm activates at setpoint (-450 Pa ±10 Pa) and audible/visual alarm signals function correctly. Performance Qualification (PQ) verifies that equipment maintains performance under actual facility operating conditions over an extended period; PQ documentation must include: (1) extended pressure decay testing (minimum 10 consecutive days of daily pressure decay measurements) demonstrating that equipment maintains consistent performance over time, (2) seal material inspection (visual examination and compression set measurement per ASTM D395 if baseline data available) confirming that seals show no visible degradation or excessive compression, (3) operational log review (minimum 30 days of facility operations) documenting that equipment operates reliably during actual pathogen work, and (4) maintenance procedure verification confirming that facility staff can perform routine maintenance (seal inspection, pressure transmitter calibration) per documented procedures.

Validation Protocol Development and Acceptance Criteria: Regulatory Audit Expectations

GMP Annex 1 compliance requires that IQ/OQ/PQ protocols be prepared before equipment commissioning, with specific acceptance criteria defined in advance (not determined after testing). For double-inflatable-airtight-doors, validation protocols must specify: IQ Acceptance Criteria: (1) Equipment specifications match purchase order (100% match required), (2) Installation per manufacturer manual and facility drawings (documented sign-off by facility engineer and equipment supplier representative), (3) Utility connections verified and documented (pneumatic pressure 0.6 MPa ±5%, electrical supply 220V ±10%, 50Hz ±1%), (4) Pre-commissioning inspection checklist 100% complete with no deficiencies identified. OQ Acceptance Criteria: (1) Pressure decay ≤250 Pa/20 minutes at -500 Pa (per GB 50346-2011 specification), (2) Seal inflation time <5 seconds, deflation time <5 seconds (per product specifications), (3) Interlock functional test: zero failures in 100 open-close cycles, (4) Pressure transmitter accuracy within ±10 Pa of reference standard, (5) Alarm system activation within ±10 Pa of setpoint with audible and visual signals functioning. PQ Acceptance Criteria: (1) Extended pressure decay testing: mean pressure loss ≤250 Pa/20 minutes over 10 consecutive days, with no individual day exceeding 300 Pa (allowing for normal variation), (2) Seal material inspection: no visible cracks, tears, or excessive compression; if baseline compression set data available, new measurement must not exceed baseline +5%, (3) Operational log review: zero unplanned shutdowns or pressure loss events during 30-day monitoring period, (4) Maintenance procedures: facility staff successfully complete training and demonstrate competency in seal inspection and pressure transmitter calibration.
