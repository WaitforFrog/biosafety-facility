hood-fumigation-chambers Installation and Commissioning: Differential Pressure Control and VHP System Integration Guide

1. Executive Summary

This guide establishes the field commissioning procedures for hood-fumigation-chambers equipment, focusing on pressure control validation, hydrogen peroxide vapor system interlock verification, and airtightness certification required for biosafety laboratory deployment. The installation sequence prioritizes mechanical integrity testing before electrical system activation, followed by integrated system performance validation under simulated operational conditions. Three critical commissioning procedures—differential pressure sensor calibration, VHP cycle interlock debugging, and pass box airflow verification—must be completed in sequence before equipment handover to ensure compliance with ISO 14644-1 cleanroom standards and WHO Laboratory Biosafety Manual requirements.

  • Differential pressure transmitters must be zero-calibrated on-site using reference gauges traceable to ISO 17025 accreditation, with as-found and as-left values documented before any operational cycle begins.
  • VHP introduction cycles require verified HVAC interlock closure and emergency exhaust activation testing at concentrations exceeding 5 ppm hydrogen peroxide to prevent explosive vapor accumulation in downstream ductwork.
  • Final commissioning documentation must cross-reference all test equipment serial numbers to valid calibration certificates and include photographic evidence of sensor mounting, cable routing, and interlock activation sequences.

2. Pre-Installation Site Verification and Structural Load Assessment

This section confirms that the installation site meets mechanical load capacity, utility infrastructure, and environmental control prerequisites before equipment delivery and mounting begins.

Prerequisite: Verifying Structural Load Capacity and Utility Infrastructure Readiness

The hood-fumigation-chambers unit requires a reinforced floor slab with minimum compressive strength of 25 MPa and verified anchor embedment depth of 80 mm minimum for M12 expansion anchors. Before equipment arrival, obtain structural drawings from the facility engineering department and confirm that the installation location has been surveyed for dead load (equipment weight typically 450–650 kg depending on chamber volume) plus dynamic load from internal circulation fans (additional 15–20% load factor per SMACNA guidelines). Verify that electrical supply meets 380 V three-phase 50 Hz or 60 Hz specification with dedicated 32 A circuit breaker and earth grounding resistance below 4 ohms per IEC 61936-1.

Utility infrastructure must include compressed air supply certified to ISO 8573-1:2010 Class 2 (oil content ≤0.1 mg/m³, water content ≤3 mg/m³) at 6 bar minimum supply pressure with 10 micron particulate filtration. Verify that the facility HVAC system can accommodate the hood-fumigation-chambers exhaust connection (typically 150–200 m³/h airflow) without exceeding duct velocity limits of 5 m/s per ASHRAE 62.1. Document all utility verification measurements on a site readiness checklist and obtain facility manager sign-off before scheduling equipment delivery.

Procedure: Foundation Anchor Installation and Torque Verification Sequence

Locate the four anchor points on the equipment base using the manufacturer-provided installation template. Mark anchor positions on the floor slab using a chalk line and center punch. Drill four holes to 12 mm diameter and 80 mm depth using a rotary hammer drill with depth stop collar. Clean each hole with compressed air to remove concrete dust. Insert M12 expansion anchors and hand-tighten until snug contact is achieved. Using a calibrated click-type torque wrench set to 80 Nm (±5% accuracy per ISO 6789), apply torque in a cross-pattern sequence: anchor 1 (front-left) → anchor 3 (rear-right) → anchor 2 (front-right) → anchor 4 (rear-left). Re-verify each anchor torque after 24 hours to confirm no settlement has occurred.

After 24-hour settlement period, re-measure all four anchor torques using the same calibrated torque wrench. All four anchors must maintain 80 Nm ±4 Nm torque value; any anchor reading below 76 Nm indicates potential concrete failure or anchor slip and requires re-drilling at an offset location. Perform a visual inspection of the concrete surface around each anchor for radial cracks or spalling; any visible damage requires structural engineer assessment before proceeding. Document anchor torque values, re-check values, and photographic evidence in the commissioning record.

3. Differential Pressure Sensor Calibration and Zero-Point Adjustment

This section establishes the field calibration procedure for installed pressure transmitters, ensuring accurate differential pressure monitoring throughout the VHP cycle and post-cycle aeration phase.

Prerequisite: Pressure Transmitter Mounting Verification and Pre-Calibration Inspection

Before any calibration work begins, verify that the differential pressure transmitter has been powered continuously for a minimum of 30 minutes to allow internal electronics to stabilize. Inspect the process connection ports (high-pressure and low-pressure sides) for any visible damage, corrosion, or loose fittings. Using a torque wrench set to 15 Nm, verify that the process connection fittings are secure; loose connections introduce measurement error that cannot be corrected by software calibration. Check that the transmitter cable shield is grounded to the control cabinet earth bus using a dedicated ground lug; ungrounded shields create 50 Hz noise that corrupts pressure readings. Visually confirm that the transmitter mounting bracket is free from stress or bending that could introduce zero offset caused by mechanical strain on the sensor element.

Procedure: Zero-Point Calibration Using Reference Pressure Gauge and Atmospheric Vent

Obtain a reference pressure gauge with ±0.05% full-scale accuracy and a valid ISO 17025 calibration certificate dated within the past 12 months. Connect the reference gauge to a tee fitting on the high-pressure port of the transmitter. Vent both the high-pressure and low-pressure ports of the transmitter to atmosphere using 6 mm tubing connected to the facility compressed air vent line (ensure the vent line is clean and oil-free). Allow 5 minutes for pressure equalization. Record the transmitter output reading (in Pa or mbar) displayed on the control system HMI. The reading should be 0.0 Pa ±2 Pa; if the reading deviates beyond this range, the transmitter zero requires adjustment. Access the transmitter zero-trim potentiometer (located on the circuit board inside the transmitter enclosure) using a small flathead screwdriver. Rotate the potentiometer clockwise to increase the zero reading or counterclockwise to decrease it. Make adjustments in 0.5 Pa increments and allow 10 seconds for the reading to stabilize after each adjustment. Once the reading stabilizes at 0.0 Pa ±1 Pa, record this as-left zero value and document the adjustment in the calibration log.

Acceptance: Span Calibration Verification and Calibration Certificate Issuance

After zero-point adjustment, apply a known reference pressure of 50 Pa to the high-pressure port using a precision pressure regulator connected to the 6 bar compressed air supply. Record the transmitter output reading. Calculate the error as: Error (%) = [(Transmitter Reading − Reference Reading) / 50 Pa] × 100. The error must not exceed ±1% full-scale (±0.5 Pa for a 0–100 Pa transmitter). If the error exceeds this limit, adjust the span potentiometer in 0.5 Pa increments until the error falls within specification. Document all as-found and as-left calibration data, reference gauge serial number, calibration certificate reference number, and next calibration due date (typically 12 months from calibration date) in the commissioning record. Issue a calibration certificate per ISO 17025 format and file it in the equipment documentation archive.

4. VHP Disinfection System Interlock Debugging and HVAC Integration Verification

This section verifies that the hydrogen peroxide vapor introduction cycle cannot proceed unless HVAC dampers close, room pressure stabilizes, and emergency exhaust systems are armed and functional.

Prerequisite: HVAC Damper Position Verification and Interlock Wiring Confirmation

Before initiating any VHP cycle test, verify that the HVAC supply and exhaust dampers are physically present and mechanically functional. Manually actuate each damper using the control system HMI to confirm that the damper moves from fully open to fully closed position within 15 seconds. Inspect the damper actuator wiring for continuity using a digital multimeter; measure resistance between the actuator common terminal and the open/close command terminals—resistance should be below 5 ohms when the actuator is de-energized. Verify that the damper position feedback sensors (typically potentiometric or inductive proximity switches) are wired to the programmable logic controller (PLC) input modules. Test each feedback sensor by manually moving the damper and confirming that the PLC input changes state within 2 seconds of damper movement. Confirm that the emergency exhaust solenoid valve is wired to a dedicated PLC output module with manual override capability. Document all damper positions, actuator resistances, and feedback sensor responses in the interlock verification log.

Procedure: VHP Cycle Simulation and Interlock Activation Sequence Testing

Initiate a VHP cycle test using the control system HMI in "commissioning mode" (if available) or using a reduced-concentration test cycle. Set the target hydrogen peroxide concentration to 0.5 mg/L (approximately 50% of the normal operating concentration) to minimize safety risk during testing. Start the cycle and monitor the following sequence: (1) HVAC supply damper closes within 15 seconds of cycle start—verify closure using damper position feedback; (2) HVAC exhaust damper closes within 15 seconds—verify closure; (3) room pressure setpoint transitions to negative 10 Pa and stabilizes within 30 seconds—verify using the differential pressure transmitter; (4) hydrogen peroxide vapor introduction begins and concentration rises toward 0.5 mg/L—monitor using the H₂O₂ concentration sensor (electrochemical or infrared, range 0–10 mg/L, accuracy ±5% reading); (5) after 10-minute dwell time, aeration phase begins and HVAC exhaust damper opens—verify damper opening; (6) hydrogen peroxide concentration decreases below 1 ppm within 20 minutes—verify using concentration sensor. If any step fails to complete within the specified time window, stop the cycle immediately, document the failure, and investigate the root cause before proceeding.

Acceptance: Emergency Exhaust Activation and High-Concentration Alarm Response

Simulate a high hydrogen peroxide concentration alarm by manually injecting a 5 ppm H₂O₂ test signal into the concentration sensor input (using a precision signal generator connected to the sensor analog input, or by temporarily disconnecting the sensor and applying a known voltage equivalent to 5 ppm). Verify that the PLC detects the high-concentration alarm within 5 seconds and activates the emergency exhaust solenoid valve. Confirm that the emergency exhaust valve opens (audible click or visual position indicator) and that room pressure returns to neutral within 30 seconds. Verify that a visual alarm indicator (LED or HMI message) activates on the control system and that an audible alarm sounds (if equipped). Document the alarm response time, emergency exhaust activation time, and pressure recovery time in the commissioning record. Repeat this test three times to confirm consistent performance. After all tests are complete, restore the concentration sensor to normal operation and verify that the alarm clears automatically when the simulated high-concentration signal is removed.

5. Pass Box Airflow Performance and Interlock Verification Testing

This section validates that the hood-fumigation-chambers pass box function (when not in VHP mode) maintains specified airflow velocity, filter integrity, and door interlock performance per IEST-RP-CC001 standards.

Prerequisite: HEPA Filter Integrity and Airflow Measurement Equipment Calibration

Before conducting airflow performance tests, verify that the HEPA filter installed in the pass box has not been previously used or has been replaced per manufacturer specifications. Inspect the filter frame for any visible damage, tears, or deformation; a damaged filter must be replaced before testing proceeds. Obtain a thermal anemometer (hot-wire or vane type, range 0–2 m/s, accuracy ±3% reading) with a valid ISO 17025 calibration certificate dated within the past 6 months. Calibrate the anemometer using a reference wind tunnel or calibration chamber if available; if not available, document the anemometer serial number and calibration certificate reference in the commissioning record. Verify that the pass box HVAC system is operating at design airflow (typically 150–200 m³/h for a 1 m³ pass box) by checking the supply fan speed and verifying that the supply damper is fully open.

Procedure: Face Velocity Measurement and DOP Leak Test Execution

Measure the HEPA filter face velocity at nine points arranged in a 3×3 grid across the filter face. Divide the filter face into nine equal squares and measure velocity at the center of each square using the thermal anemometer. Hold the anemometer probe perpendicular to the filter face and record the reading after 10 seconds of stabilization. Calculate the average face velocity from the nine measurements. The acceptance criterion is 0.35–0.5 m/s per IEST-RP-CC001. If the average face velocity falls outside this range, adjust the supply fan speed or damper position and repeat the measurement. After confirming acceptable face velocity, perform an in-situ DOP (dioctyl phthalate) or PAO (polyalphaolefin) leak test per IEST-RP-CC001 Method 3. Connect a DOP aerosol generator to the pass box inlet and introduce challenge aerosol upstream of the HEPA filter. Using a photometer probe, scan the downstream face of the HEPA filter at the same nine grid points. Record the photometer reading at each point. The acceptance criterion is no single point reading exceeding 0.01% of the upstream challenge concentration. If any point exceeds this limit, the filter has failed and must be replaced.

DOP Leak Test (single point) <0.01% upstream Photometer scan at 9 grid points

Acceptance: Door Interlock Functionality and Pass Box Pressure Differential Verification

With the pass box HVAC system operating at design airflow, verify the door interlock sequence: (1) open door A (entry side) and confirm that door B (exit side) remains locked and cannot be opened manually; (2) close door A and wait for the interlock time delay (typically 30–60 seconds); (3) verify that door B unlocks and can be opened manually; (4) repeat the sequence in reverse (open door B first, verify door A locks). If the pass box is equipped with a UV disinfection lamp, verify that the UV lamp activates automatically when both doors are closed and the interlock timer begins. Measure the pressure differential between the pass box interior and the ambient room using the differential pressure transmitter. With both doors closed and HVAC operating, the pressure differential should match the design specification (typically −10 Pa for negative pressure or +10 Pa for positive pressure). Document all interlock activation times, door lock/unlock sequences, and pressure differential readings in the commissioning record. Photograph the door interlock mechanism and pressure gauge display for archival purposes.

6. Commissioning Report Compilation and Equipment Handover Documentation

This section establishes the structure and content requirements for the final commissioning deliverable package, ensuring traceability of all test equipment and cross-referencing of calibration certificates.

Prerequisite: Test Equipment Serial Number Inventory and Calibration Certificate Verification

Before compiling the final commissioning report, create a comprehensive inventory of all test equipment used during commissioning activities. For each instrument (pressure gauge, anemometer, multimeter, torque wrench, etc.), record the manufacturer name, model number, serial number, and the date range during which the instrument was used. Obtain the ISO 17025 calibration certificate for each instrument and verify that the calibration date falls within the valid period (typically 12 months from calibration date) and that the calibration date is prior to or coincident with the commissioning test date. If any instrument's calibration certificate is expired or missing, the test results obtained using that instrument cannot be accepted and must be repeated using a properly calibrated instrument. Create a calibration certificate cross-reference table that maps each test result to the specific instrument serial number and calibration certificate reference number used to obtain that result.

Procedure: Commissioning Report Structure and Data Organization

Organize the commissioning report using the following structure: (1) Executive Summary (1 page maximum)—state the equipment model, serial number, installation location, commissioning dates, and overall pass/fail determination; (2) Commissioning Scope and Objectives (1 page)—list the specific procedures performed and the standards or specifications against which performance was validated; (3) System Description (2 pages)—provide technical specifications of the hood-fumigation-chambers unit, including chamber volume, airflow capacity, VHP system capacity, and control system architecture; (4) Commissioning Procedures and Results (10–15 pages)—for each procedure, document the test purpose, test method, as-found data, as-left data, acceptance criteria, pass/fail determination, and test equipment used (with serial number and calibration certificate reference); (5) Deviations and Resolutions (1–2 pages)—list any deviations from the planned commissioning procedure, the impact assessment, and the resolution or sign-off; (6) Calibration Certificates Appendix—include copies of all ISO 17025 calibration certificates for test equipment used; (7) Photographs Appendix—include photographs of sensor mounting, cable routing, interlock activation sequences, and final equipment configuration; (8) Conclusions and Recommendations (1 page)—state whether the equipment is ready for operational handover and identify any follow-up actions or maintenance recommendations.

The commissioning report must be signed by the commissioning engineer (printed signature and date), the facility technical representative (printed signature and date), and the equipment manufacturer's representative (printed signature and date). All three signatures confirm that the equipment has been installed and commissioned in accordance with the specified procedures and that the equipment is ready for operational handover. Deliver the report as a PDF file with bookmarks for each major section to enable rapid navigation. Store the archive in the facility's document management system and provide a backup copy to the equipment manufacturer for their records. The commissioning record must be retained for the operational lifetime of the equipment (typically 10–15 years) to support future maintenance, troubleshooting, and regulatory audits.

7. FAQ — Installation & Commissioning Guide

Q1: What is the immediate post-delivery inspection checklist before equipment installation begins?
Upon delivery, verify that the equipment serial number matches the purchase order, inspect the exterior for shipping damage (dents, cracks, or corrosion), and confirm that all accessories listed on the packing slip are present (mounting brackets, cable connectors, documentation). Open the equipment enclosure and visually inspect internal components for loose fasteners, cable damage, or component displacement; any visible damage must be documented and reported to the manufacturer before installation proceeds.

Q2: What civil works and site preparation must be completed before equipment delivery?
The installation site must have a reinforced concrete floor slab (minimum 25 MPa compressive strength), verified anchor embedment capability for M12 expansion anchors at 80 mm depth, dedicated electrical supply (380 V three-phase 50/60 Hz, 32 A circuit breaker, earth grounding <4 ohms), and compressed air supply certified to ISO 8573-1 Class 2 at 6 bar minimum. HVAC ductwork must be sized to accommodate the equipment exhaust airflow (typically 150–200 m³/h) without exceeding 5 m/s duct velocity per ASHRAE 62.1.

Q3: What differential pressure setpoints are typical for biosafety containment zones during VHP cycles?
During VHP introduction and dwell phases, the hood-fumigation-chambers chamber typically maintains negative pressure of −10 Pa relative to the surrounding room to prevent hydrogen peroxide vapor leakage into the facility. During aeration phase, pressure returns to neutral (0 Pa ±5 Pa) to allow safe door opening. These setpoints must be verified during commissioning using a calibrated differential pressure transmitter with ±0.05% full-scale accuracy.

Q4: How can airtightness be verified in the field without specialized leak detection equipment?
A preliminary airtightness check can be performed by pressurizing the chamber to 6 bar using compressed air, closing all isolation valves, and monitoring the pressure gauge for 15 minutes; pressure decay should not exceed 0.1 bar over this period per ASTM E779. However, this method does not identify specific leak locations; formal leak detection using helium mass spectrometry or sulfur hexafluoride tracer gas requires specialized equipment and should be performed by qualified personnel.

Q5: What communication protocol parameters must be verified for BMS integration of the hood-fumigation-chambers control system?
The control system typically communicates via Modbus RTU (serial RS-485) or Modbus TCP (Ethernet). Verify the slave address (typically 1–247), baud rate (typically 9600 or 19200 bps), data bits (8), stop bits (1), and parity (even or odd) against the manufacturer's communication specification. Test communication by reading a known register (e.g., equipment status register) from the BMS and confirming that the value matches the HMI display.

Q6: What spare parts and maintenance intervals are critical for long-term reliability of the hood-fumigation-chambers system?
Critical spare parts include HEPA filters (replacement interval typically 12–24 months depending on usage), differential pressure transmitter (calibration interval 12 months), hydrogen peroxide concentration sensor (replacement interval 24 months), and door seals (replacement interval 24–36 months). Maintain a spare parts inventory of at least one complete set of consumable items to minimize downtime during maintenance. Schedule preventive maintenance every 6 months to inspect seals, verify sensor calibration, and test interlock functionality.
