sterile-inspection-isolators Installation and Commissioning: Interlock Verification and Pressure Integrity Protocol

1. Executive Summary

This guide establishes the systematic installation and commissioning procedures for sterile-inspection-isolators in pharmaceutical and biosafety laboratory environments, with emphasis on interlock logic validation, differential pressure sensor calibration, and regulatory-compliant documentation. Three critical procedures must be executed in sequence: (1) interlock timing verification under normal and failure-mode conditions, with door-to-door and door-to-HVAC logic tested to confirm safe state entry within 2 seconds of power loss; (2) differential pressure transmitter zero-point and span calibration using traceable reference standards before system pressurization, with as-found and as-left data recorded per ISO 17025 format; (3) commissioning report compilation with cross-referenced calibration certificates for all test equipment, ensuring each instrument serial number links to a valid calibration certificate dated within 12 months of commissioning execution.

2. Interlock Logic Verification Under Normal and Fault-Mode Operating Conditions

This section validates door-to-door and door-to-HVAC interlock sequences to confirm safe state behavior during power loss, BMS communication failure, and sensor open-circuit faults.

Prerequisite: Confirming Electrical Power Supply Stability and Interlock Controller Firmware Version Before Testing Begins

Before interlock testing commences, verify that the facility electrical supply meets the sterile-inspection-isolators design specification: three-phase 380 V ±10%, 50 Hz ±2%, with uninterruptible power supply (UPS) backup capacity sufficient to maintain interlock controller operation for a minimum of 15 minutes during mains power loss. Confirm that the interlock controller firmware version matches the version documented in the manufacturer's design specification and FAT (Factory Acceptance Test) report; firmware version mismatch is a common cause of interlock timing drift and must be resolved before commissioning proceeds.

Procedure: Sequential Door Interlock Testing — Normal Operation, Simultaneous Open Prevention, and HVAC Coupling Verification

Execute the normal-sequence interlock test by requesting door A open via the control panel; record the time at which the pneumatic seal begins deflation, the time at which the door lock solenoid de-energizes, and the time at which the door physically opens. Verify that door B lock remains engaged throughout this sequence and that the lock re-engages within 1 second of door A closure. Immediately execute the reverse sequence (door B open request) and confirm symmetric timing. Next, attempt to open door B while door A remains open by pressing the door B open button; the control system must block this request and illuminate a "Door Interlock Active" indicator within 0.5 seconds. Record the blocking action timestamp and verify that door B lock solenoid remains energized. Finally, execute the HVAC coupling test: open door A and verify that the exhaust fan speed increases to high-speed setpoint (typically 80–100% motor speed) within 3 seconds; close door A and verify that the exhaust fan returns to normal speed (typically 40–60% motor speed) after a 5-second time delay to allow pressure stabilization.

All interlock timing measurements must fall within the ranges specified in the table above; any measurement exceeding the acceptance criterion requires investigation and correction before system pressurization. Execute the power-loss fault test by cutting mains power to the interlock controller while door A is in the open position; both door locks must enter the unlocked (de-energized) state within 1 second to permit emergency egress. Restore power and verify that the system returns to normal interlock operation without manual reset. Execute the BMS communication loss test by disconnecting the Modbus RTU cable from the interlock controller; the system must continue to operate under local control (manual door open/close buttons) without loss of interlock logic. Execute the sensor open-circuit test by disconnecting the door position sensor from door A; the control system must activate a "Sensor Fault" alarm within 2 seconds and prevent door A from opening until the sensor is reconnected and the fault is cleared.

3. Installation Qualification Protocol Execution and Regulatory Documentation Preparation

This section establishes the IQ (Installation Qualification) protocol structure, evidence collection requirements, and deviation management procedures required for FDA 21 CFR Part 211 and GMP Annex 1 compliance.

Prerequisite: Obtaining Manufacturer Design Specification, FAT Report, and Validation Master Plan Before IQ Protocol Initiation

The IQ protocol must reference three foundational documents before execution begins: the manufacturer's design specification (which defines equipment model, serial number, design pressure, design temperature, and material certifications), the FAT report (which documents factory-level performance testing and acceptance), and the validation master plan (which defines the scope of IQ, OQ, and PQ activities and acceptance criteria). Verify that the equipment delivered to the site matches the equipment described in the design specification by comparing model number, serial number, and nameplate data; any discrepancy must be documented as a deviation and resolved before IQ proceeds.

Procedure: IQ Item Verification and Objective Evidence Collection — Equipment Identification, Utilities, Materials, and Spare Parts

Execute the equipment identification IQ item by photographing the equipment nameplate (showing model, serial number, manufacturer name, year of manufacture, and design pressure/temperature ratings) and recording this data in the IQ protocol checklist. Execute the installation environment verification IQ item by measuring ambient temperature (acceptance: 15–25 °C), relative humidity (acceptance: 30–70%), and cleanroom classification if applicable (acceptance: ISO Class 7 or better per ISO 14644-1:2024 [ISO 14644-1:2024]). Execute the utilities verification IQ item by measuring electrical supply voltage (acceptance: 380 V ±10%), frequency (acceptance: 50 Hz ±2%), compressed air supply pressure (acceptance: 6.0 bar ±0.5 bar), and air quality per ISO 8573-1:2010 [ISO 8573-1:2010] Class 3 or better (oil content ≤1 mg/m³, particle count ≤1,000 particles/cm³ ≥0.5 µm). Execute the materials verification IQ item by collecting and photographing all material certifications (stainless steel mill certificates, elastomer certificates, HEPA filter certificates) and cross-referencing them to the design specification. Execute the spare parts verification IQ item by confirming that the manufacturer has provided a spare parts list and that critical items (pneumatic seals, door hinges, pressure transmitters) are available for purchase with documented lead times.

Acceptance: All IQ Items Documented with Objective Evidence and Deviations Resolved Per Formal Deviation Report

Each IQ item must be supported by at least one objective evidence document (photograph, test data, certificate, or email confirmation) linked to the specific IQ item in the protocol. Any IQ item that does not meet the acceptance criterion must be documented in a formal deviation report that includes: (1) description of the non-conformance, (2) impact assessment (does this prevent system commissioning or only delay it?), (3) corrective action (what will be done to resolve it?), (4) responsible party and target completion date, and (5) re-test data confirming closure. The IQ protocol must be signed by the commissioning engineer and the client technical representative before OQ (Operational Qualification) begins; this signature confirms that the installation environment and equipment condition are suitable for operational testing.

4. Differential Pressure Transmitter Calibration and Zero-Point Adjustment in the Installed Configuration

This section establishes the field calibration procedure for differential pressure transmitters installed on the sterile-inspection-isolators, with emphasis on pre-calibration stress checks and traceable reference standard requirements.

Prerequisite: Verifying Transmitter Mounting Torque, Cable Shield Grounding, and 30-Minute Warm-Up Period Before Calibration Begins

Before calibrating any differential pressure transmitter, power up the transmitter and allow it to stabilize for a minimum of 30 minutes at the operating temperature (typically 20–25 °C in the commissioning environment). Verify that the transmitter is mounted with correct torque on the process connection: for M14 × 1.5 process ports, torque to 25 Nm ±2 Nm using a calibrated click-type torque wrench; over-torquing or under-torquing the process connection introduces mounting stress that manifests as a zero offset and cannot be corrected by software trim alone. Verify that the transmitter cable shield is grounded to the control cabinet ground bus at a single point (not multiple points, which creates ground loops); improper grounding introduces noise that corrupts the calibration data. Verify that no visible damage exists on the transmitter body, process ports, or electrical connector; any damage requires transmitter replacement before calibration proceeds.

Procedure: Zero-Point Calibration Using Atmospheric Reference and Span Calibration Using Traceable Pressure Standard

Vent both the high-pressure and low-pressure ports of the transmitter to atmosphere (typically by connecting both ports to a common atmospheric reference manifold or by opening both vent valves if the transmitter is equipped with manual vents). Record the transmitter output reading (in milliamps, voltage, or digital display units as applicable); this is the "as-found zero" value. If the as-found zero reading deviates from 0.0 Pa by more than ±0.5 Pa, adjust the zero potentiometer (if the transmitter is analog) or execute the software zero trim function (if the transmitter is digital) until the reading stabilizes at 0.0 Pa ±0.1 Pa. Record the adjusted reading as the "as-left zero" value. Next, apply a known reference pressure using a calibrated reference pressure gauge (accuracy ±0.05% of full scale or better, with valid ISO 17025 [ISO 17025:2017] calibration certificate dated within 12 months); for a 0–100 Pa transmitter, apply 50 Pa and record the transmitter output. Calculate the error as (measured value − reference value) / full scale × 100%; if the error exceeds ±1% of full scale, adjust the span potentiometer or execute the software span trim function and re-measure. Record the as-left span value and the reference pressure gauge serial number and calibration certificate reference.

Acceptance: Transmitter Output ±1% of Full Scale Across 0–100% Range and Calibration Certificate Issued Per ISO 17025 Format

After calibration, execute a three-point verification test: apply 0 Pa (atmospheric), 50 Pa (50% full scale), and 100 Pa (100% full scale) using the reference pressure gauge and record the transmitter output at each point. All three measurements must fall within ±1% of full scale of the reference value; if any measurement exceeds this tolerance, the transmitter must be replaced and the calibration repeated. Issue a calibration certificate per ISO 17025 format that includes: transmitter model and serial number, calibration date, as-found data, as-left data, reference pressure gauge serial number and calibration certificate reference, next calibration due date (typically 12 months from calibration date), and signature of the calibration technician. Attach this certificate to the commissioning report and cross-reference it in the test equipment inventory table (see Section 5).

5. Commissioning Report Compilation and Test Equipment Traceability Documentation

This section establishes the structure and content requirements for the final commissioning report, with mandatory cross-referencing of all test equipment serial numbers to valid calibration certificates.

Prerequisite: Collecting All Test Equipment Calibration Certificates and Assigning Unique Serial Numbers to Each Instrument Before Report Compilation Begins

Before compiling the commissioning report, collect and organize all calibration certificates for every instrument used during commissioning: differential pressure gauges, multimeters, thermometers, humidity meters, particle counters, torque wrenches, and any other measurement device. Assign each instrument a unique identifier (e.g., "DP-Gauge-001," "Multimeter-002") and create a test equipment inventory table that lists instrument type, manufacturer, model, serial number, calibration certificate number, calibration date, and next calibration due date. Verify that every calibration certificate is dated within 12 months of the commissioning execution date; any certificate dated outside this window must be updated before the commissioning report is finalized. This inventory table becomes Appendix A of the commissioning report and serves as the traceability backbone for all test data presented in the report.

Procedure: Structuring the Commissioning Report with Executive Summary, Test Results, Deviations, and Appendices

Structure the commissioning report as follows: (1) Executive Summary (1 page maximum) stating the system description, commissioning scope, and overall pass/fail determination; (2) System Description (1–2 pages) with equipment model, serial number, design specifications, and installation location; (3) Commissioning Procedures and Results (5–10 pages) organized by test category (interlock verification, pressure decay, HVAC performance, electrical safety, etc.), with each test showing test purpose, test method, as-found data, as-left data, acceptance criteria, pass/fail determination, and test equipment used (with serial number reference to Appendix A); (4) Deviations and Resolutions (1–2 pages) listing all deviations encountered during commissioning, impact assessment, corrective action, and closure sign-off; (5) Photographs (1–2 pages) showing equipment installation, sensor mounting, electrical connections, and any corrective actions taken; (6) Conclusions and Recommendations (1 page) summarizing the commissioning outcome and any follow-up actions required; (7) Appendix A: Test Equipment Inventory (1 page) with serial numbers and calibration certificate references; (8) Appendix B: Calibration Certificates (5–10 pages) containing copies of all calibration certificates for test equipment used; (9) Appendix C: Deviation Reports (as needed) with formal deviation documentation for any non-conformances encountered.

The commissioning report must be signed by the commissioning engineer (confirming that all procedures were executed per protocol and all acceptance criteria were met) and by the client technical representative (confirming that the client accepts the system for operational use). The report must be issued with a version control identifier (e.g., "Rev 0," "Rev 1") and a date of issue. Deliver the report as a PDF with bookmarks per section for easy navigation, and also deliver native formats (Excel data logs, Word documents) for future reference and archiving.

6. FAQ — Installation and Commissioning Guide

Q1: What is the immediate post-delivery inspection checklist for sterile-inspection-isolators, and what acceptance criteria must be met before installation begins?

Upon delivery, inspect the equipment for visible damage (dents, cracks, corrosion) and verify that the model and serial number on the nameplate match the purchase order and design specification. Measure the equipment dimensions and verify that the installation space accommodates the equipment footprint plus 0.5 m clearance on all sides for maintenance access. Confirm that all accessories listed in the packing list (door seals, fasteners, calibration certificates, user manuals) are present and undamaged; any missing or damaged items must be documented as a delivery deviation and reported to the manufacturer within 24 hours.

Q2: What civil works and site preparation prerequisites must be completed before mechanical installation of the sterile-inspection-isolators begins?

The installation floor must be level to within ±5 mm over the equipment footprint and capable of supporting the equipment weight plus 50% dynamic load margin (typically 2,000–3,000 kg total). Electrical power supply must be verified at 380 V ±10%, 50 Hz ±2%, with a dedicated circuit breaker rated for the equipment current draw (typically 16–32 A). Compressed air supply must be verified at 6.0 bar ±0.5 bar with oil-free air quality per ISO 8573-1 Class 3 or better (oil content ≤1 mg/m³, particle count ≤1,000 particles/cm³ ≥0.5 µm). Exhaust ducting must be sized per ASHRAE standards and routed to a safe discharge location outside the facility; exhaust air must not recirculate into the facility air intake.

Q3: What are the standard differential pressure settings for biosafety containment zones within sterile-inspection-isolators, and how are these settings verified during commissioning?

Positive-pressure zones (operator work areas) are typically maintained at +10 to +25 Pa relative to the surrounding environment to prevent external contamination from entering the work zone. Negative-pressure zones (hazardous material handling areas) are typically maintained at −10 to −25 Pa relative to the surrounding environment to prevent internal contamination from escaping. Verify these settings using a calibrated differential pressure gauge (accuracy ±0.05% of full scale) connected to the pressure transmitter output; record the steady-state pressure reading after the system has operated for a minimum of 5 minutes to allow pressure stabilization.

Q4: What is a quick field-based airtightness verification procedure for sterile-inspection-isolators without specialized leak detection equipment?

Execute a pressure decay test by pressurizing the equipment chamber to the design pressure (typically 6 bar) using the compressed air supply, then isolating the chamber by closing the supply valve. Record the pressure reading at time zero and again at 15 minutes; the pressure decay must not exceed 0.1 bar over this 15-minute period per ASTM E779 [ASTM E779:2019] method. If the pressure decay exceeds this threshold, execute a visual soap bubble test on all seams, joints, and door seals to identify the leak location; any visible bubbles indicate a leak that must be sealed before system commissioning proceeds.

Q5: What are the BMS (Building Management System) integration requirements for sterile-inspection-isolators, and what communication protocol parameters must be verified during commissioning?

The sterile-inspection-isolators control system typically communicates with the facility BMS via Modbus RTU protocol over an RS-485 serial connection. Verify the following parameters during commissioning: Modbus slave address (typically 1–247, as specified in the equipment manual), baud rate (typically 9,600 or 19,200 bits/second), data bits (8), stop bits (1), parity (even or odd, as specified), and response timeout (typically 1–2 seconds). Test the communication by reading a known register (e.g., system status register) from the BMS and verifying that the value matches the equipment display; if communication fails, verify cable continuity, termination resistors, and slave address configuration.

Q6: What spare parts should be maintained on-site for sterile-inspection-isolators, and what is the typical mean time to repair (MTTR) for critical components?

Critical spare parts include pneumatic door seals (compression set ≤25% per ASTM D395 [ASTM D395:2018] after 70 hours at 70 °C), door hinges (stainless steel, rated for 100,000 open/close cycles), differential pressure transmitters (0–100 Pa range, ±1% accuracy), and HEPA filters (H13 or H14 per ISO 11135 [ISO 11135:2014], replacement interval 12 months or when pressure drop exceeds 250 Pa). Typical MTTR for seal replacement is 2–4 hours; for transmitter replacement, 1–2 hours; for HEPA filter replacement, 1 hour. Maintain at least one spare of each critical component on-site to minimize downtime during maintenance.
