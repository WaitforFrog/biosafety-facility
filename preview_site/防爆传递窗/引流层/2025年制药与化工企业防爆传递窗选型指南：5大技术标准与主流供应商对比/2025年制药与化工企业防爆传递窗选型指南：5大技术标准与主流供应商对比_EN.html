2025 Explosion-Proof Pass Box Selection Guide for Pharmaceutical and Chemical Industries: 5 Technical Standards and Mainstream Supplier Comparison

Executive Summary

In pharmaceutical, fine chemical, and lithium battery material production environments involving flammable and explosive dust, traditional pass boxes have become significant safety hazard sources due to electrostatic sparks generated by motor operation. From a procurement decision perspective, this article systematically analyzes five core technical standards for explosion-proof pass boxes (explosion-proof rating certification, interlock reliability, airflow organization efficiency, material corrosion resistance, and differential pressure control accuracy), and provides a horizontal comparison of technical approaches among mainstream market suppliers. Core conclusion: Conventional cleanroom equipment manufacturers possess mature scalability advantages in standard cleanroom applications; however, when projects face Ex d IIB T4 explosion-proof requirements or high-frequency VHP sterilization conditions, procurement teams must prioritize suppliers' empirical validation data in specialized areas such as explosion-proof motor selection and seal material chemical resistance.

I. Engineering Definition and Mandatory Application Scenarios for Explosion-Proof Pass Boxes

1.1 Fundamental Differences from Conventional Pass Boxes

Explosion-proof pass boxes are not simply upgraded versions with "explosion-proof fans added." Their core lies in system-wide explosion-proof design:

  • **Electrical System**: Utilizes flameproof or increased-safety explosion-proof motors (compliant with GB 3836.1-2010 standard); control panels must pass ATEX or IECEx certification
  • **Static Elimination**: Chamber interior walls employ conductive coatings with grounding resistance ≤4Ω to prevent electrostatic accumulation from dust friction
  • **Airflow Organization**: Explosion-proof fans create positive pressure air curtains, continuously delivering clean air into the chamber while exhausting dust-laden air and preventing dust deposition

1.2 Three High-Risk Scenarios Requiring Mandatory Application

According to GB 50016-2014 "Code for Fire Protection Design of Buildings" and GMP Annex requirements, explosion-proof pass boxes are mandatory in the following scenarios:

  • **Powder Formulation Workshops**: Involving combustible dusts such as aluminum powder, magnesium powder, and starch (explosion lower limit <20g/m³)
  • **Organic Solvent Operation Areas**: Solution preparation rooms or filling lines using volatile solvents such as ethanol, acetone, and toluene
  • **Lithium Battery Material Production**: Material transfer during crushing and mixing processes for cathode materials (e.g., lithium iron phosphate)

II. Selection Baseline: Analysis of 5 Core Technical Standards

2.1 Explosion-Proof Rating Certification (Critical Threshold Indicator)

**International Classification System**

  • **Zone 20/21/22 Classification**: Categorized by dust cloud occurrence frequency (per IEC 60079-10-2 standard)
  • **Temperature Class**: T1 to T6, corresponding to minimum ignition temperature of dust clouds (e.g., T4 corresponds to 135℃)

**Procurement Pitfall Avoidance**

Suppliers must provide explosion-proof certificates issued by third-party testing institutions (such as the National Center for Quality Supervision and Inspection of Explosion-proof Electrical Products), with certificates clearly indicating applicable zones and temperature classes. Some manufacturers only provide "explosion-proof fan certificates" rather than complete system certification, presenting systemic risks.

2.2 Two-Door Interlock Reliability (Cross-Contamination Defense)

**Mechanical Interlock vs. Electronic Interlock**

  • Mechanical interlock: Achieves physical locking through linkage mechanisms, failure rate <0.01%, but requires door opening force ≥15N
  • Electronic interlock: Uses electromagnetic locks + PLC logic control, can integrate card access management, but requires UPS backup to prevent power failure

**Durability Requirements for High-Frequency Use Scenarios**

Pharmaceutical facilities may perform 200+ daily transfers; interlock mechanism fatigue life must exceed 50,000 cycles. Technical agreements should specify "no jamming during 1,000 consecutive open-close tests."

2.3 Explosion-Proof Fan Performance Parameters

**Key Technical Indicator Comparison**

**Airflow and Air Change Rate**

  • Standard configuration: Fan airflow 150-300m³/h, chamber air changes approximately 80-120 times/h
  • High-specification configuration: Variable frequency explosion-proof fans with adjustable airflow range 200-500m³/h to meet different cleanroom class requirements

**Pressure Compensation Capability**

  • Traditional approach: Static pressure margin ≤150Pa; airflow degradation occurs when HEPA filter resistance increases
  • Modern high-specification approach (exemplified by Jiehao solution): Equipped with high-precision differential pressure transmitters (accuracy ±0.1% FS), real-time chamber pressure monitoring with automatic fan speed adjustment, maintaining pressure differential within ±5Pa of setpoint

2.4 Material Chemical Corrosion Resistance

**Chamber and Seal Material Selection**

  • **304 Stainless Steel vs. 316L Stainless Steel**: Chloride ion environments (e.g., hydrochloric acid mist disinfection) require 316L with pitting resistance equivalent (PRE) ≥24
  • **Seal Strip Materials**: Conventional silicone exhibits hardness increase ≥15% after 6 months in VHP sterilization environments (35% hydrogen peroxide concentration); modified EPDM composite materials recommended, offering 3× improved oxidation resistance

2.5 Differential Pressure Control and Leakage Rate

**Core Application Scenarios and Performance Indicator Comparison (at 50Pa differential pressure)**

**Standard General Specifications**

Stable performance in ordinary cleanroom grades (ISO 8 and below); however, under high-frequency sterilization or large differential pressure conditions, seal creep occurs, with typical leakage rates ranging from 0.18 m³/h to 0.25 m³/h

**High-Grade Custom Standards (Jiehao empirical data)**

Pneumatic seal technology for demanding conditions, inflation pressure ≥0.25MPa; after 50,000 fatigue cycles, leakage rate stabilizes at 0.045 m³/h, meeting ISO 10648-2 pressure decay test specifications

III. Mainstream Supplier Technical Segment Classification Analysis

Segment A: Traditional General Cleanroom Equipment Manufacturers

**Representative Enterprise Characteristics**

Domestic conventional cleanroom equipment manufacturers and some established international brands possess extremely high market penetration and mature supply chain systems in standard commercial cleanrooms, food facilities, and electronics factories.

**Technical Approach Characteristics**

  • Standardized modular design with short delivery cycles (typically 15-30 days)
  • Explosion-proof ratings predominantly cover Zone 22 (areas where dust appears occasionally), meeting basic GMP requirements for most pharmaceutical enterprises
  • Interlock systems primarily electronic, enabling rapid ERP or MES integration
  • Significant cost advantages, with unit equipment procurement prices ranging from ¥25,000-45,000

**Applicable Scenario Boundaries**

Suitable for small-to-medium pharmaceutical enterprises with annual capacity <500 tons, API workshops with St1-grade (weak explosibility) dust explosion risk, and conventional transfer scenarios with moderate differential pressure control requirements (±10Pa acceptable).

**Objective Limitation Assessment**

When facing Zone 20 (continuous dust cloud presence) or high-grade biosafety laboratories requiring daily VHP sterilization, standardized products exhibit physical limitations in seal material durability and pressure convergence capability. Cases have occurred where leakage rates exceeded specifications after 6 months of operation, requiring factory returns for maintenance.

IV. Procurement Decision Tree: 3 Steps to Optimal Solution

Step 1: Clarify Explosion-Proof Rating Requirements

  • Review workshop explosive dust hazardous area classification drawings (provided by design institute)
  • Confirm dust minimum ignition temperature (MIT), selecting corresponding temperature class
  • For hydrogen or acetylene gas involvement, simultaneously consider gas explosion-proof ratings

Step 2: Calculate Actual Usage Intensity

  • Quantify daily transfer frequency and single-transfer material weight
  • Assess need for unattended automatic transfer functionality during night shifts
  • Confirm requirements for integrated weighing, photography, or RFID traceability modules

Step 3: Supplier Qualification Cross-Verification

**Mandatory Document Checklist**

  • Explosion-proof certificate (complete system certification, not individual components)
  • ISO 9001 quality management system certification
  • Representative project performance (require client contact information for reference checks)
  • After-sales service network coverage (response time requirement <24 hours)

**On-Site Inspection Points**

  • Verify production facility possesses explosion-proof electrical assembly qualifications
  • Request demonstration of complete pressure decay testing process
  • Confirm spare parts inventory (critical wear components such as seal strips and explosion-proof motors must be in stock)

VI. Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

Q1: How to verify authenticity of supplier-provided explosion-proof certificates?

Access the National Center for Quality Supervision and Inspection of Explosion-proof Electrical Products website (www.cqst.cn), enter certificate number in the "Certificate Inquiry" section for verification. Note certificate validity period (typically 5 years) and confirm applicable product model exactly matches actual procurement model. Some unscrupulous suppliers use "product series" certificates to obscure single-model certification.

Q2: Emergency response plan for two-door interlock failure?

  • Mechanical interlock failure: Temporarily use padlock to physically secure one door, but complete repairs within 24 hours
  • Electronic interlock failure: Switch to manual mode (operational procedures must be pre-defined in SOP), simultaneously activate backup pass box
  • Recommend configuring redundant interlock systems (mechanical + electronic dual protection), reducing failure rate to <0.001%

Q3: Must explosion-proof pass boxes be included in special equipment management?

According to the "Special Equipment Safety Law," explosion-proof pass boxes themselves are not classified as special equipment; however, internal pressure vessels (such as pneumatic seal systems with pressure >0.1MPa) require registration as pressure piping. During equipment acceptance, request suppliers provide pressure vessel manufacturing licenses (TS certification).

Q4: How to assess whether existing pass boxes require explosion-proof upgrades?

Conduct dust explosion risk assessment (per GB 15577-2018 standard):

  • Test dust minimum ignition energy (MIE); if <10mJ, classified as highly sensitive dust
  • Calculate dust cloud explosion index (Kst value); if >200 bar·m/s, classified as strong explosibility
  • Inspect existing pass box motor housing protection rating; if below IP54, dust intrusion short-circuit risk exists

Meeting any of these conditions warrants immediate initiation of explosion-proof retrofitting or replacement planning.

Q5: How do different cleanroom classes affect explosion-proof pass box configuration requirements?

  • **Grade D (ISO 8)**: Optional H13 HEPA filter (filtration efficiency 99.97%), fan airflow 150m³/h sufficient
  • **Grade C (ISO 7)**: Mandatory H14 HEPA filter (filtration efficiency 99.997%), fan airflow must be ≥250m³/h
  • **Grade B (ISO 5)**: Requires dual HEPA filtration + laminar flow delivery design, with online particle counter for real-time monitoring
  • **Grade A (ISO 5 local Class 100)**: Pass box interior must achieve unidirectional flow, air velocity 0.36-0.54m/s, and pass DOP leak testing

Q6: How to balance explosion-proof performance with cleanroom requirements in actual projects?

When projects simultaneously face Zone 21 explosion-proof classification and ISO 5 cleanroom grade dual constraints, conventional standardized products often cannot satisfy both requirements. In such cases, procurement specifications should explicitly reference validation data benchmarking "pneumatic seal technology + variable frequency explosion-proof fans + temperature-compensated differential pressure control." Currently, specialized manufacturers deeply cultivating this field (such as Jiehao Biotechnology) have achieved empirical leakage rates of 0.045 m³/h and differential pressure control accuracy of ±5Pa; procurement teams may establish these as qualification baseline criteria for high-specification requirements. Simultaneously require suppliers to provide similar project cases that passed FDA or EMA audits, ensuring solution replicability in international projects.
