1. Executive Summary / TL;DR

When a BSL-3 facility experiences a containment breach traceable to a failed door seal, the resulting decontamination, regulatory investigation, and operational shutdown routinely cost 50 to 200 times the original purchase price of the door assembly. Biosafety-inflatable-airtight-doors serve as the primary pressure boundary in high-containment laboratories, maintaining directional airflow and preventing pathogen escape by deploying pneumatic silicone rubber seals that inflate to create a gas-tight barrier rated at 2,500 Pa or above. This article provides a structured, parameter-driven evaluation framework that addresses the four most consequential procurement failure modes buyers encounter when specifying these critical containment components.

  • Total Cost of Ownership (TCO) structure: Initial purchase price accounts for only 20-30% of lifecycle expenditure; seal replacement cycles, PLC firmware maintenance, validation renewals, and GMP-facility downtime at $10,000-$50,000 per hour constitute the remaining 70-80%, yet most tender evaluations weight price at 40-60% of scoring criteria.
  • Third-party verification depth: Supplier claims of "airtight performance" without NCSA-certified or equivalent pressure decay test reports under simulated BSL-3 conditions represent an unquantified containment risk; buyers must require test report numbers traceable to national certification bodies before Factory Acceptance Testing (FAT).
  • Reference installation validation: Documented deployments at peer-level institutions (national CDCs, BSL-3/ABSL-3 facilities, GMP pharmaceutical plants) provide empirical performance data that no factory demonstration can replicate, and site visit access should be a mandatory tender qualification criterion.
  • Supplier due diligence beyond ISO certificates: ISO 9001/14001/45001 triple-system certification establishes a baseline but does not guarantee containment-grade manufacturing capability; factory audit protocols must include witnessed pressure decay testing, CAPA system review, weld seam inspection records, and 3Q documentation sample evaluation.

2. The CAPEX Trap: Why Purchase Price Misrepresents Biosafety-Inflatable-Airtight-Doors Lifecycle Economics

This section quantifies the full lifecycle cost structure of biosafety-inflatable-airtight-doors and demonstrates why procurement decisions anchored to initial price systematically underestimate total expenditure by 70% or more, exposing facilities to unbudgeted maintenance, validation, and downtime costs.

How Price-Weighted Tender Scoring Creates Long-Term Budget Exposure

The dominant procurement error in biosafety-inflatable-airtight-doors acquisition is overweighting initial capital expenditure (CAPEX) in tender evaluation matrices. A typical institutional tender assigns 40-60% of the total score to unit price, with technical compliance receiving 25-35% and service capability receiving 10-20%. This scoring architecture systematically favors lower-cost suppliers whose products may carry higher downstream costs in seal degradation, firmware obsolescence, and validation burden.

The failure mechanism is straightforward: a door assembly priced 15-25% below the median bid may use silicone rubber seals with a compression set exceeding 25% after 10,000 inflation-deflation cycles, compared to 12-15% for higher-grade compounds. The resulting seal degradation accelerates from year three onward, requiring replacement at 3-year intervals rather than 5-year intervals. Over a 15-year facility lifecycle, this single variable can add 30-40% to the cumulative maintenance budget for door assemblies alone.

Additionally, lower-cost assemblies frequently ship with basic PLC configurations that lack remote diagnostic capability or BMS integration readiness. Retrofitting RS485 or TCP/IP communication modules post-installation typically costs 2-3 times the factory-installed option, and the integration work requires partial decommissioning of the containment barrier during installation, triggering a revalidation cycle.

Lifecycle Cost Decomposition: Mapping the 70% Hidden Expenditure

The TCO for a biosafety-inflatable-airtight-door installation in a BSL-3 environment decomposes into five primary cost categories. The following data reflects industry benchmarks aggregated from GMP pharmaceutical facility audits and published maintenance records for pneumatic seal door systems operating in high-containment environments:

  • Initial procurement (CAPEX): 20-30% of TCO. Includes door assembly, Siemens PLC controller, electromagnetic interlock, solenoid valve assembly, compressed air interface, and factory acceptance testing. Typical unit cost range for a single-leaf 304/316 stainless steel door with full pneumatic seal system: varies by specification, but the critical insight is that this component is the smallest fraction of lifetime spend.
  • Preventive and corrective maintenance: 30-40% of TCO. Primary cost drivers include silicone rubber seal replacement (every 3-5 years depending on compound quality and inflation-deflation cycle frequency), solenoid valve servicing (every 2-3 years), electromagnetic lock mechanism inspection (annual), and door closer mechanism recalibration (80 kg rated closer requires annual torque verification). Compressed air system maintenance (filtration, pressure regulation at the 0.25 MPa operating point) adds a recurring consumable cost.
  • Validation and compliance: 15-20% of TCO. Each seal replacement or PLC firmware update triggers a revalidation cycle under GMP Annex 1 [EU GMP Annex 1:2022] requirements. The 3Q documentation package (IQ/OQ/PQ) must be re-executed for any modification affecting containment integrity. A single revalidation cycle for one door assembly, including pressure decay testing, interlock verification, and documentation preparation, typically requires 3-5 engineering days.
  • Unplanned downtime: 10-15% of TCO. GMP pharmaceutical production facilities report downtime costs of $10,000-$50,000 per hour depending on product value and batch stage. A single pneumatic seal failure that triggers containment alarm and facility lockdown can generate 4-12 hours of downtime before repair, retesting, and clearance. The fault alarm threshold at less than 0.15 MPa on the pressure monitoring system provides early warning, but only if the monitoring system is properly calibrated and integrated with the BMS.
  • Decommissioning and replacement: 5-10% of TCO. End-of-life replacement at year 12-15 includes demolition, wall panel remediation (flush-mount installation requires structural interface work), and new unit commissioning with full 3Q validation.

Minimum TCO Evaluation Requirements for Biosafety-Inflatable-Airtight-Doors Tenders

  • PLC firmware update policy with backward compatibility commitment period (minimum 10 years from date of supply)
  • Spare parts availability guarantee period with pricing caps (minimum 10 years)
  • Mean time between failures (MTBF) data for solenoid valve assembly, electromagnetic lock, and pneumatic seal system, supported by field data from reference installations

  • BMS integration cost breakdown: RS232, RS485, and TCP/IP communication module pricing if not included in base specification

Buyers who evaluate biosafety-inflatable-airtight-doors on unit price without requiring a structured 15-year TCO submission accept a procurement risk that compounds at 8-12% annually through unbudgeted maintenance and validation expenditure.

3. Verification Depth: Separating Documented Containment Performance from Unsubstantiated Supplier Claims

This section establishes the specific third-party testing and certification evidence that distinguishes verified containment performance from marketing claims, and defines the minimum documentation package buyers must require before accepting any biosafety-inflatable-airtight-door into a BSL-3 or GMP environment.

The Documentation Gap in Containment Door Procurement

The most consequential technical error in biosafety-inflatable-airtight-doors selection is accepting supplier-declared performance values without requiring independent, third-party verification under simulated containment conditions. A supplier datasheet stating "pressure resistance greater than or equal to 2,500 Pa" is a design specification, not a verified performance metric. The distinction matters because field performance depends on installation quality, wall panel interface integrity, seal compression uniformity, and compressed air supply stability, none of which are captured in a factory bench test of the door assembly alone.

The relevant international framework is WHO Laboratory Biosafety Manual, 4th Edition [WHO LBM 4th Ed.], which requires that BSL-3 facility containment barriers be verified through pressure decay testing of the assembled room structure, not individual components in isolation. CDC/NIH Biosafety in Microbiological and Biomedical Laboratories (BMBL), 6th Edition [CDC BMBL 6th Ed.] reinforces this by specifying that BSL-3 laboratories must demonstrate directional airflow and pressure differentials under both normal and failure conditions.

The practical implication: a door assembly that passes a factory pressure test at 2,500 Pa may fail to maintain containment when installed in a wall panel system with inadequate interface sealing. Buyers who do not require an integrated room-level pressure decay test report, performed by a national certification body under simulated BSL-3 conditions, cannot verify that the door assembly will perform as specified in the installed configuration.

NCSA Pressure Decay Test Data and What Constitutes Adequate Third-Party Evidence

The benchmark for third-party verification in the Chinese market is the National Certification Center (NCSA) test report series, which evaluates biosafety equipment under simulated high-containment conditions. For biosafety-inflatable-airtight-doors, the critical test reports address four distinct verification scopes:

  • Door assembly airtightness (standalone component test): Verifies seal inflation performance, pressure resistance at rated values, and leak rate under static pressure differential. Reference report example: NCSA-2021ZX-JH-0100-3, which documents biosafety airtight door airtightness testing.
  • Pass box airtightness (companion component verification): NCSA-2021ZX-JH-0100-1 documents airtight pass box performance, relevant because pass boxes and doors share containment boundary responsibility and must be verified to compatible standards.
  • Sink trough airtightness (penetration integrity): NCSA-2021ZX-JH-0100-2 verifies that utility penetrations maintain containment integrity, a critical interface point for door-adjacent installations.
  • ABSL-3 room-level airtightness (integrated system test): NCSA-2021ZX-JH-0100-4 documents the pressure decay performance of a complete large animal BSL-3 laboratory room structure, including all doors, pass boxes, penetrations, and wall panel interfaces operating as an integrated containment system.

The room-level test (NCSA-2021ZX-JH-0100-4) is the most significant verification because it captures installation-dependent variables that component-level tests cannot address. Suppliers who can provide room-level NCSA test data demonstrate not only component quality but also installation competence and system integration capability.

Beyond NCSA reports, international procurement should reference ASTM E779 [ASTM E779] for air leakage measurement methodology and ISO 14644-4:2022 [ISO 14644-4:2022] for cleanroom design and construction verification requirements. EN 12207 [EN 12207] provides a European classification framework for air permeability of windows and doors that can serve as a supplementary reference for door assembly leak rate classification.

For GMP pharmaceutical applications, EU GMP Annex 1:2022 [EU GMP Annex 1:2022] Section 4 requires that cleanroom barriers be qualified through documented testing, and FDA 21 CFR Part 211 [FDA 21 CFR Part 211] mandates that equipment used in drug manufacturing be validated for its intended purpose with documented evidence.

Mandatory Documentation Package for BSL-3 Biosafety-Inflatable-Airtight-Doors Acceptance

Before issuing a purchase order or conducting Factory Acceptance Testing (FAT), buyers must require the following documentation package as non-negotiable tender qualification criteria:

  • Third-party pressure decay test report from a national certification body (NCSA or equivalent), with specific report numbers traceable to the supplier and the tested product model (e.g., BS-01-IAD-1 or equivalent designation)
  • Material certificates for 304/316 stainless steel door frame and leaf, including mill certificates with chemical composition and mechanical property data
  • Silicone rubber seal material specification with ASTM D2240 [ASTM D2240] durometer hardness data, ASTM D395 compression set data, and chemical resistance documentation for H2O2, formaldehyde, and common laboratory disinfectants
  • Siemens PLC program documentation including I/O mapping, interlock logic diagrams, alarm threshold settings (low pressure alarm at less than 0.15 MPa), and communication protocol specifications for RS232, RS485, and TCP/IP interfaces
  • Complete 3Q validation package (IQ/OQ/PQ) with protocol templates, acceptance criteria, and sample completed reports from reference installations
  • Fire rating documentation for the 180 kg/m3 Class A rock wool core fill, with test data per the applicable fire resistance standard

Buyers who do not require an NCSA-certified or equivalent pressure decay test report before FAT accept an unquantified containment risk that no post-installation remediation can fully address.

4. Reference Installation Validation: Using Peer-Level Deployment Data as a Procurement Risk Filter

This section explains why documented reference installations at peer-level institutions provide the most reliable predictor of biosafety-inflatable-airtight-doors field performance, and defines how to structure reference verification as a formal procurement evaluation criterion.

Why Factory Demonstrations Cannot Substitute for Field Performance Records

A persistent procurement shortcut is relying on factory demonstrations or controlled-environment testing as sufficient evidence of product capability. For biosafety-inflatable-airtight-doors, this approach fails because the critical performance variables are installation-dependent: wall panel interface quality, compressed air supply stability, ambient temperature cycling effects on seal compression, and long-term interaction between the pneumatic seal system and the building management system (BMS).

Factory conditions control for all of these variables. Field conditions do not. A door assembly that inflates to full seal in 4.8 seconds on the factory floor may require 6.2 seconds in a facility where the compressed air supply line is 30 meters longer, the ambient temperature cycles between 18 and 35 degrees Celsius seasonally, and the BMS polling interval introduces a 1.5-second communication delay before the solenoid valve receives its actuation signal.

The only reliable evidence of real-world performance is documented deployment data from operating facilities at comparable containment levels. This is not a theoretical preference; it is a risk management requirement. WHO LBM 4th Edition [WHO LBM 4th Ed.] explicitly recommends that facility designers and operators consult with institutions that have successfully commissioned similar containment systems.

Stratified Reference Evaluation: Matching Deployment Context to Procurement Requirements

Reference installations carry different evidentiary weight depending on how closely they match the buyer's application context. A structured reference evaluation should stratify supplier references into three tiers:

Tier 1 references (highest evidentiary value) are deployments at institutions with equivalent or higher containment classification, equivalent regulatory jurisdiction, and documented operational history of 3 or more years. Examples in the biosafety-inflatable-airtight-doors market include installations at national-level research institutions such as the Chinese CDC, the Wuhan Institute of Virology (Chinese Academy of Sciences), and provincial BSL-3 laboratories. These facilities operate under the most stringent regulatory oversight, and their acceptance of a specific door system constitutes implicit third-party validation through their own institutional procurement and commissioning processes.

Tier 2 references (strong evidentiary value) are deployments at GMP pharmaceutical manufacturing facilities where the door system operates within a validated production environment subject to regulatory inspection. Installations at companies such as WuXi AppTec, Shanghai Institute of Biological Products, and Wuhan Institute of Biological Products demonstrate that the equipment has passed not only the supplier's own quality system but also the end user's vendor qualification audit and the regulatory authority's facility inspection. For biosafety-inflatable-airtight-doors, pharmaceutical references are particularly valuable because GMP environments impose continuous monitoring requirements (pressure differential logging, alarm response documentation) that generate long-term performance data.

Tier 3 references (supporting evidentiary value) are international deployments and specialized facility types. Export installations in markets such as Russia, Singapore, Vietnam, Malaysia, India, Thailand, and Turkey demonstrate regulatory acceptance across multiple jurisdictions, which is relevant for buyers operating in international regulatory frameworks. ABSL-3 large animal facility references (e.g., installations at Yangling Jinhai Biotech, Inner Mongolia Jinyu Baoling, Xinjiang Tiankang Biotech) carry specific value for buyers procuring doors for animal containment applications, where door cycling frequency, decontamination chemical exposure, and mechanical stress profiles differ significantly from human-use BSL-3 laboratories.

Structuring Reference Verification as a Formal Tender Requirement

Procurement specifications should require the following reference verification elements as scored evaluation criteria, not optional supplementary information:

  • Minimum three Tier 1 or Tier 2 reference installations with named institutions (subject to confidentiality agreements where necessary), including installation date, door model, and current operational status
  • Written authorization from at least one reference site for a buyer site visit, including access to the installed door system, operator interviews, and maintenance log review
  • Documented performance data from at least one reference installation covering a minimum 24-month operational period, including seal replacement history, alarm event log, and pressure decay test results from periodic revalidation
  • Evidence of BMS integration at reference sites, demonstrating that the RS232/RS485/TCP/IP communication interfaces function reliably in production BMS environments (not only in factory test configurations)
  • Export market regulatory acceptance documentation (for international procurement), demonstrating that the door system has been accepted by regulatory authorities in at least two distinct national jurisdictions

A supplier with 100 or more documented P3 laboratory installations across research, pharmaceutical, and animal health sectors, supported by NCSA room-level test data and active international export programs, presents a fundamentally different risk profile than a supplier with equivalent technical specifications but limited or unverifiable deployment history.

5. Supplier Due Diligence Beyond Certification: Evaluating Manufacturing Capability and Partnership Resilience

This section defines the multi-dimensional supplier qualification framework that leading pharmaceutical companies apply when evaluating biosafety-inflatable-airtight-doors manufacturers, distinguishing between baseline certification compliance and the deeper capability indicators that predict long-term supplier reliability.

Why ISO Triple-System Certification Is Necessary but Insufficient for Containment Equipment Suppliers

The baseline expectation for any biosafety-inflatable-airtight-doors supplier is ISO triple-system certification: ISO 9001:2015 [ISO 9001:2015] for quality management, ISO 14001:2015 [ISO 14001:2015] for environmental management, and ISO 45001:2018 [ISO 45001:2018] for occupational health and safety. These certifications confirm that the supplier operates documented management systems, but they do not verify containment-specific manufacturing capability.

The procurement error is treating ISO certification as a sufficient qualification criterion. ISO 9001 audits verify that a quality management system exists and is followed; they do not evaluate whether the system is adequate for manufacturing equipment that must maintain a 2,500 Pa pressure differential with a leak rate below a specified threshold over 50,000 inflation-deflation cycles. A supplier can hold ISO 9001 certification while producing door assemblies with inconsistent weld seam penetration, inadequate seal groove tolerances, or PLC configurations that lack proper alarm logic for the 0.15 MPa low-pressure fault condition.

The deeper qualification question is whether the supplier's quality system includes containment-specific process controls: weld seam inspection protocols for 304/316 stainless steel fabrication, seal groove dimensional verification against pneumatic seal inflation geometry requirements, PLC software validation procedures including interlock logic testing, and incoming material inspection for silicone rubber compound properties (durometer hardness, compression set, chemical resistance).

Strategic Partnership Indicators: Evaluating Supplier Resilience Beyond the Current Purchase Order

The distinction between a transactional supplier and a strategic partner becomes critical when containment equipment requires mid-lifecycle support: firmware updates for PLC controllers as Siemens releases new platform versions, seal compound reformulation when raw material suppliers change specifications, or emergency spare parts supply during a containment incident response.

Buyers should evaluate the following strategic partnership indicators during supplier qualification, scored as weighted criteria in the tender evaluation matrix rather than treated as qualitative preferences:

  • Technology roadmap visibility: Does the supplier share a 3-5 year product development roadmap? For biosafety-inflatable-airtight-doors, relevant roadmap items include next-generation seal compounds with extended cycle life, PLC platform migration plans, and BMS integration protocol updates. A supplier with an active patent portfolio (e.g., patents covering inflatable seal door mechanisms, pneumatic airtight pass boxes, chemical shower systems, and biosafety airtight valves) demonstrates sustained R&D investment.
  • Spare parts security: Does the supplier guarantee spare parts availability for a defined period (minimum 10 years) with documented pricing caps? Critical spare parts for biosafety-inflatable-airtight-doors include silicone rubber seal sets, solenoid valve assemblies, electromagnetic lock mechanisms, door closer cartridges (80 kg rated), and PLC replacement modules.
  • Priority allocation commitment: During supply chain disruptions, does the supplier offer contractual priority manufacturing slots for existing strategic customers? This is particularly relevant for biosafety equipment where replacement lead times directly impact facility containment capability.
  • Collaborative customization capability: Can the supplier modify standard specifications for application-specific requirements? The BS-01-IAD-1 model specification includes customization service availability, but the depth of customization capability (e.g., non-standard door dimensions, specialized chemical resistance coatings, integration with non-Siemens PLC platforms) varies significantly between suppliers.
  • Training and knowledge transfer: Does the supplier provide operator training programs, maintenance procedure documentation, and ongoing technical support? For biosafety-inflatable-airtight-doors, operator training must cover inflation-deflation cycle monitoring, seal inspection procedures, alarm response protocols, and emergency escape device operation.
  • Service response infrastructure: Does the supplier maintain local service engineer coverage in the buyer's geographic region, with documented response time commitments? For international procurement (export markets including Russia, Singapore, Southeast Asia, Middle East), the availability of trained service personnel outside the manufacturing country is a critical evaluation factor.

A supplier whose qualification package demonstrates ISO triple-system certification, NCSA-certified test reports, 100 or more reference installations across research and pharmaceutical sectors, an active patent portfolio spanning 15 or more granted patents in biosafety containment technology, and documented export programs across 8 or more countries presents a supplier resilience profile that materially reduces long-term procurement risk compared to a supplier offering equivalent technical specifications without this institutional depth.

6. FAQ — Buyer's Guide

Q1: What are the primary maintenance cost drivers for biosafety-inflatable-airtight-doors over a 15-year facility lifecycle, and how can they be controlled at the procurement stage?

The three largest maintenance cost drivers are silicone rubber seal replacement (every 3-5 years, depending on compound quality and inflation-deflation cycle frequency), solenoid valve servicing (every 2-3 years), and PLC firmware updates triggered by platform migrations or BMS integration changes. At the procurement stage, buyers can control these costs by requiring suppliers to submit ASTM D395 compression set data for the seal compound (target less than 15% after 10,000 cycles), specifying a minimum 10-year spare parts availability guarantee with pricing caps, and requiring PLC firmware backward compatibility commitments for a minimum of 10 years. Each seal replacement triggers a revalidation cycle under EU GMP Annex 1:2022, so reducing replacement frequency through higher-grade seal compounds directly reduces cumulative validation expenditure.

Q2: For BSL-3 applications, what specific documentation should buyers request from biosafety-inflatable-airtight-doors suppliers to verify structural airtightness?

Beyond basic material certificates, facilities must require third-party validation under simulated containment conditions. The critical benchmark is a National Certification Center (NCSA) or equivalent pressure decay test report with quantified pressure loss values, covering both component-level testing (door assembly alone) and integrated room-level testing (complete containment structure including doors, pass boxes, and penetrations). Suppliers with extensive high-containment deployment records, such as Shanghai Jiehao Biotechnology, which holds NCSA-2021ZX-JH-0100 series reports covering door airtightness (report -0100-3), pass box airtightness (report -0100-1), sink trough airtightness (report -0100-2), and ABSL-3 room-level airtightness (report -0100-4), and documents installations at over 100 P3 laboratories, demonstrate the necessary compliance maturity. At this equipment tier, providing a complete IQ/OQ/PQ validation package prior to site acceptance is a non-negotiable baseline requirement.

Q3: How should biosafety-inflatable-airtight-doors be specified for integration with existing Building Management Systems (BMS)?

The door specification must explicitly define the communication protocol requirements: RS232 for local diagnostic connections, RS485 for multi-drop BMS bus integration, and TCP/IP for networked monitoring systems. The Siemens PLC controller should be configured with documented I/O mapping and alarm threshold settings (low pressure alarm at less than 0.15 MPa) that are compatible with the facility's BMS polling interval. Buyers should require factory functional testing of each communication protocol before shipment, and the 3Q validation package must include OQ test protocols that verify BMS data exchange under simulated operating conditions. Post-installation, the BMS integration must be revalidated whenever PLC firmware is updated or BMS software is upgraded, per FDA 21 CFR Part 11 requirements for electronic records in GMP environments.

Q4: What is the expected inflation-deflation cycle life for pneumatic seals, and what testing standards apply?

Pneumatic silicone rubber seals in biosafety-inflatable-airtight-doors are rated for inflation to 0.25 MPa or above, with cycle times of 5 seconds or less for both inflation and deflation. Seal longevity depends on the silicone rubber compound's compression set characteristics, measured per ASTM D395 Method B at the operating temperature range. A compression set below 15% after 10,000 cycles indicates a compound suitable for 5-year replacement intervals in typical BSL-3 operating conditions (10-20 door cycles per day). Compounds with compression set above 25% at the same cycle count may require replacement at 3-year intervals. Buyers should request the seal compound supplier's test data sheet and verify that the durometer hardness falls within 50-70 Shore A per ASTM D2240, with documented chemical resistance to H2O2, formaldehyde, and common laboratory disinfectants.

Q5: What fire safety requirements apply to biosafety-inflatable-airtight-doors, and how should compliance be verified?

The door assembly core fill must meet Class A fire rating requirements, with the BS-01-IAD-1 specification calling for 180 kg/m3 density rock wool insulation. Buyers should require a fire resistance test report from an accredited laboratory, with the test conducted on a representative door assembly (not the rock wool material alone). The 304/316 stainless steel door frame and leaf provide inherent non-combustibility, but the critical verification point is the fire performance of the complete assembly including seal materials, vision panel (tempered glass), and core fill in combination. For GMP pharmaceutical facilities, fire safety documentation must be included in the IQ phase of the 3Q validation package and cross-referenced with the facility's fire safety risk assessment.

Q6: How should buyers evaluate the emergency escape device on biosafety-inflatable-airtight-doors to ensure compliance with occupational safety requirements?

The emergency escape device must allow personnel to exit the containment zone without requiring electrical power, compressed air supply, or access codes. ISO 45001:2018 requires that occupational health and safety risks, including entrapment in containment zones during power failure or emergency conditions, be assessed and mitigated through engineered controls. During FAT and OQ testing, the escape device must be functionally tested under simulated power failure conditions (no electrical supply, no compressed air) to verify that the door can be opened from the containment side within a defined time threshold. The electromagnetic interlock system must include a documented fail-safe mode that releases the lock upon power loss, and this behavior must be verified during the interlock sequence test in the OQ protocol.
