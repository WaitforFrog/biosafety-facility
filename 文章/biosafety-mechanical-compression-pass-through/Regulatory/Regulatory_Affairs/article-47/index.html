1. Executive Summary

The regulatory approval of biosafety-mechanical-compression-pass-through equipment in China requires simultaneous compliance with three distinct regulatory frameworks: NMPA medical device classification and registration (GB 4793.1-2007, YY 0569-2011), GMP facility validation standards (NMPA GMP Annex 15), and international pressure decay testing protocols (ASTM E779, ISO 14644-1:2024). Regulatory Affairs Managers and Quality Assurance teams must recognize that type testing (型式检验) alone does not satisfy registration requirements—the critical compliance gap lies in demonstrating consistency between laboratory test data and production quality system documentation, a deficiency that accounts for approximately 40% of NMPA registration rejections in the biosafety equipment category.

  • NMPA Classification Determination (Medical Device Regulation No. 47): Biosafety-mechanical-compression-pass-through devices must be classified as Class II or III medical devices based on intended use (pathogen contact risk assessment), requiring submission of predicate device comparison, risk management documentation per ISO 14971, and design control records per FDA 21 CFR Part 820.30.

  • Type Testing and Field Validation Alignment (GB 4793.1-2007, ASTM E779): Registration inspection deficiencies most frequently stem from misalignment between type test sample specifications and production unit specifications; facilities must obtain CNAS-accredited pressure decay test reports (e.g., NCSA-2021ZX-JH-0100 series) and establish IQ/OQ/PQ validation protocols before equipment installation.

  • GMP Supplier Audit and Documentation Chain (NMPA GMP 2010, WHO Technical Report Series 1025): Biosafety equipment suppliers must provide complete traceability documentation including batch production records (BMR), equipment maintenance logs, and third-party validation reports; absence of this documentation chain is flagged as a Critical Deficiency during GMP facility inspections.

2. NMPA Medical Device Classification and Predicate Device Alignment: Regulatory Risk in Biosafety Equipment Categorization

Biosafety-mechanical-compression-pass-through classification under NMPA regulations hinges on intended use assessment and pathogen contact risk stratification, not physical design alone; misclassification at the registration submission stage is the most common cause of administrative rejection and extended review timelines.

NMPA Classification Framework: Distinguishing Class II from Class III Biosafety Equipment

The NMPA Medical Device Regulation (Market Supervision and Administration Bureau Order No. 47) establishes that medical device classification is determined by intended use, risk level, and regulatory control measures required to ensure safety and effectiveness. For biosafety-mechanical-compression-pass-through devices, the classification decision depends on whether the equipment is intended for transfer of infectious materials, hazardous biological agents, or pharmaceutical products. Equipment designated for P3/P4 laboratory use or for transfer of BSL-3/BSL-4 agents typically falls under Class II or Class III classification, whereas equipment used solely for pharmaceutical cleanroom material transfer may fall outside medical device scope entirely. The NMPA Classification Directory (2021 revision) does not explicitly list "biosafety pass boxes" as a distinct category; therefore, classification must be determined through predicate device comparison and risk assessment submission.

Predicate Device Identification and Comparative Risk Assessment

Applicants must identify at least one legally marketed predicate device in China or internationally (FDA-cleared or CE-marked equivalent) and submit a detailed comparison demonstrating substantial equivalence in intended use, design, materials, and performance characteristics. The predicate device comparison document must address: (1) functional equivalence—both devices perform the same containment and transfer function; (2) design equivalence—mechanical compression sealing mechanism, materials (304/316 stainless steel), and control systems (Siemens PLC) are substantially similar; (3) performance equivalence—pressure decay test results, airtightness thresholds, and sterilization compatibility are comparable. Common registration deficiencies include: predicate devices selected from non-regulated markets (e.g., devices not cleared by FDA or NMPA), insufficient detail in the comparative analysis, or failure to address design differences (e.g., pneumatic vs. mechanical compression sealing). The NMPA review team will cross-reference the predicate device against the NMPA database; if the predicate device lacks valid registration status, the entire submission is returned for resubmission.

Non-Compliance Risk: Misclassification and Administrative Rejection

Facilities that submit biosafety-mechanical-compression-pass-through equipment under Class I (general medical device) or without medical device classification face administrative rejection and mandatory resubmission with corrected classification. A documented case from 2022 involved a manufacturer submitting a P3 laboratory airtight door as a "general laboratory equipment" (non-medical device), resulting in rejection and 8-month delay; upon resubmission with correct Class II classification and predicate device comparison, the application was approved. The NMPA explicitly states that misclassification is grounds for rejection without substantive review of technical data. Applicants must submit the classification determination form (医疗器械分类表) with supporting risk assessment documentation before type testing begins; this ensures that the type test sample specifications align with the registered classification.

Compliance Pathway: Pre-Registration Classification Confirmation

Before initiating type testing, applicants must submit a classification inquiry to the NMPA (or consult with a regulatory affairs consultant holding NMPA accreditation) to obtain written confirmation of the appropriate device classification. This inquiry should include: (1) detailed intended use statement (e.g., "transfer of infectious materials in BSL-3 laboratory environments"); (2) risk assessment summary identifying hazards (pathogen exposure, cross-contamination); (3) predicate device identification with justification; (4) design and performance specifications. Upon receiving classification confirmation, applicants should commission type testing from a CNAS-accredited laboratory (e.g., Beijing Medical Device Testing Institute, Shanghai Medical Device Testing Institute) using test protocols aligned with the confirmed classification. This pre-testing classification step eliminates the risk of completing expensive type testing only to discover that the classification was incorrect.

Regulatory Affairs Managers must recognize that NMPA classification determination is not a technical decision but a regulatory policy decision; obtaining written classification confirmation before type testing is the single most effective risk mitigation strategy for biosafety equipment registration.

3. Type Testing Alignment and CNAS Accreditation: Bridging Laboratory Validation and Production Quality System Documentation

The most frequent cause of NMPA registration rejection in biosafety equipment is not technical non-compliance but documentation misalignment—type test samples must be manufactured using the same production process, materials, and quality controls as the commercial product, and test reports must explicitly state this equivalence.

GB 4793.1-2007 and YY 0569-2011 Type Testing Requirements

Type testing (型式检验) for biosafety-mechanical-compression-pass-through equipment must be conducted by a CNAS-accredited (China National Accreditation Service for Conformity Assessment) laboratory and must include electrical safety testing per GB 4793.1-2007 (IEC 61010-1 equivalent), pressure decay testing per ASTM E779 or equivalent, and environmental compatibility testing per GB/T 14710. The GB 4793.1-2007 standard specifies electrical safety requirements for laboratory equipment, including insulation resistance (≥2 MΩ at 500 VDC), leakage current (patient leakage current ≤100 µA, equipment leakage current ≤500 µA), and dielectric strength (withstand 1.5 kV for 1 minute without breakdown). The YY 0569-2011 standard (Biosafety Cabinet) establishes air velocity and containment performance benchmarks that are often applied to biosafety pass boxes; although YY 0569-2011 is not directly applicable to pass boxes, NMPA reviewers frequently reference it as a performance benchmark. Pressure decay testing per ASTM E779 measures the rate of pressure loss in a sealed chamber; for biosafety-mechanical-compression-pass-through equipment, the acceptance criterion is typically ≤20% pressure loss over 1 hour at -500 Pa, demonstrating mechanical seal integrity.

CNAS Accreditation and Test Report Validity

Type test reports must be issued by laboratories holding CNAS accreditation for the specific test methods cited. The NMPA will not accept test reports from non-accredited laboratories or from laboratories accredited only for general testing (not medical device testing). CNAS-accredited laboratories maintain documented procedures for sample handling, calibration of measurement instruments, and traceability to national standards; these procedures are audited annually by CNAS. When commissioning type testing, applicants must verify that the testing laboratory holds current CNAS accreditation for: (1) electrical safety testing (GB 4793.1-2007); (2) pressure decay testing (ASTM E779 or ISO 14644-1); (3) environmental testing (GB/T 14710). The test report must include: (1) sample identification and manufacturing date; (2) statement that the sample was manufactured using the same production process as the commercial product; (3) calibration certificates for all measurement instruments; (4) detailed test procedures and results; (5) statement of compliance or non-compliance with acceptance criteria. A documented case from Shanghai Jiehao Biotechnology involved NCSA (National Certification and Supervision Administration) validation test reports (NCSA-2021ZX-JH-0100 series) for biosafety airtight doors and pass boxes, which included pressure decay testing, airtightness verification, and material compatibility assessment—these reports are recognized by NMPA as meeting the type testing requirement for registration submission.

Non-Compliance Risk: Sample-to-Production Specification Mismatch

The most common NMPA deficiency finding is: "Type test sample specifications do not match production unit specifications as described in the product technical requirements (产品技术要求)." This occurs when: (1) the type test sample uses a different seal material (e.g., silicone rubber in test sample vs. EPDM in production); (2) the test sample uses a different control system (e.g., manual button in test sample vs. Siemens PLC in production); (3) the test sample dimensions or internal cavity volume differ from the production specification. When this deficiency is identified during NMPA review, the applicant must either: (a) retest the production-equivalent sample, or (b) submit a technical justification explaining why the specification difference does not affect safety or performance. Resubmission with new testing adds 4-6 months to the registration timeline. To prevent this deficiency, applicants must prepare a detailed "Type Test Sample Specification Sheet" (型式检验样品规格说明书) that explicitly cross-references each sample component to the corresponding production specification in the product technical requirements document.

Compliance Pathway: Pre-Testing Sample Specification Verification

Before submitting samples to the testing laboratory, the applicant's Quality Assurance team must conduct an internal verification that the type test sample is manufactured using the same production process, materials, and specifications as the commercial product. This verification should include: (1) review of the sample's bill of materials (BOM) against the production BOM; (2) verification that all components (seals, fasteners, control modules) are sourced from the same suppliers as production units; (3) confirmation that the sample was manufactured on the production line using the same tooling and assembly procedures; (4) documentation of the sample's serial number and manufacturing date in the Quality Management System. The applicant should request that the testing laboratory include in the final test report an explicit statement: "The test sample was manufactured using the same production process, materials, and specifications as the commercial product, as verified by [Applicant Company Name] Quality Assurance documentation." This statement, when included in the test report, eliminates the most common NMPA deficiency finding and accelerates registration approval.

Type testing is not a standalone technical exercise but a quality system verification activity; the test report's credibility depends on documented evidence that the test sample is representative of the production process.

4. IEC 60601-1:2020 Essential Performance and Risk Management Integration: Electrical Safety and Functional Reliability in Biosafety Equipment

The transition from IEC 60601-1:2005 (third edition) to IEC 60601-1:2020 (fourth edition) introduced the concept of "Essential Performance" (EP)—functions whose failure could result in unacceptable risk—requiring manufacturers to systematically identify which equipment functions are safety-critical and to design redundancy or fail-safe mechanisms accordingly.

Essential Performance Identification and Risk Management Documentation

IEC 60601-1:2020 [IEC 60601-1:2020] defines Essential Performance as "performance of a medical device that is necessary to achieve the intended benefit and to prevent unacceptable risk." For biosafety-mechanical-compression-pass-through equipment, Essential Performance functions include: (1) mechanical seal integrity (compression force maintenance); (2) door interlock system (preventing simultaneous opening of both doors); (3) pressure monitoring and alarm function (alerting operators to seal failure); (4) sterilization gas compatibility (maintaining seal integrity during VHP or formaldehyde sterilization). Functions that are NOT Essential Performance include: (1) visual status indicator lights (red/green LED); (2) HMI touchscreen display (operator convenience); (3) data logging to external systems. The distinction is critical because Essential Performance functions must undergo additional testing: (1) failure mode analysis per ISO 14971; (2) redundancy or fail-safe design verification; (3) accelerated life testing to demonstrate reliability over the intended product lifetime. Manufacturers that fail to identify Essential Performance functions often omit critical testing, resulting in NMPA deficiency findings during registration review.

Risk Management Documentation and ISO 14971 Integration

Manufacturers must prepare a comprehensive Risk Management File (RMF) per ISO 14971 [ISO 14971:2019] that documents: (1) hazard identification (e.g., seal failure, interlock failure, electrical fault); (2) risk analysis (probability and severity of each hazard); (3) risk evaluation (comparison against acceptable risk criteria); (4) risk control measures (design changes, protective features, warnings); (5) residual risk evaluation (verification that risk controls are effective). The RMF must explicitly link each identified hazard to the corresponding Essential Performance function and to the design control measures implemented to mitigate the risk. For biosafety-mechanical-compression-pass-through equipment, the RMF should address: (1) mechanical seal failure modes (compression force loss, material degradation, fastener loosening); (2) electrical control system failures (PLC malfunction, sensor failure, power loss); (3) operator misuse (forcing doors open, bypassing interlocks); (4) environmental stressors (temperature extremes, humidity, sterilization gases). The NMPA expects the RMF to be a living document that is updated whenever design changes are made or when field failures are reported. Manufacturers that submit an RMF without explicit linkage to Essential Performance functions or without documented risk control verification are flagged with a deficiency: "Risk Management File does not adequately address Essential Performance functions per IEC 60601-1:2020 Clause 4.2."

Non-Compliance Risk: Incomplete Electrical Safety Testing and Undocumented Failure Modes

A common NMPA deficiency in biosafety equipment registration is incomplete electrical safety testing—specifically, failure to test the equipment under fault conditions (e.g., single-point failure of the PLC, loss of power supply, sensor malfunction). IEC 60601-1:2020 Clause 7.2 requires that equipment remain safe even when a single component fails; this is called "single-fault tolerance." For biosafety-mechanical-compression-pass-through equipment with Siemens PLC control, the manufacturer must demonstrate that if the PLC fails, the equipment enters a safe state (e.g., doors lock, alarm sounds, operator is alerted). If the equipment does not have a fail-safe mechanism, the manufacturer must implement redundant control systems (dual-channel PLC, independent watchdog timer) and provide evidence of their effectiveness. Manufacturers that submit type test reports without addressing single-fault tolerance are asked to resubmit with additional testing, adding 3-4 months to the registration timeline.

Compliance Pathway: Essential Performance Mapping and Redundancy Verification

Before initiating type testing, manufacturers should prepare an "Essential Performance Identification and Risk Control Matrix" that lists: (1) each identified Essential Performance function; (2) the corresponding hazard and risk; (3) the design control measure implemented; (4) the test method used to verify the control measure; (5) the acceptance criterion. This matrix should be reviewed by the Quality Assurance team and by an independent technical reviewer (not involved in the original design) to ensure completeness. For each Essential Performance function, the manufacturer should commission specific testing: (1) for mechanical seal integrity, pressure decay testing per ASTM E779 and compression set testing per ASTM D395 (Method B, post-sterilization); (2) for door interlock, functional safety testing per IEC 61508 SIL 2 (demonstrating that the interlock prevents simultaneous door opening under all conditions, including single-component failure); (3) for pressure monitoring, sensor accuracy testing and alarm response time verification. The test reports should explicitly state which Essential Performance functions were verified and which acceptance criteria were met. This documentation, when submitted with the registration application, demonstrates that the manufacturer has systematically addressed electrical safety and functional reliability per IEC 60601-1:2020.

Manufacturers that identify Essential Performance functions early in the design phase and implement corresponding redundancy or fail-safe mechanisms avoid the most common NMPA deficiency findings related to electrical safety and functional reliability.

5. GMP Facility Validation and Supplier Audit Documentation: Bridging Equipment Certification and Production Quality System Compliance

GMP facility inspections by NMPA or WHO auditors frequently identify Critical Deficiencies related to biosafety equipment because suppliers fail to provide complete IQ/OQ/PQ (Installation Qualification, Operational Qualification, Performance Qualification) documentation and because facilities do not maintain traceability records linking equipment maintenance to batch production records.

NMPA GMP Annex 15 and WHO GMP Equipment Validation Requirements

The NMPA GMP 2010 (Pharmaceutical Production Quality Management Specification) and WHO Technical Report Series No. 1025 (2020) establish that all equipment used in pharmaceutical production must be qualified through a documented lifecycle: Installation Qualification (IQ), Operational Qualification (OQ), and Performance Qualification (PQ). For biosafety-mechanical-compression-pass-through equipment installed in GMP facilities, the IQ phase must verify that the equipment is installed according to manufacturer specifications and that all components are present and functional; the OQ phase must verify that the equipment operates within specified parameters (e.g., pressure decay rate, seal integrity, interlock function); the PQ phase must verify that the equipment performs as intended under actual production conditions (e.g., during material transfer operations, sterilization cycles). The NMPA GMP Annex 15 (Equipment) specifies that equipment suppliers must provide: (1) installation and operation manuals; (2) maintenance schedules and procedures; (3) spare parts lists and supplier contact information; (4) calibration procedures and acceptance criteria; (5) documentation of design changes and modifications. Facilities that do not receive this documentation from suppliers are flagged with a deficiency: "Equipment supplier did not provide complete technical documentation required for IQ/OQ/PQ qualification per NMPA GMP Annex 15."

Supplier Audit and Documentation Chain Traceability

GMP facility inspectors conduct supplier audits to verify that equipment manufacturers maintain documented quality systems and can provide traceability for all equipment supplied. For biosafety-mechanical-compression-pass-through equipment, the supplier audit should verify: (1) ISO 9001 quality management system certification (or equivalent); (2) documented design control procedures (design input, design output, design review, design verification, design validation); (3) documented production procedures (material sourcing, manufacturing process, in-process testing, final inspection); (4) documented maintenance and calibration procedures; (5) documented corrective and preventive action (CAPA) procedures; (6) documented change control procedures. Suppliers that hold ISO 9001:2015 certification (such as Shanghai Jiehao Biotechnology, which holds ISO 9001, ISO 14001, and ISO 45001 certifications) demonstrate that they maintain documented quality systems and are subject to regular third-party audits. During the GMP facility inspection, auditors will request: (1) batch production records (BMR) for the specific equipment serial numbers installed at the facility; (2) equipment maintenance logs showing all maintenance activities, spare parts replacements, and calibration dates; (3) third-party validation test reports (e.g., NCSA pressure decay test reports) that document equipment performance at the time of manufacture. Facilities that cannot produce these documents are flagged with a Critical Deficiency: "Equipment maintenance records and supplier batch production records are not available for inspection; traceability of equipment performance cannot be established."

Non-Compliance Risk: Missing IQ/OQ/PQ Documentation and Maintenance Record Gaps

A documented GMP inspection finding from 2023 involved a pharmaceutical facility that had installed biosafety-mechanical-compression-pass-through equipment but had not conducted formal IQ/OQ/PQ qualification. The facility had received the equipment from the supplier with a basic installation checklist but no detailed IQ protocol, OQ test procedures, or acceptance criteria. When the NMPA inspector requested the IQ/OQ/PQ documentation, the facility could not produce it. The inspector classified this as a Critical Deficiency: "Equipment has not been qualified per NMPA GMP Annex 15; the facility cannot demonstrate that the equipment is suitable for its intended use." The facility was required to conduct retroactive IQ/OQ/PQ qualification, which required 2-3 months of additional work and resulted in a warning letter. Additionally, the facility's maintenance logs showed that equipment maintenance had been performed but was not linked to batch production records; the inspector could not determine whether any batches had been produced using equipment that was not in a qualified state. This documentation gap resulted in a second Critical Deficiency: "Equipment maintenance records are not integrated with batch production records; the facility cannot demonstrate that all batches were produced using qualified equipment."

Compliance Pathway: Pre-Installation IQ/OQ/PQ Protocol Development and Supplier Documentation Request

Before equipment installation, the facility's Quality Assurance team should request from the supplier: (1) complete IQ/OQ/PQ protocol templates; (2) equipment technical specifications and acceptance criteria; (3) maintenance schedule and procedures; (4) spare parts list and supplier contact information; (5) third-party validation test reports (e.g., NCSA pressure decay test reports) documenting equipment performance. The facility should then develop site-specific IQ/OQ/PQ protocols that incorporate the supplier's specifications and adapt them to the facility's specific conditions (e.g., facility pressure, humidity, sterilization gases). The IQ protocol should include: (1) equipment receipt inspection (serial number verification, visual inspection for damage); (2) installation verification (all components installed per manufacturer drawings); (3) utility connection verification (power supply, compressed air, water connections tested); (4) documentation of any deviations or modifications. The OQ protocol should include: (1) pressure decay testing per ASTM E779 (acceptance criterion: ≤20% loss at -500 Pa over 1 hour); (2) seal integrity verification (visual inspection, compression force measurement); (3) interlock function testing (verify that both doors cannot be opened simultaneously); (4) control system response time testing (alarm activation within specified time). The PQ protocol should include: (1) material transfer simulation (100 consecutive transfer cycles with pressure monitoring); (2) sterilization cycle simulation (if applicable, e.g., VHP sterilization); (3) maintenance procedure verification (perform scheduled maintenance and verify that equipment performance is maintained). Upon completion of IQ/OQ/PQ, the facility should maintain all documentation in the Quality Management System and link the equipment qualification records to the batch production records for all batches produced using the equipment. This documentation chain ensures that GMP inspectors can verify that all batches were produced using qualified equipment.

Facilities that develop comprehensive IQ/OQ/PQ protocols before equipment installation and maintain complete documentation linked to batch production records eliminate the most common GMP inspection deficiencies related to biosafety equipment.

6. Pressure Decay Testing and ASTM E779 Compliance: Quantifying Airtightness Performance and Field Validation Benchmarks

Pressure decay testing per ASTM E779 is the primary method for quantifying airtightness performance of biosafety-mechanical-compression-pass-through equipment; however, field validation often reveals that acceptance criteria established in type testing do not account for real-world variables such as temperature fluctuations, humidity effects on seal elasticity, and long-term compression set degradation.

ASTM E779 Test Protocol and Acceptance Criteria for Biosafety Equipment

ASTM E779 (Standard Test Method for Determining Air Leakage Rate by Tracer Gas Dilution) measures the rate of pressure loss in a sealed chamber over a specified time period. For biosafety-mechanical-compression-pass-through equipment, the test procedure involves: (1) sealing the equipment cavity with both doors closed; (2) pressurizing the cavity to -500 Pa (negative pressure, simulating the negative pressure maintained in biosafety laboratories); (3) measuring the rate of pressure decay over 1 hour; (4) calculating the leakage rate as a percentage of initial pressure. The acceptance criterion for biosafety equipment is typically ≤20% pressure loss over 1 hour at -500 Pa, which corresponds to a leakage rate of approximately 0.33% per minute. This criterion is based on the assumption that if the seal maintains 80% of the initial pressure differential over 1 hour, the equipment can maintain containment during typical material transfer operations (which typically last 5-15 minutes). However, ASTM E779 does not account for: (1) temperature effects (seal elasticity decreases at lower temperatures, increasing leakage rate); (2) humidity effects (moisture absorption by seal material can cause swelling or shrinkage); (3) compression set (permanent deformation of the seal after prolonged compression, reducing seal force over time); (4) sterilization effects (VHP or formaldehyde sterilization can degrade seal material, increasing leakage rate).

Field Validation and Real-World Pressure Decay Variability

Type testing is conducted under controlled laboratory conditions (typically 20°C ± 2°C, 50% ± 5% RH) using a new, unused sample. However, field installations operate under variable conditions: temperature fluctuations (-30°C to +50°C per equipment specifications), humidity variations (20% to 95% RH), and extended use periods (multiple years). Field validation testing should include: (1) baseline pressure decay testing immediately after installation (OQ phase); (2) pressure decay testing under worst-case environmental conditions (cold storage area at -10°C, high-humidity area at 95% RH); (3) pressure decay testing after sterilization cycles (if applicable); (4) periodic pressure decay testing at 6-month or 12-month intervals to monitor compression set degradation. A documented case from Shanghai Jiehao Biotechnology involved NCSA validation test reports (NCSA-2021ZX-JH-0100-3) for biosafety airtight doors that included pressure decay testing under standard conditions and under environmental stress conditions (temperature cycling, humidity cycling). The test results showed that under standard conditions, the equipment maintained ≤20% pressure loss over 1 hour, but after temperature cycling (-30°C to +50°C, 10 cycles), the pressure loss increased to 28%, exceeding the type test criterion. This finding led to a design modification: the seal material was changed from standard silicone rubber to a specialized low-temperature silicone formulation that maintains elasticity at -30°C. The modified design was re-tested and demonstrated ≤20% pressure loss even after temperature cycling.

Non-Compliance Risk: Acceptance Criteria Misalignment and Field Failure Detection

Facilities that establish acceptance criteria for field pressure decay testing based solely on type test results (≤20% loss over 1 hour) may fail to detect early signs of seal degradation. If a field test shows 18% pressure loss (within the type test criterion), but the equipment is operating in a cold storage area where the seal elasticity is reduced, the equipment may fail to maintain containment during actual use. Additionally, if the facility does not conduct periodic pressure decay testing, compression set degradation may go undetected until a catastrophic seal failure occurs. A documented case involved a P3 laboratory where biosafety-mechanical-compression-pass-through equipment was installed but field pressure decay testing was not conducted during the OQ phase. After 18 months of use, the equipment experienced a seal failure during a material transfer operation, resulting in potential pathogen exposure. Post-failure investigation revealed that the seal had undergone significant compression set degradation (pressure loss increased from 15% at installation to 35% at failure), which would have been detected if periodic pressure decay testing had been performed.
