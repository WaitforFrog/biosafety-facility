1. Executive Summary

Regulatory Framework and Compliance Scope: Double-inflatable-airtight-doors represent a critical containment barrier in biosafety laboratory infrastructure, subject to overlapping regulatory frameworks including ISO 14644-1:2024 (cleanroom air classification), GB 50346-2011 (biosafety laboratory building specifications), and GMP Annex 1 (pharmaceutical manufacturing environment controls). Compliance validation requires a structured qualification pathway integrating Installation Qualification (IQ), Operational Qualification (OQ), and Performance Qualification (PQ) protocols, supported by third-party pressure decay testing and documented airtightness verification.

  • Regulatory Requirement 1: Pressure decay testing under ASTM E779 methodology must demonstrate that room pressure remains stable within ±250 Pa over 20 minutes at -500 Pa differential, with independent NCSA validation reports required for NMPA/FDA registration submissions.

  • Regulatory Requirement 2: IQ/OQ/PQ validation documentation must establish clear traceability between design specifications (URS), commissioning data (FAT/SAT), and field qualification results, with documented evidence that installation configuration matches approved technical drawings and that all calibration certificates maintain measurement uncertainty ≤10% of acceptance criteria.

  • Regulatory Requirement 3: Pneumatic seal integrity verification requires PAO particle scanning per ISO 14644-3:2019 methodology, with edge-frame sealing assessment and documented remediation protocols for any detected leakage exceeding 0.01% local transparency threshold.

2. Commissioning and Qualification Boundary Definition: Distinguishing FAT/SAT Data from IQ/OQ Validation Evidence

Commissioning activities establish baseline equipment functionality; qualification activities independently verify that installed equipment meets user requirements and regulatory standards. The critical distinction lies in purpose and independence: commissioning proves the equipment operates per supplier specifications (Prove It Works), while qualification proves the equipment satisfies user needs and regulatory requirements (Prove It Is Suitable for Intended Use).

GB 50346-2011 and GMP Annex 1: The Regulatory Separation of Commissioning from Qualification

Commissioning encompasses Design Qualification (DQ), Factory Acceptance Testing (FAT), Site Acceptance Testing (SAT), and initial functional testing conducted by or under supplier supervision. Qualification encompasses IQ (Installation Qualification), OQ (Operational Qualification), and PQ (Performance Qualification) conducted by or under user/quality assurance supervision. The regulatory requirement is explicit: commissioning data may be referenced within qualification protocols, but qualification activities must independently verify the completeness and compliance status of commissioning data rather than accepting commissioning results as direct substitutes for qualification testing.

ISPE GAMP 5 and EU GMP Annex 15: Documentation Traceability and Data Cross-Reference Requirements

When commissioning and qualification use identical test methodologies (e.g., pressure decay testing), commissioning data may be cross-referenced in qualification protocols only if: (1) the test methodology is documented as equivalent to the qualification protocol, (2) the commissioning test report includes all required GMP documentation elements (signatures, dates, equipment calibration records, environmental conditions), and (3) the qualification protocol explicitly references the commissioning report by document number and includes independent verification that the commissioning data meets qualification acceptance criteria. Common non-compliance findings include: commissioning test reports lacking GMP-required signatures or dates; commissioning and qualification protocols using different acceptance standards without documented justification; and qualification protocols stating "per commissioning report" without independent data verification or cross-reference documentation.

Handover Package Requirements and File Integrity Verification

The transition from commissioning to qualification requires a formal handover package containing: commissioning completion report with sign-off, all commissioning test records with raw data, operator training records, spare parts inventory, and complete operation/maintenance manuals. Qualification specialists must verify that the handover package is complete and that all referenced test data is retrievable and legible before accepting commissioning data as supporting evidence for qualification. If commissioning test records are incomplete (missing environmental conditions, calibration data, or acceptance criteria), the qualification protocol must specify that the affected tests will be repeated during OQ rather than relying on incomplete commissioning data.

Non-Compliance Risk: Missing IQ/OQ Documentation Chain and Regulatory Audit Consequences

Regulatory inspections frequently identify deficiencies where commissioning and qualification activities are conflated without clear documentation boundaries. Specific audit findings include: (1) IQ protocols that reference FAT/SAT data without independent verification of data completeness, (2) OQ test reports that cite commissioning pressure decay results without re-testing to confirm data validity, and (3) missing traceability between design specifications (URS), commissioning configuration, and qualification test parameters. These deficiencies result in FDA Form 483 observations or NMPA non-conformance notices requiring remediation documentation and, in severe cases, product hold or facility inspection escalation.

Compliance Action Roadmap: Establish a formal document control procedure that designates commissioning activities as supplier-led and qualification activities as user-led, with explicit sign-off gates between phases. Require that all commissioning test reports be reviewed by quality assurance before acceptance into the qualification file. Implement a traceability matrix linking URS requirements → design specifications → commissioning test parameters → qualification acceptance criteria, with documented evidence that each requirement is addressed at each phase. For pressure decay testing specifically, require that OQ include at least one independent pressure decay test conducted by user personnel or third-party laboratory, even if commissioning pressure decay data is available, to establish independent verification of airtightness compliance.

3. HEPA Filter Integrity Verification: PAO Scanning Methodology and Installation Sealing Assessment Under ISO 14644-3:2019

Approximately 60% of field HEPA filter integrity test failures result from installation sealing defects rather than filter media degradation; systematic edge-frame assessment and installation configuration verification are prerequisite to filter replacement decisions. ISO 14644-3:2019 specifies that HEPA filter integrity testing must include both filter media scanning and edge-frame sealing verification, with particular attention to the filter pack seal interface where the filter frame contacts the installation frame.

ISO 14644-3:2019 and IEST-RP-CC034: PAO Scanning Protocol and Acceptance Criteria for Biosafety Installations

The regulatory requirement for HEPA filter integrity testing in biosafety laboratory installations is established by ISO 14644-3:2019 Section 7 (Particle Counting and Sizing Equipment) and Section 8 (Airborne Particle Concentration Measurement), which mandate that HEPA filter scanning be conducted using Photometric Aerosol Analyzer (PAO) methodology with particle size detection at 0.5 micrometers. The scanning protocol requires: (1) upstream challenge aerosol concentration uniformity verification before scanning begins, (2) scanning path overlap of ≥30%, (3) scanning speed ≤5 cm/s, (4) scanning head distance from filter surface ≤25 mm, and (5) edge-frame scanning within 13 mm of the filter-to-frame interface. Acceptance criteria specify that overall filter transparency must not exceed 0.01% and that any localized area exceeding 0.01% transparency must not occupy more than 0.5% of total filter area.

Common Installation Sealing Defects and Remediation Pathways

When PAO scanning detects localized transparency exceeding 0.01%, the first diagnostic step is to assess whether the leakage is located at the filter pack seal interface (edge-frame region) or within the filter media itself. If leakage is concentrated at the edge-frame, the defect is typically an installation sealing issue rather than filter media failure. Remediation options include: (1) verifying that all installation fasteners (typically 4-8 bolts around the filter frame perimeter) are tightened to specification (typically 15-25 N·m torque), (2) inspecting the filter pack seal gasket for compression set or material degradation, (3) applying silicone sealant to small localized gaps (≤2 mm width) at the filter-frame interface, or (4) replacing the filter pack seal gasket if compression set exceeds 25% per ASTM D395 Method B. If leakage persists after these remediation steps, filter replacement is indicated. If leakage is distributed across the filter media rather than concentrated at edges, filter replacement is the appropriate action.

Regulatory Audit Focus: Documentation of Filter Integrity Test Results and Traceability to Installation Configuration

Regulatory inspections of biosafety facilities frequently identify deficiencies in HEPA filter integrity documentation, including: (1) PAO scanning reports lacking quantified transparency data or acceptance criteria reference, (2) missing documentation of upstream aerosol uniformity verification, (3) no traceability between filter model/serial number and scanning results, (4) absence of edge-frame scanning data or justification for why edge-frame assessment was omitted, and (5) no documented remediation actions when scanning results approach or exceed acceptance thresholds. These deficiencies result in FDA Form 483 observations requiring submission of corrective action plans and evidence of repeat testing with documented compliance.

Compliance Action Roadmap: Establish a HEPA filter integrity testing protocol that mandates PAO scanning at installation and annually thereafter, with documented upstream aerosol uniformity verification, edge-frame scanning coverage, and quantified transparency data by scanning zone. Require that all scanning reports include filter model, serial number, installation location, scanning date, and acceptance criteria reference. Implement a decision tree for remediation: if edge-frame leakage is detected, conduct fastener torque verification and gasket inspection before authorizing filter replacement. Document all remediation actions with before/after scanning data. Maintain a filter integrity test file with all scanning reports, remediation records, and filter replacement justifications for regulatory inspection readiness.

4. Measurement Equipment Calibration and Traceability: Ensuring Pressure and Temperature Sensor Validity for Qualification Testing

Calibration certificate validity does not guarantee measurement data usability; measurement uncertainty must be quantified and verified to be ≤10% of acceptance criteria range to ensure qualification test results withstand regulatory scrutiny. Pressure sensors used in double-inflatable-airtight-doors qualification testing (e.g., differential pressure transmitters for pressure decay testing) must maintain calibration certificates that explicitly state measurement uncertainty, with documented evidence that uncertainty is proportional to the acceptance criteria range.

ISO 17025 and ISO 10012: Calibration Traceability Chain and Measurement Uncertainty Requirements

The regulatory requirement for measurement equipment calibration is established by ISO 17025:2017 (General Requirements for the Competence of Testing and Calibration Laboratories) and ISO 10012:2003 (Measurement Management Systems — Requirements for Measuring Equipment). These standards mandate that all measurement equipment used in qualification testing must be calibrated by laboratories accredited to ISO 17025 or equivalent, with calibration certificates that include: (1) equipment identification (model, serial number), (2) calibration date and valid-until date, (3) calibration method and environmental conditions, (4) calibration data with quantified measurement uncertainty, (5) traceability statement linking the calibration to national or international standards, and (6) accreditation body identification. Measurement uncertainty must be expressed as an expanded uncertainty (typically at 95% confidence level, k=2) and must be documented in the calibration certificate.

Pressure Sensor Calibration for Pressure Decay Testing: Acceptance Criteria Alignment and Uncertainty Quantification

For double-inflatable-airtight-doors pressure decay testing, the acceptance criterion is typically ±250 Pa pressure change over 20 minutes at -500 Pa differential (per GB 50346-2011). The differential pressure transmitter used to measure this parameter must have calibration uncertainty ≤25 Pa (i.e., ≤10% of the 250 Pa acceptance range). If a pressure transmitter has calibration uncertainty of ±50 Pa, the measurement uncertainty is 20% of the acceptance range, which is excessive and renders the test data unsuitable for regulatory submission. Calibration certificates must explicitly state uncertainty; if a certificate states only "±0.5% of full scale" without specifying the full-scale range, the actual uncertainty in Pa cannot be determined and the certificate is insufficient for qualification purposes.

Calibration Traceability Chain and Documentation Requirements for Regulatory Submission

The traceability chain for measurement equipment must be documented as: Measurement Equipment → Calibration Laboratory (ISO 17025 accredited) → Reference Standard (ISO 17025 accredited) → National/International Standard (NIST, PTB, or equivalent). Each link in the chain must be supported by a calibration certificate. If a calibration laboratory uses a reference standard, that reference standard must have its own valid calibration certificate from a higher-level laboratory. Regulatory inspections frequently identify deficiencies where: (1) calibration certificates lack measurement uncertainty statements, (2) reference standards used by the calibration laboratory are not themselves calibrated, (3) calibration certificates are expired or approaching expiration without evidence of recalibration, or (4) measurement equipment is used in qualification testing before calibration certificates are obtained. These deficiencies result in regulatory findings that qualification test data is not traceable to recognized standards and therefore unsuitable for product registration.

In-Process Calibration Verification and Mid-Test Validation Protocols

For high-risk qualification tests (e.g., pressure decay testing for airtightness verification), regulatory best practice includes in-process calibration verification: before and after the test, the measurement equipment is compared against a secondary reference standard or calibrated check standard to verify that no drift has occurred during the test. If drift exceeds ±5% of the measurement uncertainty, the test is considered invalid and must be repeated. This practice is particularly important for pressure transmitters used in extended pressure decay tests (20+ minutes), where sensor drift can accumulate. Documentation of in-process verification must be included in the test report.

Compliance Action Roadmap: Establish a measurement equipment management procedure that requires all calibration certificates to explicitly state measurement uncertainty in the same units as the acceptance criteria (e.g., Pa for pressure, °C for temperature). Before using any measurement equipment in qualification testing, verify that the calibration certificate is current (within valid-until date) and that measurement uncertainty is ≤10% of the applicable acceptance criteria range. Maintain a calibration tracking log with equipment serial number, calibration date, valid-until date, and uncertainty value. For pressure decay testing specifically, implement in-process calibration verification by comparing the differential pressure transmitter against a calibrated reference gauge before and after the test. Document all calibration certificates and verification records in the qualification file for regulatory inspection readiness.

5. Installation Qualification Protocol Development: Bridging User Requirements Specifications (URS) to Field Verification Checklist

IQ protocols that replicate equipment manufacturer documentation without independent verification of site-specific installation configuration represent a common regulatory deficiency; effective IQ must verify that actual installation matches both URS requirements and approved technical drawings. Installation Qualification establishes the baseline that the equipment is installed correctly, with all utilities connected per specification, all safety interlocks functional, and all documentation complete before operational testing begins.

ISPE GAMP 5 and EU GMP Annex 15: IQ Protocol Structure and Acceptance Criteria Specification Requirements

The regulatory requirement for IQ protocol development is established by ISPE GAMP 5 (Good Automated Manufacturing Practice) Section 2 (Lifecycle Approach) and EU GMP Annex 15 (Qualification and Validation), which mandate that IQ protocols must: (1) reference the User Requirements Specification (URS) and design specifications, (2) define specific installation verification activities with quantifiable acceptance criteria, (3) identify responsible parties and sign-off authorities, (4) specify environmental conditions and prerequisites for IQ execution, and (5) include a checklist of installation configuration items to be verified. IQ acceptance criteria must be measurable and verifiable; statements such as "equipment installed per supplier specifications" are insufficient without explicit reference to the specific supplier document and verification method.

IQ Verification Checklist: Equipment Identity, Installation Configuration, and Utility Connection Verification

The IQ checklist for double-inflatable-airtight-doors must include: (1) equipment model and serial number verification against purchase order and delivery documentation, (2) physical inspection for shipping damage or corrosion, (3) installation location verification against approved site layout drawings (dimensions, clearances, orientation), (4) door frame installation verification (frame dimensions, wall thickness compatibility, fastener torque specifications), (5) pneumatic gas source connection verification (inlet pressure 0.6 MPa per specification, dual-channel regulator configuration, outlet pressure 0.2-0.3 MPa to pneumatic seals), (6) electrical power connection verification (220V 50Hz, 0.5 kW capacity, grounding continuity test per IEC 61010-1), (7) control system wiring verification (door lock solenoid, indicator lights, emergency stop button functionality), (8) calibration certificate verification for all measurement equipment (differential pressure transmitters, temperature sensors), and (9) documentation package completeness (operation manual, maintenance manual, spare parts list, training records).

Common IQ Non-Compliance Findings and Regulatory Audit Consequences

Regulatory inspections frequently identify IQ deficiencies including: (1) IQ protocols that reference "per supplier specifications" without explicit acceptance criteria or verification methods, (2) missing verification of pneumatic gas source pressure or regulator configuration, (3) no documentation of electrical grounding continuity testing, (4) calibration certificates for measurement equipment that are expired or lack measurement uncertainty statements, (5) IQ checklists that are completed without evidence of actual verification (e.g., no measurement data, no photographs, no signatures), and (6) discrepancies between approved installation drawings and actual field installation without documented deviation justification. These deficiencies result in FDA Form 483 observations or NMPA non-conformance notices requiring submission of corrective action plans and evidence of repeat IQ execution with documented compliance.

IQ Deviation Management and Change Control Integration

If IQ verification identifies a discrepancy between approved installation drawings and actual field installation (e.g., door frame width differs from drawing by >50 mm, pneumatic gas source pressure is 0.55 MPa instead of 0.6 MPa), the deviation must be documented in a formal deviation report that includes: (1) description of the discrepancy, (2) root cause analysis, (3) assessment of impact on equipment functionality and regulatory compliance, (4) proposed remediation action (e.g., adjust regulator pressure, modify installation configuration, revise approved drawing), and (5) approval by quality assurance and facility management. The deviation report must be cross-referenced in the IQ protocol and included in the qualification file. Deviations that cannot be remediated must result in a documented decision to reject the installation and require corrective action before IQ sign-off.

Compliance Action Roadmap: Develop an IQ protocol template that includes a detailed installation verification checklist with specific acceptance criteria for each item (e.g., "pneumatic inlet pressure 0.6 MPa ±0.05 MPa" rather than "pneumatic pressure per specification"). Require that all IQ verification activities include quantified measurement data, photographs of critical installation points (gas connections, electrical grounding, control system wiring), and signatures of personnel performing verification. Implement a deviation management procedure that requires formal documentation and approval for any discrepancies between approved drawings and actual installation. Before OQ begins, conduct a final IQ sign-off review that confirms all verification activities are complete, all acceptance criteria are met, and all deviations are resolved or formally approved.

6. Regulatory Pathway and Certification Requirements: NMPA, FDA, and CE MDR Registration for Biosafety Equipment

Product registration pathways for double-inflatable-airtight-doors differ significantly across jurisdictions; NMPA registration requires Class II medical device classification with IQ/OQ/PQ validation documentation, FDA registration requires 510(k) submission with biocompatibility and sterility data (if applicable), and CE MDR registration requires technical file assembly with risk management documentation per ISO 14971. Understanding jurisdiction-specific requirements and documentation expectations is essential for regulatory submission success.

NMPA Registration Pathway: Medical Device Classification and Technical Documentation Requirements

In China, double-inflatable-airtight-doors are typically classified as Class II medical devices under NMPA (National Medical Products Administration, formerly CFDA) regulations. The registration pathway requires submission of: (1) product technical documentation including design specifications, materials of construction, and performance test data, (2) biocompatibility assessment (if materials contact biological agents), (3) sterilization validation (if applicable), (4) clinical evaluation or substantial equivalence assessment, (5) risk management documentation per ISO 14971, (6) quality management system documentation (ISO 13485 compliance), and (7) IQ/OQ/PQ validation protocols and reports demonstrating that the equipment meets intended use requirements. NMPA registration typically requires 6-12 months from submission to approval, with potential for additional information requests (ARIs) that extend the timeline.

FDA 510(k) Submission and Predicate Device Selection for Biosafety Equipment

In the United States, double-inflatable-airtight-doors are subject to FDA regulation as medical devices (21 CFR Part 820). The registration pathway typically requires a 510(k) submission demonstrating substantial equivalence to a predicate device already cleared by FDA. The 510(k) submission must include: (1) device description and intended use statement, (2) comparison to predicate device (performance characteristics, materials, design, manufacturing process), (3) performance testing data (pressure decay testing, seal integrity verification, control system functionality), (4) biocompatibility assessment (if materials contact biological agents), (5) sterilization validation (if applicable), (6) labeling and instructions for use, and (7) quality system documentation. FDA 510(k) review typically requires 30-90 days (standard review) or 10-30 days (expedited review if predicate device is well-established).

CE MDR Technical File Assembly and Notified Body Engagement

In the European Union, double-inflatable-airtight-doors are subject to Medical Device Regulation (MDR) 2017/745. CE MDR registration requires assembly of a technical file containing: (1) device description and intended use, (2) risk management documentation per ISO 14971 (hazard identification, risk analysis, risk evaluation, risk control measures), (3) design and development documentation (design specifications, design review records, design verification and validation records), (4) manufacturing documentation (manufacturing process, quality control procedures, traceability procedures), (5) performance testing data (pressure decay testing, seal integrity verification, environmental testing), (6) biocompatibility assessment (if applicable), (7) sterilization validation (if applicable), (8) clinical evaluation or clinical data, (9) post-market surveillance plan, and (10) quality management system documentation (ISO 13485 compliance). For Class II devices, CE MDR registration requires engagement with a notified body (third-party conformity assessment organization) to review the technical file and issue a CE certificate. The notified body assessment typically requires 3-6 months.

IQ/OQ/PQ Validation Documentation as Regulatory Evidence for Product Registration

Across all regulatory jurisdictions, IQ/OQ/PQ validation documentation serves as critical evidence that the equipment meets intended use requirements and performs reliably in the field. Regulatory submissions must include: (1) IQ protocol and report demonstrating correct installation and utility connections, (2) OQ protocol and report demonstrating that equipment operates within specified parameters (pressure decay testing, seal integrity verification, control system functionality), (3) PQ protocol and report demonstrating that equipment performs under actual use conditions (extended operational testing, environmental monitoring), and (4) third-party validation reports (e.g., NCSA pressure decay test reports) that provide independent verification of critical performance parameters. Regulatory reviewers assess the quality and completeness of validation documentation to determine whether the equipment is suitable for its intended use and whether the manufacturer has adequate quality systems to ensure consistent performance.

Common Regulatory Submission Deficiencies and Remediation Strategies

Regulatory submissions frequently contain deficiencies that result in information requests (ARIs) or rejection, including: (1) incomplete IQ/OQ/PQ documentation (missing protocols, incomplete test data, unsigned reports), (2) pressure decay test data that lacks quantified values or acceptance criteria reference, (3) risk management documentation that does not address specific hazards relevant to biosafety applications (e.g., seal failure leading to containment breach), (4) quality management system documentation that does not address manufacturing controls for critical components (pneumatic seals, door frame welds), (5) biocompatibility assessment that does not address materials in contact with biological agents, and (6) missing traceability between design specifications, manufacturing procedures, and validation testing. Remediation requires submission of supplemental documentation addressing each deficiency with specific evidence of compliance.

Compliance Action Roadmap: Before initiating regulatory submission, conduct a jurisdiction-specific regulatory assessment to identify applicable standards, device classification, and required documentation. Assemble a complete IQ/OQ/PQ validation package with protocols, reports, and supporting data (calibration certificates, test equipment specifications, environmental monitoring records). Engage a regulatory consultant or notified body early in the process to review the technical file for completeness and compliance with jurisdiction-specific requirements. For NMPA registration, ensure that risk management documentation addresses specific hazards relevant to biosafety applications and that quality management system documentation demonstrates manufacturing controls for critical components. For FDA 510(k) submission, identify an appropriate predicate device and document substantial equivalence across all performance characteristics. For CE MDR registration, engage a notified body to conduct conformity assessment and issue a CE certificate before marketing the device in the European Union.

6. FAQ — Regulatory Compliance Guide

Q1: What specific documentation should a facility request from a double-inflatable-airtight-doors supplier to support NMPA registration submission?

A: Facilities must request a complete validation documentation package including IQ/OQ/PQ protocols and reports, third-party pressure decay test reports with quantified values (e.g., NCSA-2021ZX-JH-0100 series reports), risk management documentation per ISO 14971, and quality management system certificates (ISO 9001, ISO 13485). Suppliers with documented deployment at 100+ P3 laboratories and NCSA-certified test reports demonstrate the documentation maturity required for regulatory submission. At this equipment tier, providing a full IQ/OQ/PQ validation package with original NCSA test reports prior to Factory Acceptance Testing is a non-negotiable baseline for NMPA/FDA/CE registration support.

Q2: How should a validation specialist interpret pressure decay test results, and what acceptance criteria apply under GB 50346-2011?

A: GB 50346-2011 specifies that room pressure must remain stable within ±250 Pa over 20 minutes at -500 Pa differential pressure. Pressure decay test results must be reported as quantified pressure change (in Pa) over the 20-minute test period, with documented environmental conditions (temperature, humidity) and calibration certificates for the differential pressure transmitter. Acceptance is achieved if measured pressure decay does not exceed 250 Pa; if pressure decay exceeds 250 Pa, the installation fails the test and remediation (seal repair or replacement) is required before re-testing.

Q3: What are the key differences between commissioning (FAT/SAT) and qualification (IQ/OQ/PQ), and can commissioning data be used to satisfy qualification requirements?

A: Commissioning proves equipment operates per supplier specifications; qualification proves equipment meets user requirements and regulatory standards. Commissioning data may be referenced in qualification protocols only if the test methodology is documented as equivalent, the commissioning report includes all GMP documentation elements (signatures, dates, calibration records), and the qualification protocol includes independent verification that commissioning data meets qualification acceptance criteria. Commissioning data alone does not satisfy qualification requirements; at minimum, OQ must include independent pressure decay testing conducted by user personnel or third-party laboratory.

Q4: What are common audit deficiencies related to HEPA filter integrity testing, and how should facilities prepare for regulatory inspection?

A: Common deficiencies include: PAO scanning reports lacking quantified transparency data, missing upstream aerosol uniformity verification, no edge-frame scanning documentation, and absent remediation records when scanning results approach acceptance thresholds. Facilities should maintain a HEPA filter integrity testing file with all scanning reports (including quantified data by scanning zone), upstream aerosol uniformity verification records, edge-frame scanning coverage documentation, and remediation actions with before/after scanning data. Annual re-testing with documented compliance demonstrates ongoing commitment to containment integrity.

Q5: How should measurement equipment calibration certificates be evaluated to ensure qualification test data is valid for regulatory submission?

A: Calibration certificates must explicitly state measurement uncertainty in the same units as acceptance criteria (e.g., Pa for pressure). Measurement uncertainty must be ≤10% of the applicable acceptance criteria range; if uncertainty exceeds this threshold, test data is unsuitable for regulatory submission
