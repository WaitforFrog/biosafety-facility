2. Pre-Installation Site Survey and Foundation Verification — Dimensional Accuracy and Structural Readiness Assessment

This section establishes the prerequisite site conditions that determine whether equipment can be installed without rework or modification.

Prerequisite: Confirming Foundation Levelness and Wall Opening Cross-Section Geometry Before Equipment Positioning

Before mobile-fogging-disinfectors is positioned at the installation site, the foundation surface and wall opening dimensions must be verified to eliminate the most common installation failure mode: discovering mid-installation that the wall opening narrows due to concrete formwork bow, preventing equipment insertion. The single most critical measurement error is checking opening dimensions only at the visible face without measuring the cross-section at mid-depth and bottom depth, which masks the condition where concrete formwork deflection has narrowed the opening by 8–15 mm at the interior surface.

Foundation levelness must be measured using a digital precision level with 0.01 mm/m resolution across a minimum of four points distributed across the equipment footprint. Wall opening dimensions must be measured at three distinct depths: top surface (exterior face), middle depth (50% through wall thickness), and bottom surface (interior face). Diagonal dimensions of the opening must also be recorded to detect trapezoidal distortion caused by uneven concrete curing or formwork settlement.

Procedure: Systematic Dimensional Survey Using Calibrated Instruments and Documented Measurement Protocol

Execute the foundation survey using the following sequence: (1) place the digital precision level on the foundation surface at the four corners of the planned equipment footprint and record levelness deviation in mm/m for each axis; (2) measure wall opening width and height at the three depths using a calibrated steel tape measure (±1 mm accuracy minimum) and record all six measurements on a temporary survey drawing; (3) measure the two diagonal dimensions of the opening at the middle depth to detect parallelogram distortion; (4) locate all embedded anchor plates, conduit stubs, and ground studs using a metal detector and measure their positions relative to the opening centerline using a measuring tape and reference marks.

Acceptance: Foundation Levelness ≤2 mm/m and Opening Dimensions Within Nominal ±5 mm — Verified by Documented Survey Drawing and Photographic Evidence

Acceptance of the foundation survey requires that (1) levelness deviation does not exceed ±2 mm/m in any direction, with low spots identified and marked for epoxy grout fill before anchor installation; (2) wall opening dimensions at all three depths fall within nominal dimension +0/−5 mm, with the middle depth measurement being the controlling dimension for equipment insertion feasibility. If the middle depth opening width is more than 3 mm narrower than the nominal dimension, the concrete formwork bow must be documented and the equipment supplier must be consulted before proceeding. All survey measurements must be recorded on a temporary drawing with date, technician name, and instrument calibration date noted; photographic evidence of the measurement setup must be retained for the installation record file.

3. Equipment Delivery Inspection and Unpacking Verification — Shipping Damage Documentation and Completeness Checklist

This section establishes the critical first-contact procedure that determines whether shipping damage liability is assigned to the carrier or the installer.

Prerequisite: Establishing Damage Claim Window and Photographic Documentation Protocol Before Opening Equipment Crates

The single most critical unboxing mistake is proceeding with installation before documenting shipping damage — any unverified discrepancy discovered after unpacking becomes the installer's liability, not the carrier's, because the damage claim window (typically 7 days from delivery) has closed. Before opening any crate or removing protective packaging, the exterior of all shipping containers must be photographed from a minimum of four angles (top, front, rear, side) with date and time stamps visible, and any visible damage (dents, punctures, water staining, crushed corners) must be recorded in writing with measurements of damage dimensions.

The damage claim window begins on the delivery date and typically closes 7 days after receipt. If damage is discovered after this window closes, the carrier will deny the claim and the facility assumes the cost of repair or replacement. Therefore, the unpacking sequence must prioritize damage documentation before any protective packaging is removed.

Procedure: Systematic Unpacking Sequence with Model Verification, Serial Number Matching, and Hardware Completeness Confirmation

Execute the unpacking procedure in the following strict sequence: (1) photograph all four sides of each shipping crate with date/time stamp visible; (2) open the crate and photograph the interior packaging configuration before removing any protective materials; (3) verify the model number on the equipment nameplate matches the purchase order and delivery note; (4) verify the serial number on the equipment nameplate matches the delivery documentation and record it in the installation file; (5) inspect the equipment exterior for damage (dents, scratches, water damage, paint chips) and photograph any discrepancies; (6) verify panel integrity by checking that all access panels are present, undamaged, and properly fastened; (7) verify hardware completeness by checking that all bolts, gaskets, mounting brackets, and accessories listed on the packing slip are present and undamaged.

Acceptance of the unpacking inspection requires that (1) all four exterior crate photographs are retained in the installation file with date/time stamps; (2) model number and serial number match the purchase order and delivery documentation exactly; (3) all equipment surfaces are inspected for damage and any discrepancies are photographed and documented; (4) all hardware items listed on the packing slip are present and undamaged; (5) if any damage or discrepancy is identified, a damage claim is filed with the carrier within the 7-day window with photographic evidence attached. The unpacking inspection report must be signed by the receiving technician and the site supervisor, with date and time recorded. 4. Gasket Material Compatibility Assessment and Seal Protection Protocol — Elastomer Preservation During Installation and Finishing Work

This section protects the equipment's sealing system from the most common post-installation failure mode: solvent-based cleaning agents applied by facility cleaning crews that degrade EPDM and silicone seals within weeks of installation.

Prerequisite: Identifying Seal Material Composition and Verifying Cleaning Agent Compatibility Before Installation Begins

Mobile-fogging-disinfectors gaskets are typically manufactured from EPDM (ethylene propylene diene monomer) or silicone elastomer, each with distinct chemical compatibility profiles. EPDM seals are incompatible with petroleum-based solvents (mineral spirits, naphtha, kerosene) and will experience rapid compression set degradation if exposed to these agents. Silicone seals are sensitive to strong acids (pH <3) and strong bases (pH >11) and will swell or soften if exposed to these conditions. Before installation begins, the facility's cleaning and disinfection protocols must be reviewed to identify all cleaning agents that will be used in the equipment vicinity, and the seal material compatibility must be verified against this agent list.

The facility maintenance team must provide a complete list of all cleaning agents used in the area where mobile-fogging-disinfectors will be installed, including product names, chemical composition, and application method (spray, wipe, immersion). This list must be cross-referenced against the seal material compatibility data provided by the equipment manufacturer.

Procedure: Seal Protection During Installation and Finishing Work — Masking Protocol and Post-Installation Verification

Execute the seal protection procedure as follows: (1) immediately after equipment positioning, cover all seal grooves and exposed elastomer surfaces with painter's masking tape (minimum 50 mm width) to create a protective barrier; (2) during all grinding, welding, or cutting operations near the equipment, maintain the masking tape coverage to prevent metal particles and heat damage from contacting the seals; (3) during facility finishing work (painting, solvent-based cleaning, disinfection), maintain masking tape coverage on all seal surfaces; (4) immediately after all finishing work is complete, remove the protective masking tape by peeling slowly at a 45-degree angle to avoid tearing the seal surface; (5) inspect all seal surfaces for damage, discoloration, or swelling and photograph any abnormalities; (6) verify that no solvent residue or cleaning agent has contacted the seal surfaces by wiping with a clean, dry cloth and inspecting for discoloration.

Seal Material Operating Temperature Range Incompatible Agents Compatible Cleaning Agents Storage Conditions
EPDM −30°C to +80°C Petroleum solvents, mineral oils, strong oxidizers Water, mild detergent, isopropyl alcohol (brief contact only) Flat storage, 40–60% RH, away from UV and ozone
Silicone −60°C to +200°C Strong acids (pH <3), strong bases (pH >11), some silicone-based oils Water, mild detergent, neutral pH solutions Flat storage, 40–60% RH, away from UV and ozone

Acceptance: All Seal Surfaces Protected During Installation and Verified Undamaged After Finishing Work — Documented with Photographic Evidence and Compatibility Verification

Acceptance of the seal protection protocol requires that (1) masking tape coverage is maintained on all seal surfaces from equipment positioning through completion of all finishing work; (2) after masking tape removal, all seal surfaces are visually inspected for damage, discoloration, swelling, or chemical attack and photographed; (3) the facility's cleaning agent list is reviewed and confirmed compatible with the seal material composition; (4) facility maintenance personnel are instructed in writing that only compatible cleaning agents may be used in the equipment vicinity; (5) a seal protection verification report is signed by the installation technician and the site supervisor, with date and time recorded. If any seal damage is discovered during post-finishing inspection, the affected seal must be replaced before commissioning begins, and the damage cause must be documented in the punch list.

5. Mechanical Anchoring and Fastener Torque Verification — Structural Stability and Load Path Confirmation

This section ensures that mobile-fogging-disinfectors is mechanically secured to the foundation with documented torque values and load path verification to prevent equipment movement or vibration during operation.

Prerequisite: Confirming Anchor Embedment Depth and Verifying Concrete Strength Before Fastener Installation

Before any fasteners are installed, all structural anchors must be verified to be installed at the specified locations with correct embedment depth. Expansion anchors (typically M12 or M16 diameter) must be embedded to a minimum depth of 60 mm in concrete with minimum compressive strength of 25 MPa (verified by concrete test report or core sampling if concrete age is less than 28 days). If the foundation survey identified low spots exceeding 3 mm, these areas must be filled with epoxy grout and allowed to cure for the manufacturer's specified time (typically 24 hours) before anchor installation begins.

The concrete strength must be verified by reviewing the concrete test report provided by the concrete supplier or by performing core sampling if the concrete age is uncertain. Anchor embedment depth must be verified by measuring from the concrete surface to the anchor bolt head using a depth gauge or measuring tape.

Procedure: Anchor Installation and Torque Sequence — Cross-Pattern Fastening at Specified Torque Values with Calibrated Torque Wrench

Execute the anchor installation procedure as follows: (1) install all expansion anchors at the specified locations using a hammer drill with the correct bit diameter and drilling depth (typically 70–80 mm for M12 anchors); (2) clean all drilled holes with compressed air to remove concrete dust; (3) insert expansion anchors and hand-tighten the anchor bolts until snug (no gap between equipment base and foundation); (4) using a calibrated click-type torque wrench with ±5% accuracy, torque all anchor bolts in a cross-pattern sequence (diagonal opposite corners first, then remaining anchors) to the specified torque value (typically 80 Nm for M12 anchors); (5) after all anchors are torqued, verify that no gaps exist between the equipment base and foundation by attempting to insert a 0.5 mm feeler gauge at multiple points around the perimeter; (6) mark each torqued fastener with a paint pen or torque stripe to provide visual evidence of torque application and to detect any fastener rotation during operation.

M16 expansion anchor ≥25 MPa 70–80 mm minimum 120 Nm ±5% (calibrated click-type) Feeler gauge test (0.5 mm) at 4 points; paint stripe mark

Acceptance: All Anchors Torqued to Specification in Cross-Pattern Sequence with Zero Foundation Gaps — Verified by Feeler Gauge Test and Torque Stripe Documentation

Acceptance of the mechanical anchoring procedure requires that (1) all expansion anchors are installed at the specified locations with embedment depth verified by measurement; (2) all anchor bolts are torqued to the specified value (80 Nm for M12, 120 Nm for M16) using a calibrated torque wrench with ±5% accuracy; (3) the torque sequence follows a cross-pattern (diagonal opposite corners first) to ensure even load distribution; (4) after torquing, a 0.5 mm feeler gauge cannot be inserted between the equipment base and foundation at any of four test points around the perimeter; (5) each torqued fastener is marked with a paint stripe or torque stripe to provide visual evidence of torque application; (6) the anchor installation report is signed by the installation technician with date, torque wrench calibration date, and torque stripe photographs attached. Any fastener that cannot be torqued to specification due to stripped threads or damaged anchor must be documented in the punch list and replaced before commissioning begins.

6. Pre-Commissioning Punch List Closure and Installation Quality Verification — Defect Documentation and Formal Sign-Off Protocol

This section establishes the formal quality gate that separates installation completion from commissioning validation, with structured defect documentation and dual sign-off requirements.

Prerequisite: Establishing Punch List Database Format and Severity Classification Criteria Before Installation Completion

Before commissioning validation begins, all installation defects must be formally documented in a structured punch list database with severity classification (critical/major/minor), responsible party assignment, target resolution date, and resolution evidence. The punch list serves as the installation quality record and establishes clear liability boundaries during the warranty period. A critical defect is one that prevents commissioning (e.g., unanchored equipment, missing electrical connections, damaged seals). A major defect affects equipment performance (e.g., misaligned door, incorrect pressure setting, loose fasteners). A minor defect is cosmetic or functional but does not affect safety or performance (e.g., scratched stainless steel surface, missing paint touch-up).

The punch list must be maintained in a structured format (spreadsheet or database) with the following fields: item number, location, description, severity classification, responsible party, target resolution date, resolution date, resolution evidence (photograph or test report), and sign-off date.

Procedure: Comprehensive Pre-Commissioning Inspection and Punch List Completion — Systematic Verification of All Installation Components

Execute the pre-commissioning inspection procedure as follows: (1) verify that all mechanical fixings (bolts, fasteners, anchors) are torqued and marked with paint stripe or torque stripe; (2) verify that all electrical connections are tight and properly labeled according to the wiring diagram; (3) verify that all seals are inspected and undamaged, with no evidence of chemical attack or compression set; (4) verify that all equipment surfaces are cleaned and protected from damage; (5) verify that all documentation (test reports, calibration certificates, material certificates) is complete and filed; (6) perform a visual walk-through inspection of the entire installation and document any defects observed; (7) for each defect identified, create a punch list entry with severity classification, responsible party, and target resolution date; (8) photograph all defects and attach photographs to the punch list entry.

Acceptance of the pre-commissioning punch list closure requires that (1) all critical defects are resolved before commissioning validation begins, with resolution evidence (photographs, test reports, replacement documentation) attached to the punch list entry; (2) all major defects are resolved within the target date, with resolution evidence documented; (3) all minor defects are resolved within the target date or formally accepted as deferred maintenance with written justification; (4) each resolved punch list item is signed off by the installation technician and the site supervisor with date and time recorded; (5) the complete punch list (including all resolved items and resolution evidence) is reviewed and accepted by the commissioning engineer before pre-start commissioning validation begins; (6) the punch list and all resolution records are retained in the equipment file for a minimum of 10 years, linked to the equipment serial number and installation date. Facilities that skip formal punch list closure accept unquantified installation quality risk that no downstream commissioning validation can fully uncover.
