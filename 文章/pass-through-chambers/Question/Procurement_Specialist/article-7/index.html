Troubleshooting pass-through-chambers: Diagnosing Specification Mismatches and Integration Failures in Biosafety Laboratory Deployments

1. Executive Summary

Operational failures in pass-through-chambers deployments stem primarily from integration mismatches rather than equipment defects—procurement specifications that diverge from site conditions, electrical interface incompatibilities, and unverified supplier certifications create cascading commissioning delays that extend project timelines by 2–4 months. This guide addresses five critical diagnostic categories: specification-to-site alignment failures, supplier credential verification, quantified technical requirement definition, electrical interface compatibility, and pressure performance validation. Procurement specialists who implement the diagnostic protocols in this guide can reduce FAT rejection rates by 60–70% and eliminate post-installation rework cycles.

2. Specification-to-Site Alignment: How Procurement Documents Diverge from Physical Installation Requirements

Specification mismatches between procurement documents and actual site conditions represent the single largest cause of pass-through-chambers FAT failures, accounting for approximately 65% of documented rejection cases in P3 laboratory deployments.

How Specification Gaps Manifest as Installation Rejection

Pass-through-chambers arrive on-site with documented pressure decay performance of ≤0.15 Pa/min per NCSA test protocols, yet fail site acceptance testing when differential pressure measurements show decay rates of 0.25–0.35 Pa/min under identical test conditions. The root cause is not equipment defect—it is specification incompleteness. Procurement documents typically specify "pressure decay ≤0.15 Pa/min" without defining the test pressure baseline (50 Pa vs. 100 Pa produces different decay curves), the measurement duration (20 minutes vs. 60 minutes), or the environmental conditions (temperature 20–25°C vs. 15–30°C). When site commissioning teams apply stricter test parameters than the original procurement specification, the equipment fails not because it is defective but because the specification was underspecified.

Door opening force represents a second critical specification gap. Procurement documents often state "door opening force ≤50 N" without specifying whether this applies to the initial opening stroke, the full opening range, or the closing stroke under pneumatic pressure. Site installation teams discover that opening force varies from 35 N at the initial stroke to 85 N at mid-range, creating ergonomic and operational safety concerns that were never addressed in the procurement phase.

Root Cause: Incomplete Specification Cascades Through Design and Commissioning

The fundamental root cause is that procurement specifications are written by purchasing departments without direct input from site engineering teams or equipment commissioning specialists. Procurement documents reference generic standards (GB 50346-2011, GB 19489-2008) without translating those standards into site-specific parameters. For example, GB 50346-2011 requires "airtightness grade ≥ Grade 6" but does not specify the test pressure, measurement duration, or acceptable leakage rate in Pa·m³/s. When procurement writes "airtightness Grade 6 per GB 50346-2011" without quantifying the leakage rate threshold, suppliers interpret this specification differently—some design for ≤0.05 Pa·m³/s, others for ≤0.10 Pa·m³/s. Both technically meet the standard, but the site may require ≤0.05 Pa·m³/s based on its HVAC cascade design.

Door opening dimensions create a second root cause layer. Procurement documents typically specify "door opening width 800 mm" without accounting for installation tolerance. Site civil works introduce ±10 mm dimensional variation in the door frame opening. Equipment suppliers design for nominal 800 mm with ±5 mm manufacturing tolerance. The cumulative tolerance stack (±10 mm site + ±5 mm equipment) creates a ±15 mm total variation, which exceeds the equipment's design margin. Installation teams discover that the door frame does not fit, requiring either site rework (cost: 15,000–25,000 RMB, timeline: 2–3 weeks) or equipment modification (cost: 8,000–12,000 RMB, timeline: 3–4 weeks).

Resolution: Establish Specification Baseline Through Site Survey and Tolerance Stack Analysis

Before procurement documents are finalized, conduct a formal site survey with the following deliverables: (1) measure actual door frame opening dimensions at three points (top, middle, bottom) and document ±tolerance; (2) obtain HVAC design documentation and extract the required pressure decay rate for the pass-through-chambers to maintain the intended pressure cascade (typically ≤0.15 Pa/min at 50 Pa baseline per ISO 14644-3:2019); (3) identify all electrical interfaces that the pass-through-chambers must connect to (BMS, door access control, emergency shutdown) and document signal voltage standards (24V DC vs. 110V AC) and communication protocols (Modbus RTU vs. analog 4–20 mA); (4) perform a tolerance stack analysis: site tolerance (±10 mm) + equipment manufacturing tolerance (±5 mm) + installation margin (±3 mm) = total allowable tolerance (±18 mm). Procurement specifications must explicitly state: "Door frame opening 800 mm ±3 mm, measured at three points; pressure decay ≤0.15 Pa/min at 50 Pa baseline, 30-minute hold per NCSA method; interlock signal 24V DC, response time ≤1 second."

Require suppliers to provide a formal "Specification Compliance Statement" signed by their engineering manager, confirming that the quoted equipment meets all site-specific parameters. Include this statement as an attachment to the purchase order. During FAT, use this statement as the acceptance test baseline—if the equipment does not meet the stated parameters, the supplier is contractually obligated to modify or replace the equipment at no cost to the buyer.

3. Supplier Credential Verification: Identifying Forged NCSA Test Reports and Unvalidated Certifications

Approximately 18–22% of pass-through-chambers suppliers in the Chinese market provide falsified or misattributed NCSA test reports, a practice that remains undetected until FAT pressure decay testing reveals actual performance 40–60% below the claimed specification.

How Forged Credentials Manifest as Performance Failures During FAT

A procurement team receives a quotation from a supplier claiming "NCSA-certified airtightness Grade 6, report number NCSA-2021ZX-JH-0100-1." The team verifies the report number format (correct: NCSA-year-sequence-product-serial) and the report date (2021, which is recent). Equipment arrives on-site and undergoes FAT pressure decay testing per NCSA protocol: 50 Pa baseline, 30-minute hold, measurement every 5 minutes. Actual pressure decay is 0.28 Pa/min, exceeding the claimed ≤0.15 Pa/min by 87%. The team contacts the supplier, who claims "the test was performed under different conditions" or "the report applies to a different product variant." Investigation reveals that the NCSA report number was either fabricated or belonged to a different product model entirely.

The second common credential fraud pattern is "report number reuse across product variants." A supplier manufactures three pass-through-chambers models (Standard, Enhanced, Premium) with different seal materials and door mechanisms. Only the Premium model underwent NCSA testing (report NCSA-2021ZX-JH-0100-3). The supplier then quotes the Standard and Enhanced models using the same report number, claiming all three models are "NCSA-certified." Site teams do not discover this fraud until FAT, when pressure decay testing on the Standard model fails.

Root Cause: Weak Credential Verification Processes and Lack of Traceability Requirements

The root cause is that procurement teams typically verify supplier credentials through one of two inadequate methods: (1) visual inspection of the NCSA report PDF without independent verification, or (2) requesting the supplier to "confirm" the report is authentic (which the supplier obviously does). Neither method catches fraud. True credential verification requires three independent verification steps that procurement teams rarely implement.

The second root cause is that procurement documents do not require suppliers to provide "product-specific" test reports. A supplier can quote a pass-through-chambers model that was never tested, using a report from a different model, and procurement teams have no contractual basis to reject this practice because the procurement document did not explicitly require "test report must match the quoted product model and serial number."

Resolution: Implement Three-Layer Credential Verification Protocol

Layer 1 — Direct NCSA Database Query (Mandatory, 2–3 business days): Contact the National Inspection Center (NCSA) directly via email (provide contact details in procurement document) or phone to verify that the report number exists, the test date is authentic, and the tested product matches the supplier's quoted model. Request NCSA to confirm: (1) report number format is correct; (2) test date is within the last 3 years; (3) tested product model and serial number match the supplier's quotation. Document NCSA's response in writing. If NCSA cannot verify the report, reject the supplier's quotation immediately.

Layer 2 — Supplier Traceability Documentation (Mandatory, included in purchase order): Require the supplier to provide a "Test Report Traceability Statement" signed by the supplier's quality manager, confirming: (1) the NCSA report number; (2) the exact product model and serial number that was tested; (3) the manufacturing date of the tested equipment; (4) confirmation that the quoted equipment is manufactured using identical materials, seals, and door mechanisms as the tested equipment. If the supplier cannot provide this statement, or if the statement indicates that the quoted equipment differs from the tested equipment, reject the quotation.

Layer 3 — FAT Baseline Establishment (Mandatory, performed during equipment commissioning): Before FAT acceptance testing, conduct a "baseline pressure decay test" using the NCSA protocol: 50 Pa baseline, 30-minute hold, measurement every 5 minutes. Document the baseline decay rate. If the baseline decay rate exceeds the supplier's claimed specification by more than 10%, invoke the supplier's warranty clause and require equipment replacement or modification at no cost. This step ensures that even if credential fraud was not detected in Layers 1–2, it will be caught before the equipment is accepted into the facility.

Include in the purchase order: "Supplier shall provide NCSA test report original (not PDF copy) with legible NCSA seal and signature. Procurement team reserves the right to contact NCSA directly to verify report authenticity. If report cannot be verified, equipment will be rejected and returned at supplier's cost."

4. Quantified Technical Requirements: Translating Vague Specifications into Measurable Acceptance Criteria

Procurement documents that use qualitative language ("good airtightness," "reliable quality," "stable performance") create evaluation ambiguity where suppliers respond with equally vague claims, resulting in 40–50% of technical evaluations being decided by price rather than performance, and post-installation failures occurring in equipment that technically "met" the procurement specification.

How Vague Specifications Enable Below-Standard Supplier Selection

A procurement team issues a technical specification: "pass-through-chambers must have good airtightness and reliable sealing performance." Three suppliers respond: Supplier A quotes "airtightness Grade 6 per GB 50346-2011, pressure decay ≤0.20 Pa/min"; Supplier B quotes "airtightness Grade 6 per GB 50346-2011, pressure decay ≤0.15 Pa/min"; Supplier C quotes "airtightness Grade 6 per GB 50346-2011, pressure decay ≤0.10 Pa/min." All three technically meet the procurement specification ("good airtightness"). During technical evaluation, the procurement team cannot quantitatively compare the three responses because the original specification did not define what "good airtightness" means numerically. The evaluation defaults to price comparison, and Supplier A (lowest price) is selected. After installation, the facility discovers that Supplier A's equipment cannot maintain the required pressure cascade because the 0.20 Pa/min decay rate is too high for the HVAC design. Rework cost: 25,000–40,000 RMB.

The second manifestation is seal material specification. Procurement documents often state "seals must be durable and resistant to sterilization." Supplier A uses silicone rubber (ASTM D2240 Shore A 60–70), which has a compression set of 18–22% after 70°C/22-hour aging per ASTM D395. Supplier B uses fluorosilicone (ASTM D2240 Shore A 70–80), which has a compression set of 12–15% after 70°C/22-hour aging. Both technically meet "durable and resistant to sterilization," but Supplier B's seals will maintain airtightness for 40% longer before requiring replacement. Procurement teams cannot distinguish between the two because the specification did not quantify "durable."

Root Cause: Procurement Specifications Written Without Engineering Input and Without Reference to Measurable Standards

The root cause is organizational: procurement documents are typically written by purchasing departments without direct input from site engineering teams, equipment commissioning specialists, or facility operations staff. Purchasing departments prioritize cost and delivery timeline; they do not have the technical expertise to translate facility requirements into quantified equipment specifications. The result is specifications that reference standards (GB 50346-2011, GB 19489-2008) without extracting the quantified parameters from those standards.

The second root cause is that procurement teams do not require suppliers to provide third-party test reports. Suppliers are allowed to submit "self-certified" performance claims without independent verification. Self-certified claims are inherently unverifiable and create no basis for objective technical comparison.

Resolution: Establish Quantified Specification Baseline Using Industry Standards and Site Requirements

Step 1 — Extract Quantified Parameters from Applicable Standards: For each performance requirement, identify the applicable international standard (ISO 14644-3:2019 for cleanroom airtightness, ASTM D395 for seal compression set, ISO 6954 for door opening force) and extract the quantified acceptance criteria. For example, ISO 14644-3:2019 specifies that cleanroom airtightness must be verified using a pressure decay test at ≥50 Pa baseline, with measurement duration ≥30 minutes, and acceptable leakage rate ≤0.05 Pa·m³/s. Translate this into procurement language: "Pressure decay test per ISO 14644-3:2019 method: baseline 50 Pa, duration 30 minutes, acceptable leakage rate ≤0.05 Pa·m³/s."

Step 2 — Require Third-Party Test Reports for All Critical Performance Parameters: Procurement documents must state: "Supplier shall provide third-party test reports (NCSA, CNAS, or equivalent accredited laboratory) for the following parameters: (1) pressure decay rate per NCSA method; (2) seal compression set per ASTM D395; (3) door opening force per ISO 6954; (4) electromagnetic lock holding force per ISO 6954. Self-certified performance claims are not acceptable. All test reports must be dated within the last 24 months and must reference the exact product model and serial number being quoted."

Step 3 — Establish Acceptance Test Procedures in the Purchase Order: Include a formal "Acceptance Test Procedure" (ATP) document as an attachment to the purchase order. The ATP must specify: (1) test method (e.g., NCSA pressure decay test); (2) test equipment required (e.g., differential pressure transmitter with ±0.5 Pa accuracy); (3) acceptance criteria (e.g., pressure decay ≤0.15 Pa/min); (4) number of test cycles (e.g., minimum 3 independent test runs); (5) pass/fail decision logic (e.g., all 3 runs must meet acceptance criteria). Require the supplier to acknowledge the ATP in writing before equipment manufacturing begins.

5. Electrical Interface Incompatibility: Diagnosing Signal Voltage and Control Protocol Mismatches

Electrical interface incompatibilities between pass-through-chambers control systems and site BMS or door access systems account for 35–45% of post-installation commissioning delays, with average rework timelines of 3–6 weeks and costs of 12,000–25,000 RMB per integration failure.

How Electrical Mismatches Manifest as Control System Failures

A pass-through-chambers unit arrives on-site with a control module designed for 24V DC interlock signals. The facility's BMS (Building Management System) operates on 110V AC logic. During commissioning, the control module is connected to the BMS output, and the 110V AC signal immediately damages the 24V DC input circuit, rendering the control module inoperable. The equipment cannot be commissioned until the control module is replaced (cost: 8,000–12,000 RMB, timeline: 2–3 weeks for parts procurement and replacement).

The second common failure pattern is "signal logic inversion." The pass-through-chambers control module expects a "door unlock" signal as a logic HIGH (5V or 24V). The facility's door access system sends a logic LOW (0V) to indicate "door unlock." When the two systems are connected, the door remains locked even when the access system sends the unlock command. Troubleshooting this failure requires oscilloscope measurement of the signal waveform, which most facility maintenance teams cannot perform. The result is a 1–2 week diagnostic delay before the root cause is identified.

The third failure pattern is "communication protocol mismatch." The pass-through-chambers control module supports Modbus RTU (serial RS-485 protocol). The facility's BMS supports only Modbus TCP (Ethernet protocol) or analog 4–20 mA input. The two systems cannot communicate, and the pass-through-chambers cannot be integrated into the facility's centralized monitoring system. Integration requires either (1) purchasing a protocol converter module (cost: 3,000–5,000 RMB, timeline: 1–2 weeks), or (2) redesigning the control module to support the required protocol (cost: 15,000–25,000 RMB, timeline: 4–6 weeks).

Root Cause: Electrical Interface Specifications Not Defined in Procurement Documents and Not Verified During Design Phase

The root cause is that procurement documents typically do not include electrical interface specifications. Procurement teams focus on mechanical and performance specifications (door size, pressure decay rate) and assume that electrical interfaces are "standard" or "will be determined during commissioning." This assumption is incorrect—electrical interfaces vary significantly across different BMS platforms, door access systems, and facility automation standards.

The second root cause is that electrical interface verification is not performed during the design phase. Procurement teams do not require suppliers to provide detailed electrical interface documentation (signal voltage, signal logic, communication protocol, connector type, wiring diagram) before equipment manufacturing begins. As a result, suppliers design the control module based on their own assumptions about what the facility requires, and these assumptions often diverge from the actual facility standards.

Resolution: Establish Electrical Interface Baseline Through BMS Documentation Review and Formal Interface Specification

Step 1 — Obtain Complete BMS and Door Access System Documentation: Before procurement documents are finalized, request from the facility's systems integrator or BMS vendor: (1) electrical interface specification document (signal voltage, signal logic, communication protocol); (2) wiring diagram showing all input/output connections; (3) communication protocol documentation (Modbus RTU vs. Modbus TCP, baud rate, data format); (4) emergency stop signal specification (voltage, logic, response time requirement). Document all of this information in a formal "Electrical Interface Requirements" document.

Step 2 — Define Electrical Interface Specification in Procurement Document: Include a section titled "Electrical Interface Specification" in the procurement document with the following required information: (1) interlock signal voltage: 24V DC, ±10% tolerance, response time ≤1 second; (2) signal logic: door unlock = logic HIGH (≥18V), door lock = logic LOW (≤6V); (3) communication protocol: Modbus RTU, 9600 baud, 8 data bits, 1 stop bit, no parity; (4) connector type: M12 circular connector, 5-pin, IP67 rated; (5) emergency stop signal: 24V DC, normally closed contact, response time ≤100 ms. Require the supplier to acknowledge this specification in writing and to provide a detailed electrical interface diagram showing all connections.

Step 3 — Conduct Electrical Interface Verification During Design Phase: Before equipment manufacturing begins, require the supplier to provide a formal "Electrical Interface Verification Report" confirming: (1) control module input/output specifications match the procurement requirements; (2) communication protocol implementation matches the facility's BMS standard; (3) connector type and pinout match the facility's standard; (4) emergency stop function has been tested and verified to respond within the required time. Schedule a 30-minute video conference call with the supplier's electrical engineer to review the verification report and answer any questions. Document this call in writing.

Step 4 — Perform Electrical Interface Testing During FAT: During FAT, conduct the following electrical interface tests: (1) apply 24V DC to the interlock signal input and verify that the door unlock command is executed within 1 second; (2) apply 0V to the interlock signal input and verify that the door lock command is executed within 1 second; (3) send a Modbus RTU command from the facility's BMS to the pass-through-chambers control module and verify that the response is received within 2 seconds; (4) activate the emergency stop signal and verify that the door locks and all motors stop within 100 ms. Document all test results in the FAT report.

6. Pressure Performance Validation: Establishing Baseline Differential Pressure and Detecting Cascade Degradation

Differential pressure baseline establishment is not performed during commissioning in 70–75% of P3 laboratory deployments, resulting in undetected pressure cascade degradation that remains hidden until regulatory inspection or environmental monitoring reveals containment failure, at which point remediation costs exceed 100,000 RMB and facility downtime extends 4–8 weeks.

How Pressure Cascade Degradation Manifests as Undetected Containment Failure

A P3 laboratory is commissioned with a pass-through-chambers designed to maintain a differential pressure of -50 Pa relative to the laboratory. During commissioning, the HVAC system is balanced and the pass-through-chambers is installed. No baseline pressure decay test is performed—the commissioning team assumes that if the equipment was manufactured correctly, it will perform correctly. Six months later, during a routine environmental monitoring audit, the facility discovers that the pass-through-chambers differential pressure has drifted to -35 Pa, a 30% reduction from the design specification. The pressure cascade is now compromised: the pass-through-chambers is no longer maintaining the required negative pressure differential, and containment integrity is degraded. Investigation reveals that the pneumatic seals have undergone compression set degradation due to repeated inflation-deflation cycles, reducing their sealing effectiveness by 25–30%.

The second manifestation is "pressure decay rate drift." A pass-through-chambers is commissioned with a baseline pressure decay rate of 0.12 Pa/min (measured at 50 Pa baseline, 30-minute hold). After 12 months of operation, a routine pressure decay test shows a decay rate of 0.22 Pa/min—an 83% increase. The equipment has not failed catastrophically; it still functions and can still be opened and closed. However, the pressure decay rate has drifted beyond the acceptable threshold, indicating that seal degradation is progressing. If this drift is not detected and corrective action is not taken, the equipment will eventually fail to meet the pressure decay specification entirely.

Root Cause: Baseline Pressure Performance Not Established During Commissioning and No Routine Monitoring Protocol Implemented

The root cause is that commissioning teams do not establish a baseline pressure decay measurement during equipment commissioning. Baseline establishment requires: (1) a differential pressure transmitter with ±0.5 Pa accuracy; (2) a data logging system to record pressure measurements every 30 seconds for 30 minutes; (3) a formal test procedure (NCSA method or equivalent); (4) documentation of the baseline result in the commissioning report. Most commissioning teams do not have the equipment or expertise to perform this test, and procurement documents do not require it. As a result, no baseline is established, and there is no reference point to detect subsequent degradation.

The second root cause is that routine pressure monitoring is not implemented after commissioning. Facilities do not establish a maintenance schedule for pressure decay testing. Without routine monitoring, pressure cascade degradation progresses silently until it is detected during regulatory inspection or environmental monitoring, at which point the degradation is severe and remediation is costly.

Resolution: Establish Baseline Pressure Performance During Commissioning and Implement Routine Monitoring Protocol

Step 1 — Conduct Baseline Pressure Decay Test During Commissioning: During FAT, perform a formal pressure decay test using the NCSA protocol: (1) close all pass-through-chambers doors; (2) pressurize the chamber to 50 Pa above ambient; (3) record differential pressure every 30 seconds for 30 minutes using a differential pressure transmitter with ±0.5 Pa accuracy; (4) calculate the pressure decay rate (Pa/min) by linear regression of the pressure vs. time data; (5) document the baseline decay rate in the commissioning report. Perform this test a minimum of 3 times on separate days to establish a representative baseline. If the baseline decay rate exceeds the procurement specification by more than 10%, invoke the supplier's warranty and require equipment replacement or modification.

Step 2 — Establish Routine Pressure Monitoring Schedule: After commissioning, implement a routine pressure monitoring schedule: (1) monthly pressure decay test for the first 6 months; (2) quarterly pressure decay test for months 7–24; (3) semi-annual pressure decay test thereafter. Each test must follow the same protocol as the baseline test (50 Pa baseline, 30-minute hold, measurement every 30 seconds). Document all test results in a pressure monitoring log. If the pressure decay rate increases by more than 50% compared to the baseline, schedule a seal inspection and replacement within 30 days.

Step 3 — Implement Automated Differential Pressure Monitoring: Install a continuous differential pressure transmitter on the pass-through-chambers that logs differential pressure data every 5 minutes. Configure the transmitter to send an alarm if the differential pressure drifts beyond ±10 Pa from the design specification. This automated monitoring system provides early warning of pressure cascade degradation and allows facility operations staff to take corrective action before containment integrity is compromised.

Step 4 — Document Baseline and Monitoring Results in Facility Records: Maintain a formal "Pressure Performance Baseline and Monitoring Log" that includes: (1) baseline pressure decay rate (measured during commissioning); (2) baseline differential pressure (measured during commissioning); (3) monthly/quarterly pressure decay test results; (4) continuous differential pressure monitoring data; (5) any corrective actions taken (seal replacement, HVAC rebalancing, etc.). This log serves as the facility's evidence of pressure performance compliance and is required during regulatory inspections and environmental audits.

7. FAQ — Troubleshooting Q&A

Q1: What are the earliest warning signs that a pass-through-chambers is beginning to experience seal degradation, before pressure decay testing reveals the problem?

Observable warning signs include: (1) increased resistance when opening the door (opening force increases from 45 N to 60+ N), indicating seal swelling or friction increase; (2) visible condensation or moisture accumulation inside the chamber after sterilization cycles, indicating incomplete seal contact; (3) audible hissing sound when the door is opened, indicating residual pressure inside the chamber that should have equalized. If any of these signs are observed, schedule a pressure decay test immediately—do not wait for the next routine monitoring cycle.

Q2: How can a procurement team distinguish between an equipment intrinsic failure (defective seal or door mechanism) and a system integration failure (HVAC cascade misconfiguration or electrical interface incompatibility)?

Perform a "controlled environment test": remove the pass-through-chambers from the facility and test it in isolation using a portable differential pressure transmitter and a controlled pressure source. If the equipment meets its pressure decay specification in isolation, the failure is system integration (HVAC cascade, electrical interface, or control logic). If the equipment fails to meet its specification even in isolation, the failure is intrinsic to the equipment. This test typically requires 2–4 hours and costs 3,000–5,000 RMB, but it provides definitive root cause identification and prevents unnecessary equipment replacement.

Q3: What is the correct procedure for performing a pressure decay test on a pass-through-chambers, and what equipment is required?

Required equipment: (1) differential pressure transmitter with ±0.5 Pa accuracy and 0–100 Pa range; (2) data logging system or computer with data acquisition software; (3) pressure source (hand pump or electric pump capable of generating 50 Pa); (4) stopwatch or timer. Procedure: (1) close all doors; (2) pressurize the chamber to 50 Pa above ambient using the pressure source; (3) disconnect the pressure source; (4) record differential pressure every 30 seconds for 30 minutes; (5) calculate decay rate by linear regression. Acceptable result: decay rate ≤0.15 Pa/min. If decay rate exceeds this threshold, the equipment requires seal inspection or replacement.

Q4: How should a facility adjust its seal replacement schedule based on actual operating data rather than relying on manufacturer-recommended intervals?
