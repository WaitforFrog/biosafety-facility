vhp-generators: Regulatory Compliance Framework and Standards Requirements for Biosafety Equipment Certification

1. Executive Summary

Vaporized hydrogen peroxide (VHP) generators used in biosafety laboratory pass boxes and containment systems must satisfy integrated regulatory requirements spanning electrical safety (IEC 60601-1), risk management (ISO 14971), device identification (FDA 21 CFR Part 830), air quality standards (ISO 14644-1), and jurisdiction-specific registration pathways (NMPA, FDA, EU MDR). The regulatory compliance framework for vhp-generators is not a single standard but a convergent system of overlapping requirements that must be satisfied simultaneously during design, manufacturing, field validation, and post-market surveillance.

  • Electrical safety compliance under IEC 60601-1:2005+A1+A2 (third edition) requires manufacturers to identify "essential performance" functions—those whose failure creates unacceptable risk—and validate them through specific test protocols including pressure decay testing, leakage current measurement, and moisture preconditioning, with non-compliance resulting in regulatory rejection during NMPA/FDA/CE technical file review.

  • Risk management documentation aligned with ISO 14971:2019 must establish a closed-loop traceability chain from hazard identification through control implementation to residual risk evaluation, with specific attention to "reasonably foreseeable misuse" scenarios such as simultaneous door opening or pressure differential failure, ensuring that device instructions and labeling directly address identified failure modes.

  • Unique Device Identification (UDI) compliance under FDA 21 CFR Part 830 and NMPA regulations requires permanent direct marking of device identifiers on equipment, complete GUDID database submission with production identifiers, and documented traceability protocols that persist through field installation, maintenance, and decommissioning phases, with missing or incomplete UDI data creating audit findings during regulatory inspections.

2. Electrical Safety Classification and Essential Performance Identification Under IEC 60601-1:2005+A1+A2

The third edition of IEC 60601-1 [IEC 60601-1:2005+A1+A2] fundamentally restructured electrical safety requirements by introducing the "essential performance" (EP) concept—a function whose loss creates unacceptable risk to patient or operator safety—and this classification directly determines which test protocols apply to vhp-generators during regulatory submission. Manufacturers that fail to correctly identify essential performance functions during design phase typically discover this gap during regulatory audit, resulting in incomplete test data packages and delayed market approval.

IEC 60601-1 Third Edition: Essential Performance Classification and Test Protocol Mapping

The regulatory requirement under IEC 60601-1 Clause 3.2 defines essential performance as "performance of a medical device which is necessary to achieve the intended benefit and to prevent unacceptable risk." For vhp-generators, essential performance functions include: (1) pressure differential maintenance within specified range (±5 Pa typical), (2) hydrogen peroxide vapor concentration control (target 600–800 mg/m³), (3) cycle timing accuracy (±2% tolerance), and (4) alarm activation upon pressure loss or sensor failure. Non-essential functions—such as data logging or user interface responsiveness—do not require the same rigor of electrical safety testing.

Moisture Preconditioning and Leakage Current Testing: Regulatory Audit Focus Areas

The most frequently cited non-compliance finding in NMPA and FDA regulatory audits of vhp-generators is incomplete moisture preconditioning data. IEC 60601-1 Clause 8.3.4 requires that electrical safety tests (insulation resistance, dielectric strength, leakage current) be performed on samples that have been preconditioned at 25°C ± 2°C and 93% ± 3% relative humidity for 48 hours. Manufacturers that conduct these tests at ambient laboratory conditions (typically 20–25°C, 40–60% RH) without documented preconditioning produce test reports that regulatory reviewers classify as non-compliant, even if the actual test results pass. Leakage current limits under IEC 60601-1 Clause 8.2.3 specify: patient leakage current ≤100 µA (normal condition) and ≤500 µA (single-fault condition); operator leakage current ≤5 mA (normal) and ≤10 mA (single-fault). Vhp-generators with multiple electrical circuits (pump motor, heating element, control board) must demonstrate leakage current compliance for each circuit independently and in combination.

Non-Compliance Pathways and Regulatory Audit Deficiency Patterns

Regulatory audits of vhp-generator installations at GMP-registered facilities have identified three recurring deficiency patterns: (1) risk management documentation (ISO 14971 file) does not explicitly link essential performance functions to specific electrical safety test protocols, creating a gap between design intent and validation evidence; (2) third-party test reports (from NCSA, ICAS, or equivalent notified bodies) lack traceability to specific equipment serial numbers or manufacturing batches, making it impossible to verify that the tested sample matches the installed equipment; (3) post-installation electrical safety verification is not performed, leaving no documented evidence that the equipment maintains compliance after field assembly and connection to facility power systems. The consequence of these gaps is regulatory rejection of the technical file during NMPA pre-submission review or FDA 510(k) evaluation, requiring manufacturers to conduct additional testing and resubmit, extending market approval timelines by 6–12 months.

Compliance Roadmap: Five-Step Electrical Safety Validation Protocol

Manufacturers and facility validation teams must execute the following sequence to achieve electrical safety compliance: (1) Conduct design-phase essential performance analysis using ISO 14971 methodology, documenting which functions are essential and which are non-essential, with risk justification for each classification; (2) Develop electrical safety test plan that maps each essential performance function to specific IEC 60601-1 test protocols (pressure decay, leakage current, dielectric strength, moisture preconditioning), with acceptance criteria derived from applicable standards; (3) Engage third-party notified body (NCSA, ICAS, or equivalent) to conduct moisture-preconditioned electrical safety testing on representative samples, with test reports explicitly identifying equipment model, serial number, manufacturing date, and test conditions; (4) Establish field validation protocol (IQ/OQ) that includes post-installation electrical safety spot-checks (insulation resistance measurement, leakage current verification) to confirm that equipment maintains compliance after facility integration; (5) Maintain traceability documentation linking design specifications → risk management file → test protocols → test reports → field validation records, with this chain accessible during regulatory audit. Facilities that implement this five-step protocol and maintain complete documentation packages achieve NMPA/FDA/CE approval with minimal audit findings.

3. Risk Management Integration and Reasonably Foreseeable Misuse Under ISO 14971:2019

ISO 14971:2019 [ISO 14971:2019] expanded the definition of "reasonably foreseeable misuse" to include operator errors, environmental stressors, and maintenance failures that are not explicitly prohibited in device instructions, and vhp-generator manufacturers that do not establish closed-loop traceability between hazard identification and device labeling face regulatory rejection during CE MDR technical file review. The most common audit deficiency is a risk management file that identifies hazards (e.g., simultaneous door opening, pressure differential loss) but fails to demonstrate how device design, instructions, or labeling prevent or mitigate these hazards.

ISO 14971:2019 Hazard Identification and Reasonably Foreseeable Misuse Scenarios

ISO 14971 Clause 5.3 requires manufacturers to identify all hazards associated with the device, including those arising from "reasonably foreseeable misuse." For vhp-generators integrated into biosafety pass boxes, reasonably foreseeable misuse scenarios include: (1) operator opening both chamber doors simultaneously (despite interlock design), (2) facility maintenance personnel bypassing pressure differential alarms to expedite cycle completion, (3) installation of equipment in environments exceeding specified temperature/humidity ranges (e.g., outdoor temporary facilities), (4) use of hydrogen peroxide solutions exceeding the specified concentration (>35% H₂O₂), and (5) failure to perform required maintenance (pump seal replacement, catalyst cartridge change) within specified intervals. Each scenario must be documented in the risk management file with: hazard description, potential harm (severity classification: negligible, minor, serious, critical), probability of occurrence (rare, unlikely, possible, probable), and risk control measures (design changes, protective features, warnings, instructions).

Closed-Loop Traceability: From Hazard Identification to Device Labeling and Instructions

The regulatory requirement under ISO 14971 Clause 7.1 (Risk Control) and Clause 7.4 (Residual Risk Evaluation) mandates that every identified hazard must have a corresponding risk control measure, and every risk control measure must be reflected in device design, instructions, or labeling. Regulatory auditors verify this traceability by cross-referencing the risk management file against the device's Instructions for Use (IFU), labeling, and design specifications. A common audit deficiency occurs when the risk management file identifies "simultaneous door opening" as a hazard but the device IFU does not explicitly instruct operators on interlock function or consequences of bypass attempts. Similarly, if the risk file identifies "H₂O₂ concentration exceeding 35%" as a hazard but the device labeling does not specify the maximum allowable concentration or consequences of exceeding it, regulatory reviewers classify this as incomplete risk control. The consequence is a regulatory finding that requires manufacturers to revise device labeling, resubmit the technical file, and potentially conduct additional field validation to demonstrate that the revised labeling effectively communicates risk to end users.

Post-Market Surveillance and Adverse Event Reporting Integration

ISO 14971 Clause 8 (Production and Post-Production Information) requires manufacturers to establish processes for collecting, analyzing, and responding to adverse events and field failures that may indicate previously unidentified hazards or inadequate risk controls. For vhp-generators, post-market surveillance must include: (1) documented procedures for facility personnel to report equipment malfunctions, pressure differential losses, or alarm failures; (2) manufacturer analysis of reported events to determine whether they represent new hazards or indicate that existing risk controls are inadequate; (3) corrective action procedures (design changes, labeling updates, field recalls) if post-market data reveal unacceptable residual risk. Regulatory audits specifically examine whether manufacturers have established these post-market surveillance processes and whether they have documented responses to reported adverse events. Facilities that deploy vhp-generators must maintain records of all equipment malfunctions and report serious events (patient/operator harm, uncontrolled pathogen release) to the relevant regulatory authority (NMPA, FDA, EudaMed) within specified timeframes (typically 30 days for serious events).

Compliance Pathway: Risk Management File Structure and Regulatory Submission Requirements

Manufacturers must structure the risk management file according to ISO 14971 Annex A (informative) and ensure that the file includes: (1) Risk Management Plan (RMP) documenting the overall risk management strategy, responsibilities, and timelines; (2) Hazard Analysis table listing all identified hazards, severity/probability classifications, and risk control measures; (3) Risk Control Verification records (test reports, design reviews, field validation data) demonstrating that each risk control measure has been implemented and validated; (4) Residual Risk Evaluation documenting that residual risks are acceptable and that benefits outweigh risks; (5) Risk Management Review summary confirming that the risk management process has been completed and that no unacceptable risks remain. For NMPA registration, manufacturers must submit a standalone "Risk Management Report" (风险管理报告) as part of the technical file. For FDA 510(k) submissions, risk analysis documentation must be included in the predicate device comparison and substantial equivalence justification. For CE MDR submissions, the risk management file must be included in the technical documentation (Annex II) and must demonstrate compliance with MDR Annex I Chapter 1 (General Safety and Performance Requirements). Facilities that procure vhp-generators should request the complete risk management file from suppliers and verify that it addresses site-specific hazards (e.g., outdoor installation, non-standard environmental conditions) before equipment deployment.

4. Unique Device Identification (UDI) Compliance and Traceability Requirements Under FDA 21 CFR Part 830

FDA 21 CFR Part 830 [FDA 21 CFR Part 830] and NMPA regulations require manufacturers to assign unique device identifiers (UDI) to all medical devices and maintain complete traceability data throughout the device lifecycle, but the practical implementation challenge for vhp-generators is that field installation, configuration, and maintenance activities may alter device identifiers or create ambiguity about which UDI corresponds to which installed equipment. Regulatory audits frequently identify UDI non-compliance not because manufacturers failed to assign identifiers, but because the traceability chain between manufacturing UDI, field installation configuration, and post-market surveillance records is incomplete or inconsistent.

UDI Structure and Regulatory Requirements Under FDA 21 CFR Part 830.20

The FDA UDI regulation requires that each medical device carry a unique identifier consisting of two components: (1) Device Identifier (DI)—a code that identifies the specific version or model of the device, including manufacturer, product name, version, and packaging configuration; (2) Production Identifier (PI)—a code that identifies the specific unit produced, including serial number, lot number, expiration date, and manufacturing date. For vhp-generators, the DI might be structured as: "JIEHAO-VHP-GEN-2024-MODEL-A-SINGLE-UNIT," while the PI includes the serial number (e.g., "SN-2024-001847") and manufacturing date (e.g., "MFG-2024-05-15"). The FDA requires that the UDI be displayed on the device label in both human-readable and machine-readable (barcode/RFID) formats. The regulation specifies that for devices with a lifespan exceeding one year, the UDI must be permanently marked directly on the device (Direct Marking requirement), not merely on packaging or labels that may be removed or replaced during field service. For vhp-generators, this typically means laser-etching or mechanical engraving of the UDI on the stainless steel housing, ensuring that the identifier persists through the device's operational life.

GUDID Database Submission and Production Identifier Tracking

FDA 21 CFR Part 830.20(c) requires manufacturers to submit device identification information to the FDA's Global Unique Device Identification Database (GUDID) before commercial distribution. The GUDID submission must include: (1) Device Identifier (DI) with complete product description, intended use, and regulatory classification; (2) Production Identifier (PI) data structure (serial number format, lot number format, expiration date format); (3) UDI carrier type (barcode, RFID, or other machine-readable format); (4) Device characteristics (dimensions, weight, materials, electrical specifications). For vhp-generators, the GUDID submission must specify that the device is a Class II medical device (or Class III, depending on jurisdiction), intended for use in biosafety laboratory pass boxes, and that it incorporates hydrogen peroxide vapor generation and pressure differential monitoring. The submission must also document the UDI labeling location (e.g., "laser-etched on rear panel of stainless steel housing") and the barcode format (e.g., "GS1-128 barcode encoding DI and PI"). Regulatory audits verify that the GUDID submission matches the actual device labeling and that production records document the assignment of serial numbers and lot numbers in accordance with the submitted PI data structure.

Field Installation Configuration and UDI Traceability Challenges

A critical implementation challenge for vhp-generators is that field installation and configuration may alter the device's functional characteristics in ways that affect UDI assignment. For example, a vhp-generator may be manufactured as a standalone unit (DI: "JIEHAO-VHP-GEN-STANDALONE") but then integrated into a pass box system at the facility (creating a new configuration: "JIEHAO-VHP-GEN-PASSBOX-INTEGRATED"). The question arises: does the integrated system require a new UDI, or does the original device UDI persist? FDA guidance (FDA UDI Guidance for Industry, 2020) specifies that if the field integration creates a new "finished device" with distinct intended use or regulatory classification, a new UDI must be assigned. However, if the integration is merely a physical assembly without functional modification, the original device UDI may persist. For vhp-generators, this distinction is critical: if the device is integrated into a pass box with new pressure differential monitoring or alarm functions, the integrated system likely requires a new UDI and separate regulatory submission. Conversely, if the device is simply connected to facility power and control systems without functional modification, the original device UDI may be retained. Regulatory audits examine whether manufacturers and facilities have correctly interpreted this distinction and whether traceability records accurately reflect the UDI assignment decision.

Post-Market Surveillance and Adverse Event Traceability

FDA 21 CFR Part 803 (Medical Device Reporting) requires manufacturers to report serious adverse events to the FDA within 30 days of discovery. The adverse event report must include the device UDI (or serial number if UDI is not available), allowing the FDA to trace the reported device back to its manufacturing batch and identify whether other devices in the same batch may be affected. For vhp-generators, this traceability requirement means that manufacturers must maintain a complete mapping between device serial numbers (PI component) and manufacturing batches, and this mapping must be accessible during adverse event investigations. Facilities that deploy vhp-generators must also maintain records linking the device serial number to the specific installation location and operational history, enabling rapid identification and isolation of affected equipment if an adverse event is reported. Regulatory audits verify that both manufacturers and facilities have established these traceability systems and that they can rapidly retrieve device history records when requested by regulatory authorities.

Compliance Pathway: UDI Implementation and Traceability System Establishment

Manufacturers must execute the following steps to achieve UDI compliance: (1) Develop UDI assignment strategy that clearly defines when new UDIs are required (e.g., design changes, new configurations, regulatory reclassification) and when existing UDIs persist (e.g., minor cosmetic changes, packaging modifications); (2) Establish manufacturing process controls that ensure each device receives a unique serial number (PI component) and that the serial number is recorded in the manufacturing batch record before device release; (3) Implement permanent direct marking of the UDI on the device housing using laser etching or mechanical engraving, with durability testing to confirm that the marking persists through the device's operational life; (4) Submit complete device identification data to GUDID before first commercial distribution, with submission confirmation documented in the regulatory file; (5) Establish post-market traceability system that links device serial numbers to manufacturing batches, field installation locations, and operational history, with procedures for rapid device identification during adverse event investigations. Facilities that procure vhp-generators should verify that suppliers have completed GUDID submission and can provide documentation of UDI assignment and permanent marking before equipment deployment. Facilities should also establish their own traceability records linking device serial numbers to installation locations and maintenance history, enabling rapid response to regulatory inquiries or adverse event investigations.

5. Air Quality Classification and Pressure Differential Validation Under ISO 14644-1:2024

ISO 14644-1:2024 [ISO 14644-1:2024] establishes air cleanliness classification requirements for cleanrooms and controlled environments, and vhp-generators used in biosafety pass boxes must maintain specified pressure differentials and air change rates to prevent uncontrolled pathogen release and ensure compliance with facility classification standards. The regulatory requirement is not merely that equipment functions, but that it maintains quantified air quality parameters throughout its operational life, with field validation (IQ/OQ) protocols documenting that these parameters are achieved and sustained.

ISO 14644-1:2024 Classification and Pressure Differential Requirements for Biosafety Pass Boxes

ISO 14644-1 Clause 5 defines air cleanliness classes based on particle concentration (particles ≥0.5 µm per cubic meter): Class 1 (≤10 particles/m³), Class 2 (≤100 particles/m³), Class 3 (≤1,000 particles/m³), Class 4 (≤10,000 particles/m³), Class 5 (≤100,000 particles/m³), Class 6 (≤1,000,000 particles/m³), Class 7 (≤10,000,000 particles/m³), Class 8 (≤100,000,000 particles/m³). Biosafety pass boxes used for transfer of infectious materials typically operate at ISO Class 5 or Class 6 during the sterilization cycle, with pressure differential maintained at +10 to +50 Pa relative to the surrounding laboratory (positive pressure to prevent external contamination ingress). The vhp-generator must maintain sufficient air flow and pressure differential to achieve and sustain the target air cleanliness class throughout the sterilization cycle. ISO 14644-1 Clause 8 specifies that pressure differential must be measured and documented at least monthly, with acceptance criteria typically ±5 Pa from the target setpoint. Facilities that fail to maintain documented pressure differential records during regulatory audit face findings that the equipment does not meet ISO 14644-1 requirements and that the facility's contamination control strategy is unvalidated.

Pressure Decay Testing and ASTM E779 Compliance

The primary field validation method for pressure differential maintenance is pressure decay testing per ASTM E779 (Standard Test Method for Determining Air Leakage Rate of Enclosures by Pressure Decay). The test procedure involves: (1) sealing the pass box chamber, (2) pressurizing to a target differential (typically +50 Pa), (3) holding pressure for a specified duration (typically 10 minutes), (4) measuring the rate of pressure loss (Pa/minute). The acceptance criterion is typically ≤5 Pa loss over 10 minutes, corresponding to an air leakage rate of ≤0.5 Pa/minute. ASTM E779 specifies that the test must be conducted at controlled temperature (20–25°C) and humidity (40–60% RH), with documentation of ambient conditions. For vhp-generators integrated into pass boxes, pressure decay testing must be performed on the complete integrated system (generator + pass box + interconnecting ducting), not merely on the generator in isolation. Regulatory audits verify that pressure decay test data are documented, that test conditions meet ASTM E779 requirements, and that acceptance criteria are met. A common non-compliance finding is that pressure decay testing was performed but test conditions (temperature, humidity) were not documented, making it impossible to verify that the test was conducted per standard.

Installation Qualification (IQ) and Operational Qualification (OQ) Protocols

Regulatory requirements under GMP Annex 1 (EU) and FDA 21 CFR Part 211 mandate that facilities conduct Installation Qualification (IQ) and Operational Qualification (OQ) before placing vhp-generators into routine use. IQ verifies that the equipment has been installed according to manufacturer specifications and that all components are present and functional. IQ activities include: (1) verification that equipment model and serial number match purchase order and regulatory documentation; (2) visual inspection of equipment for damage or defects; (3) verification that all required accessories (pressure transducers, humidity sensors, alarm systems) are installed; (4) verification that electrical connections meet facility standards and that grounding is adequate; (5) documentation of as-installed equipment configuration (photographs, dimensional verification, connection diagrams). OQ verifies that the equipment performs according to design specifications under normal operating conditions. OQ activities include: (1) pressure decay testing per ASTM E779 to verify that the pass box maintains specified pressure differential; (2) hydrogen peroxide vapor concentration measurement during a representative sterilization cycle, with documentation that target concentration (600–800 mg/m³) is achieved and maintained; (3) cycle timing verification to confirm that each cycle phase (conditioning, sterilization, drying, catalytic degradation) completes within specified timeframes; (4) alarm function testing to verify that alarms activate upon pressure loss, temperature deviation, or sensor failure; (5) documentation of all test results with acceptance criteria and pass/fail determination. Regulatory audits verify that IQ/OQ protocols have been completed before equipment deployment and that all test data are retained in the facility's validation file.

Performance Qualification (PQ) and Biological Indicator Testing

Performance Qualification (PQ) verifies that the vhp-generator achieves the intended sterilization outcome (inactivation of target microorganisms) under actual facility conditions. PQ activities include: (1) biological indicator (BI) testing using standardized test organisms (typically Geobacillus stearothermophilus spores for hydrogen peroxide sterilization) to verify that the sterilization cycle achieves the required log reduction (typically ≥6 log reduction, corresponding to a sterility assurance level of 10⁻⁶); (2) chemical indicator testing to verify that the sterilization conditions (vapor concentration, temperature, humidity, time) are achieved throughout the chamber; (3) material compatibility testing to verify that the sterilization process does not damage or degrade materials in contact with hydrogen peroxide vapor (stainless steel, elastomers, plastics); (4) documentation of PQ results with acceptance criteria and pass/fail determination. Regulatory audits verify that PQ has been completed and that biological indicator data demonstrate adequate sterilization efficacy. A common audit deficiency is that PQ was performed but biological indicator data are not retained or are not linked to specific equipment serial numbers, making it impossible to verify that the tested equipment matches the installed equipment.

Compliance Pathway: Comprehensive IQ/OQ/PQ Validation and Documentation

Facilities must execute the following validation sequence before deploying vhp-generators: (1) Develop comprehensive IQ/OQ/PQ protocol that specifies all required tests, acceptance criteria, and documentation requirements, with protocol reviewed and approved by quality assurance before execution; (2) Conduct IQ activities to verify equipment installation, with all findings documented and any deficiencies corrected before proceeding to OQ; (3) Conduct OQ activities to verify equipment performance under normal operating conditions, with all test data retained and acceptance criteria verified; (4) Conduct PQ activities to verify sterilization efficacy using biological indicators, with results documented and linked to equipment serial numbers; (5) Establish ongoing monitoring program to verify that equipment continues to meet performance specifications throughout its operational life, with pressure differential and particle concentration measured at least monthly and documented in the facility's validation file. Facilities that implement this comprehensive validation approach and maintain complete documentation packages achieve regulatory compliance and can demonstrate to auditors that equipment performance is validated and sustained.

6. Regulatory Pathway Harmonization and Jurisdiction-Specific Registration Requirements

Vhp-generators intended for distribution across multiple regulatory jurisdictions (NMPA, FDA, EU MDR) must satisfy overlapping but distinct registration requirements, and manufacturers that develop a single technical file without jurisdiction-specific adaptation face rejection during regulatory review due to missing or non-compliant documentation. The regulatory pathway is not a single linear process but a convergent system of parallel requirements that must be satisfied simultaneously, with each jurisdiction imposing specific documentation, testing, and approval timelines.

NMPA Registration Pathway and Technical File Requirements

The China National Medical Products Administration (NMPA) classifies vhp-generators as Class II medical devices (or Class III, depending on intended use and risk profile), requiring manufacturers to submit a comprehensive technical file for regulatory review before market approval. The NMPA technical file must include: (1) Device Description and Intended Use—detailed description of device structure, materials, electrical specifications, and intended use in biosafety laboratory pass boxes; (2) Regulatory History—documentation of regulatory approvals in other jurisdictions (FDA, CE MDR) if applicable; (3) Standards and Guidance Documents—list of applicable international standards (ISO 14644-1, ISO 14971, IEC 60601-1) and NMPA guidance documents; (4) Design and Development—design specifications, design history file (DHF), design verification and validation (DVV) reports; (5) Risk Management Report—comprehensive risk analysis per ISO 14971, including hazard identification, risk evaluation, risk control measures, and residual risk assessment; (6) Electrical Safety and EMC—test reports from NCSA or equivalent notified body demonstrating compliance with IEC 60601-1 and IEC 60601-1-2 (electromagnetic compatibility); (7) Performance Testing—test reports demonstrating that the device achieves specified performance (pressure differential maintenance, hydrogen peroxide vapor concentration control, cycle timing accuracy); (8) Biocompatibility Assessment—assessment of materials in contact with hydrogen peroxide vapor, with testing per ISO 10993 series if applicable; (9) Labeling and Instructions—device label, instructions for use (IFU), and training materials; (10) Manufacturing Information—manufacturing process description, quality control procedures, and batch record examples. The NMPA review timeline is typically 60–90 days for Class II devices, with potential for additional review cycles if deficiencies are identified. Common deficiencies include: incomplete risk management documentation, missing electrical safety test reports, inadequate performance testing data, and IFU that does not address identified hazards or foreseeable misuse scenarios.
